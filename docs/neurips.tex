\begin{abstract}

Synapse-level connectomes describe the structure of circuits, but not the electrical and chemical dynamics.  Conversely, large-scale recordings of neural activity capture these dynamics, but not the circuit structure.  We asked whether combining binary connectivity and recorded neural activity can be used to infer mechanistic models of neural circuits.  We trained graph neural network models (GNN) to forecast the activity of \emph{Drosophila} visual system simulations.  Trained on activity trajectories in response to visual inputs, the models recover effective connectivity weights, neuron types, and nonlinear activation functions, even when 400\% random connections are added to the given connectivity.  Moreover, they correctly predict causal effects of connection removal, demonstrating the ability to infer mechanistic dependencies directly from activity data.

\end{abstract}

\section{Related work}


Prior studies were successful in retrieving functional connectivity, functional properties of neurons, or predictive models of neural activity at the population level, but a method to infer an interpretable mechanistic model of complex neural assemblies is yet missing. \citet{mi_connectome-constrained_2021} infer the hidden voltage dynamics and functional connectivity from calcium recordings of parts of the \emph{C. elegans} nervous system using a simple biophysical neuron model.  While its predictive performance is modest, it demonstrates that integrating real activity recordings with connectivity can yield experimentally testable predictions beyond simulation.  \citet{pospisil_fly_2024} used the connectome of \emph{Drosophila melanogaster} \citep{dorkenwald_neuronal_2024} to create simulations of full brain activity and inferred effective connectivity from known perturbations of individual neurons with a simple linear model.  Lacking information about connectivity, \citet{wang_foundation_2025} trained a foundation model of mouse visual cortex activity on real two-photon recordings from 135,000 neurons, which predicts responses to both natural videos and out-of-distribution stimuli across animals.
\citet{prasse_predicting_2022} focus on accurate prediction without recovering connectivity while \citet{yu_discovering_2025} infer symbolic dynamical equations without validating connectivity weights. Both work with simulated data and do not learn neuronal heterogeneity.

Our method learns the functional properties of individual neurons and their relative connectivity weights, enabling transfer of the learned dynamics to different network configurations with modified connectivity (or different neuron type compositions).

\section{Methods}

\subsection*{Simulations} \label{method:simulation}


We simulated neural activity in the \emph{Drosophila} visual system using \textit{flyvis} pretrained models \citep{lappalainen_connectome-constrained_2024}. We restricted the original $721$
retinotopic columns to the central subset of $217$
columns, covering the functionally relevant central visual field while substantially reducing memory and runtime requirements. This way, the recurrent neural network contains $13{,}741$ neurons from $65$ cell types and $434{,}122$ synaptic connections. To address different modeling assumptions and scalability, we simulate model variants as specified in Table~\ref{tab:network_variants} -- for instance the full network with  $45{,}669$ neurons  and $1{,}513231$ synaptic connections. 
Each neuron is modeled as a non-spiking voltage compartment governed by
\begin{equation}
\tau_i\frac{dv_i(t)}{dt} = -v_i(t) + V_i^{\text{rest}}
+ \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i}\mathbf{W}_{ij}\,\text{ReLU}\!\big(v_j(t)\big)
+ I_i(t) + \sigma\,\xi_i(t),
\label{eq:simulated_update}
\end{equation}
where $v_i$ is the neuron membrane voltage, $\tau_i$ and $V_i^{\mathrm{rest}}$ are cell-type parameters, $\mathbf{W}_{ij}$ is the connectome synaptic weight, $I_i(t)$ is the visual input, and $\xi_i(t)\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ is an independent Gaussian noise scaled by $\sigma$. The later defines an model noise perturbing the dynamical trajectory of $v_i(t)$. We also added measurement noise to $v_i$(t), Gaussian noise scaled by $\gamma$. 

Visual inputs are derived from the DAVIS dataset~\cite{perazzi_benchmark_2016} and YouTube-VOS~\cite{perazzi_benchmark_2016}, both natural video benchmarks. Frames are  converted to grayscale, and resampled
onto the hexagonal photoreceptor lattice of $217$ columns, giving $1{,}736$ input neurons ($8$ photoreceptor types R1--R8 per column). DAVIS sequences are augmented with horizontal/vertical
flips and four rotations and split $80/20$ into train/validation. YouTube-VOS sequences serve as a held-out test set that was not used for training or hyperparameter tuning. All reported metrics and tables are based on the held out test data unless specified otherwise.

\subsubsection*{Graph neural network model} \label{method:GNN}
We approximate the simulated voltage dynamics by a message-passing GNN \cite{gilmer_neural_2017}
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\hat{v}_i(t)}{dt}
= f_\theta\!\Big(v_i(t),\,\mathbf{a}_i,\,
\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i} \widehat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}\,
g_\phi\!\big(v_j(t),\mathbf{a}_j\big)^2,\,
I_i(t)\Big).
\label{eq:gnn_ct}
\end{equation}
Nodes of the GNN correspond to neurons and edges correspond to functional synaptic connections. The GNN learns a latent embedding $\mathbf{a}_i\in\mathbb{R}^2$ for each neuron. The learnable vectors $\mathbf{a}_i\in\mathbb{R}^2$ represent compact latent identity, they can map to cell-type-specific properties (like time constants and nonlinearities). Neuron update $f_\theta=\text{MLP}_0$ and edge message $g_\phi=\text{MLP}_1$ are three-layer perceptrons (width $64$, ReLU, linear output). $g_\phi$ maps presynaptic inputs $v_j$ to nonnegative messages (via squaring) depending on latent embedding $\mathbf{a}_j$, weighted by $\widehat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}$. $f_\theta$ processes the postsynaptic voltage $v_i$, aggregated input, and external input $I_i(t)$ to predict $d\hat{v}_i(t)/dt$, depending also on latent embedding $\mathbf{a}_i$. During training, inputs $I_i(t)$, adjacency $\mathcal{N}_i$, and activity $v_i(t)$ are given. The MLPs, $\widehat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}$, and $\mathbf{a}_i$ are optimized by minimizing difference between simulator $y_i(t)=dv_i(t)/dt$ and GNN predictions $\hat{y}_i(t)=d\hat{v}_i(t)/dt$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}} = \sum_{i,t}\|\hat{y}_i(t) - y_i(t)\|_2
\label{eq:loss_pred}
\end{equation}
Many synaptic weight configurations give rise to identical neural activity, making the recovery of $\widehat{\mathbf{W}}$ from voltage traces fundamentally non-unique (see Appendix \ref{app:degeneracy}). To constrain the solution space, we added regularization terms to $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}$
\begin{eqnarray}
\mathcal{L} &=& \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}
+ \lambda_0\|\theta\|_1 + \lambda_1\|\phi\|_1 + \lambda_2\|\theta\|_2 + \lambda_3\|\phi\|_2
+ \gamma_0\|\widehat{\mathbf{W}}\|_1 + \gamma_1\|\widehat{\mathbf{W}}\|_2 \nonumber\\
&& +\; \mu_0\!\left\|\mathrm{ReLU}\!\left(-\tfrac{\partial\,f_\theta(v,m,\mathbf{a},I)}{\partial m}\right)\right\|_2
+ \mu_1\!\left\|\mathrm{ReLU}\!\left(-\tfrac{\partial\,g_\phi(v,\mathbf{a})}{\partial v}\right)\right\|_2 \nonumber +\; \mu_2\|g_\phi(v_\star,\mathbf{a})-v_\star\|_2.
\label{eq:loss_full}
\end{eqnarray}
The $\ell_1$ penalties $\lambda_0,\lambda_1$ on the MLP weights $\theta$ and $\phi$ promote sparsity, biasing $f_\theta$ and $g_\phi$ toward simpler functions; the $\ell_2$ penalties $\lambda_2,\lambda_3$ prevent weight explosion and stabilize training. The $\ell_1$ term $\gamma_0$ on $\widehat{\mathbf{W}}$ promotes sparse effective connectivity, while the $\ell_2$ term $\gamma_1$ penalizes large synaptic weights. The monotonicity term $\mu_0$ penalizes negative slopes of $f_\theta$ with respect to its aggregated synaptic input $m=\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i}\widehat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}\,g_\phi(v_j,\mathbf{a}_j)$, encoding the prior that the postsynaptic derivative increases with net excitatory drive. The monotonicity term $\mu_1$ penalizes negative slopes of $g_\phi$ with respect to $v$, encoding the prior that synaptic drive increases with presynaptic voltage. The normalization term $\mu_2$ anchors $g_\phi$ at a reference voltage $v_\star$, removing the scale degeneracy between $g_\phi$ and $\widehat{\mathbf{W}}$ in Eq.~\ref{eq:gnn_ct}. We tuned all the hyperparameters $\lambda_0,\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\lambda_3,\gamma_0,\gamma_1,\mu_0,\mu_1,\mu_2$ and the MLPs structure with an agentic optimization procedure described below.


\subsubsection*{Agent-in-the-loop parameter optimisation}
\label{method:agent}
The combined space of simulation and machine learning parameters was too large for grid search. We instead deploy an agent-in-the-loop system in which a large language model agent (Claude Code, Anthropic) interprets experiment results,
maintains a structured summary, and proposes the next experiment by changing
the simulation regime and/or machine learning parameters
(Appendix~\ref{app:agent}). The primary optimisation target was either connectivity identifiability
($R^2_{\mathbf{W}}$) or rollout prediction accuracy. To explore the landscape of experiments, the agent followed a hypothesis-driven protocol. A testable hypothesis must be stated before each experiment, and only experimental outcomes can validate or falsify it. Experiments are organised into blocks of $8$--$16$ iterations, with $4$ runs executed in parallel so that the agent reasons over a batch of
outcomes, sharpening both validation and falsification. Using hypothesis-driven exploration allowed us to optimize machine learning model parameters in effiecient and interpretable manner.

\section{Results}

We evaluate the GNN and the different other machine learning models across a grid of simulations spanning the main sources of variability and degradation expected in realistic experimental settings. In particular, we tested three levels of
model noise ($\sigma \in \{0, 0.05, 0.5\}$), measurement noise
($\gamma \in \{0.1, 0.2\}$), false-positive edges ($+400\%$ added null edges),
false-negative edges ($-20\%$ removed edges), and partial data in both time
($1/5$ frames kept) and neurons ($10\%$ and $20\%$ hidden). 
Across all simulations we fixed the training data size to $64{,}000$ frames. The number of model training iterations varied between $5{\times}10^5$ and $2{\times}10^6$ depending on noise level and model. For most of simulation condition, an independent agentic loop was run to identify the optimal model architecture and training parameters (see \ref{app:agent}, Table \ref{tab:unified_hp}, ?? \paragraph{Degeneracy.} Before testing the ability to predict activty and recover circuit parameters from data alone, we first established how much the problem was ill-posed. In the particular case of the FLyvis model were a columnar structure containing 65 different cell types is repeated hundreds of times over an hexagonal lattices, it appears that the problem is very ill-posed. We show in the appendix \ref{app:degeneracy} that the simulation model can be fully linearized so that the connectivity unknows (>400K weights) are the solution of a  matrix equations. Because many neurons within a given cell type have similar role in the computation, their corresponding matrix coefficients can be permutated and/or largely modified. So that many solutions exists and they can be very different one another. Emprically we found a model with only 40K edges (vs 400K) that is still able to rollout activity (r~0.99 over 800K frames). This implies that having a model able to predict activity is one solution amongst many other and drawing conclusions from this learned model alone would be misleading.

\paragraph{GNN recovers flyvis with realistic FlyWire edges}
% This fits into results section
\cite{lappalainen_connectome-constrained_2024} compiles whole-eye connectivity from local reconstructions. The resulting flyvis model connectome is a convolutional (translation-invariant) graph: Every visual column shares the same set of 2,140 edge templates, each parametrized by source type, target type, and column offset. While this assumption is a good approximation for the underlying biology, it discards per-column variation of connectivity that is present in the fly and available in more recent whole-brain reconstructions (FlyWire) \cite{dorkenwald_neuronal_2024, }. Moreover, the real  connectivity from FlyWire is sparser than the flyvis convolutional filters because flyvis constructed convolutional templates merging all existing per-type-pair connectivity [refine]. To test the counterfactual whether the GNN would recover a dynamical visual system with real biological synaptic wiring variability -- or in other words, whether the GNN inference capability was limited by the convolutional assumption --  we constructed a hybrid connectome variant that augments the flyvis graph with column-resolved FlyWire connectivity data, while remaining compatible with the trained flyvis model parameters (Methods). 

We generated simulated data from the flyvis ODE on the hybrid FlyWire-RF connectome (328,092 edges; Table~\ref{tab:hybrid_recovery}) and trained the GNN to recover the dynamical system from noisy observations ($\sigma=0.05$). The GNN achieved a one-step Pearson $r=0.973$ and rollout $r=0.970$, closly matching the convolutional flyvis baseline (rollout $r=0.984$ on $451,451$ edges). Per edge synaptic weight recovery remained high ($R^2=0.924$), with time-constant recovery $R^2=0.974$, confirming that the GNN can infer heterogenous, column-specific connectivity without loss of accuracy.

\paragraph{GNN dynamical system inference with realistic connectivity uncertainty}


In practice, datasets that combine anatomical and functional measurements are rare but becoming more readily available in different recording modalities, such as X [CITE], Y [CITE], Z [CITE]. Different measurement techniques pose different trade-offs and typically combining neural activity recordings with mesoscale anatomical reconstructions [CITE] is cheaper than combining it with synapse-level EM microscopy reconstruction. We asked, what if we pretended we had coarse mesoscale connectivity and single-neuron neural activity measurements but did not know synapse-level connectivity? [TODO: refine]

To model this scenario, we constructed a connectivity graph that augments the FlyWire-RF connectome with realistic zero-weight edges representing uncertainty: Neuron pairs lying within a radius of $~6\,\mu\text{m}$ of each other (approximately one-column in the fly visual system) inhert all their neighbors edges as zero edges, across all types. This increases the edge-count from $328,092$ to $1,959,994$. The zero edges represent spatially plausible but unmeasured edges and the GNN must learn which carry nonzero weight.

\paragraph{GNN dynamical system inference under coregistration uncertainty}

Training connectome-constrained models with neural activity data at single-neuron resolution poses the challenge of coregistration uncertainty: Neural recordings and connectomic reconstructions can be coregistered to 5-10 microns precision \cite{Brezovec2024-zc}, on the order of a small neuron or ROI, such that measured activity of a specific neuron cannot always be assigned to a single reconstructed neuron from a connectome. \section{Degeneracy of the inverse problem}
\label{app:degeneracy}


FROM RESULTS:
In each case, the model almost perfectly predicts the one-step update and is able to perform an auto-regressive rollout on held out stimuli for 8000 steps. However, despite perfect information about the dynamical equation we find that certain parameters cannot be accurately inferred. Parameter recovery improves upon the addition of a small amount of noise $\sigma=0.05$ and the recovery is perfect at the highest level of $\sigma=0.5$. This is due to a continuous degeneracy in the parameter space of the FlyVis ODE model simulated on naturalistic videos, that is, there is a \emph{degenerate subspace} in parameter space along which different ODEs with the same initial conditions produce the exact same solution. The chosen simulation parameters $(\tau_i, W_{ij}, \vrest_i)$ comprise one point in the degenerate subspace from which we generate a training dataset $\{v_i(t) | \ t \in [0, T]\}$ but gradient descent with random initialization of parameters can converge to any point in this space. Concretely, as a toy example, the parameters $\theta_1, \theta_2$ of the ODE $\dot{x} = -(\theta_1 + \theta_2)x, \ x(0) = 0$, cannot be uniquely determined from the solution $x(t)$ alone, but the combination $\theta_1 + \theta_2$ can be. To understand the degenerate space in equation \ref{eq:simulated_update}, first we observe that while the equation is nonlinear in $v(t)$, it is linear in the parameters $\theta_\alpha := (\tau_i, W_{ij}, \vrest_i)$, and therefore the problem of determining the parameters from given training data $v(t)$ can be written as $A\theta = b$, where $A$ is $(NT - 1) \times (2N + N_E)$ design matrix and $b$ is a vector of length $NT-1$. With this formulation, it is easy to see that if the training data contained a neuron $i$ that was always at its resting potential, \ie, $v_i(t) = \vrest_i \ \forall \ t$, then it is impossible to identify $\tau_i$ because the column multiplying $\tau_i$ in $A$ is identically 0. However, upon the addition of model noise, the stochastic dynamics decreases the likelihood of finding such a neuron. Similarly, if a neuron is in a constantly excited state the training data for that neuron would allow us to probe its presynaptic weights accurately but we would be underpowered to determine $\tau$ and $\vrest$. We do not rule out the possibility that there exists a family of stimuli, perhaps uncorrelated white noise, that would allow for perfect recovery of parameters in the absence of noise. This baseline helps demonstrate that the flyvis in the degenerate subspace can produce perfect one-step predictions and perfect rollouts but fail to recover the true parameters of the system. For further details on the degeneracy and parameter recovery, we refer the interested reader to Appendix \ref{app:degeneracy}.

Recall that the FlyVis stochastic dynamical system in \ref{eq:simulated_update}, in the absence of noise ($\sigma=0$), takes the form:
\begin{equation}
    \tau_i\frac{dv_i(t)}{dt} = -v_i(t) + V_i^{\text{rest}}
+ \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i}\mathbf{W}_{ij}\,\text{ReLU}\!\big(v_j(t)\big)
+ I_i(t) \label{eq:flyvis-app-noise-free-ode}
\end{equation}
Our goal is to infer the circuit parameters $(\tau_i, \vrest_i, W_{ij})$ from one solution of this ODE $\tilde{v}_i(t)$ and the stimulus $I_i(t)$. The ODE parameters can be decomposed into a separate set for each neuron $i$, namely, $\theta_{i\alpha} := (\tau_i, \vrest_i, W_{ij})$ is a vector of length $2 + |\mathcal{N}_i|$ indexed by $\alpha$, where $\mathcal{N}_i$ is the set of presynaptic partners of neuron $i$. We assume the solution $\tilde{v}_i(t)$ was generated by a parameter set $\theta$ and solving the inverse problem gives us an inferred set $\hat{theta}$. We show here that there are \emph{continuous} deformations in parameter space that lead to equivalent solutions, therefore, the system does not admit a unique solution making the inverse problem ill-posed.

Equation \ref{eq:flyvis-app-noise-free-ode} can be rewritten, for the purposes of parameter inference
\begin{equation}
    -\tau_i \dot{\tilde{v}}_i + \vrest_i + \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}_i} W_{ij}h_j(t) = \tilde{v}_i(t) - I_i(t)
    \label{eq:flyvis-linear-rewrite}
\end{equation}
where $h_j(t) := \relu(\tilde{v}_j(t))$ are the pre-synaptic activities. Observe that while the ODE is non-linear in the state variable $v$ it is \emph{linear} in the circuit parameters $\theta_{i\alpha}$ defined above. So, given a solution $\tilde{v}_i(t)$ sampled at discrete time points $t=0, ..., (T-1)$, we can rewrite \ref{eq:flyvis-linear-rewrite} as a separate linear system to solve for each neuron $i$, 
\begin{equation}
    A(i)\cdot \theta_i = b_i
    \label{eq:parameter-linear-system}
\end{equation}
in terms of the design matrix $A(i)$ of dimension $T \times (2 + \mathcal{N}_i)$ and vectors $\theta_i$ of dimension $2 + \vert \mathcal{N}_i \vert$, and $b_i$ of dimension $T$:
\begin{equation}
    A(i) := \begin{pmatrix}
        -\dot{\tilde{v}}_i(0) & 1 & h_{j_1}(0) & h_{j_2}(0) & \cdots  \\
        -\dot{\tilde{v}}_i(1) & 1 & h_{j_1}(1) & h_{j_2}(1) & \cdots  \\
        \vdots & \vdots & \vdots &\vdots & 
    \end{pmatrix}, \ \theta_i := \begin{pmatrix}
        \tau_i \\
        \vrest_i \\
        W_{ij_1} \\
        W_{ij_2} \\
        \vdots
    \end{pmatrix}, \ b_i := \begin{pmatrix}
        \tilde{v}_i(0) - I_i(0) \\
        \tilde{v}_i(1) - I_i(1) \\
        \vdots 
    \end{pmatrix}
    \label{eq:explicit-linear-system}
\end{equation}
where $j_1, j_2, \cdots $ are the pre-synaptic neurons in $\mathcal{N}_i$. We emphasize that this linear system is not a linearization or approximation but a property of the voltage-based firing rate models used in FlyVis \cite{Lappalainen2024-tq}. 


The linear system \ref{eq:parameter-linear-system} can be solved to infer the simulation parameters $\theta_{i\alpha}$. If the column space is not full rank the system admits a continuous degeneracy defined by the kernel of $A(i)$. In particular, if there exists a vector $\eta_{i\alpha}$ satisfying $A(i)\cdot \eta_i = 0$ for all $i$, the family of parameters $\theta'_{i\alpha} = \theta_{i\alpha} + \lambda \eta_{i\alpha}$ for all $\lambda$ are solutions to the inverse problem. Furthermore, if we were to perform a forward simulation with parameters $\theta'$ with the same stimulus $I_i$ and initial condition $\tilde{v}_(t=0)$ we would generate the same solution $\tilde{v}_i(t)$.

In the following, we present explicit examples of the degenerate space and this serves to explain why the parameter recovery from neural activity in the FlyVis system \emph{without} intrinsic noise doesn't work perfectly even when the activation functions (Known ODE) are known. This issue is exacerbated when fitting GNNs without noise where the activation functions are estimated simultaneously along with the parameters.

\subsection{Parameter recovery}

\subsubsection*{$\tau$ recovery}

If the network structure and stimulus are such that a neuron $i$ is in a resting state during the entire experiment, then we have $\tilde{v}_i(t) = \vrest_i$ and $\dot{\tilde{v}}_i(t) = 0$ for all observed $t$. In this case, the first column of $A(i)$ is identically zero and hence it is impossible to recover $\tau_i$. A neuron can be in constant rest, \eg, if it does not receive an external stimulus and all its pre-synaptic neurons contribute sub-threshold/negative activities, resulting in $h_{j_1}(t), h_{j_2}(t), \cdots = 0$. In this case, the corresponding weights to $W_i$ are also impossible to determine. \subsubsection*{$\vrest$ recovery}

Other scenarios make it impossible to unambiguously recover $\vrest$. 
Let's suppose two neurons $i$ and $j$ are both at rest, and that $j$ is a pre-synaptic partner of $i$. If all incoming connections to $i$ and $j$ are sub-threshold, except for $j\rightarrow i$ and if $\vrest_j > 0$, then
\begin{equation}
    A(i) := \begin{pmatrix}
        0 & 1 & \vrest_j & 0 & \cdots  \\
        0 & 1 & \vrest_j & 0 & \cdots  \\
        \vdots & \vdots & \vdots &\vdots & 
    \end{pmatrix} \text{and} \ A(j) := \begin{pmatrix}
        0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots  \\
        0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots  \\
        \vdots & \vdots & \vdots &\vdots & 
    \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
We now have a family of solutions where $\hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_j = \vrest_j $ and $\hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_i + \vrest_j\hat{W}_{ij} = \vrest_j$. Like in the previous example, $\tau_i$ and $\tau_j$ are undetermined because the neurons are at rest, but we have a new degeneracy with a solution $\hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_j = \vrest_j, \hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_i = \vrest_j ( 1 - \hat{W}_{ij} )$ for \emph{any} value of $\hat{W}_{ij}$.% generated by the optimization.

To study degeneracies within the space of weight matrices, we fix the values of $\vrest$ and $\tau$ and rearrange \cref{eq:flyvis-linear-rewrite} yielding

\begin{equation}

\end{equation}
Redefining the residual term $b_i(t) = \tau_i \frac{dv_i(t)}{dt} + v_i(t) - V_i^{\text{rest}} - I_i(t)$, we have 
% Defining the presynaptic activity $h_j(t) = \text{ReLU}(v_j(t))$ and the
% residual $b_i(t) = \tau_i \frac{dv_i(t)}{dt} + v_i(t) - V_i^{\text{rest}} -

  \label{eq:linear_system}
\end{equation}

activity matrix, $d_i$ is the in-degree of neuron $i$, $\mathbf{w}_i \in
\mathbb{R}^{d_i}$ the incoming weight vector, and $\mathbf{b}_i \in
\mathbb{R}^T$ the stacked residuals. This holds for each postsynaptic neuron

\in \ker(\mathbf{H}_i)$ satisfies Eq.~\eqref{eq:linear_system} equally and
produces identical dynamics. In the following, we characterise the
dynamical equivalence class of $\mathbf{W}$, the set of all weight
matrices that reproduce the observed trajectories.

spans 217 retinotopic columns, and each postsynaptic neuron $i$ typically
receives input from $k_{i\alpha} > 1$ neurons of the same cell type $\alpha$

\section{Sensitivity analysis of the FlyVis ODE}

%%% Goal of this section: explain why the known ODE method does not recover tau %%%

Given an ODE, sensitivity analysis [TODO: right term?][TODO: ref] helps identify directions in parameter space that minimally impact the solution. The solution $x_i(t)$ to a general ODE
\begin{equation}
\dot{x_i} = f_i(x, \theta)    
\end{equation}
with the state $x \in \R^n$ and parameters $\theta \in \R^p$, implicitly depends on $\theta$. The \emph{sensitivity} $S_{i\alpha} := \partial x_i / \partial \theta_\alpha$ of the $i$-th component to the specific parameter direction $\theta_\alpha$ is the solution to another ODE:
\begin{equation}
\dot{S_{i; \alpha}} = \sum_j \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j} S_{j; \alpha} + \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial \theta_\alpha} \label{eq:sens-ode}
\end{equation}
The ODE is initialized $x(t=0) = x_0$ to a value independent of the parameters $\theta$ and therefore we have $S_{i; \alpha}(t=0)= 0$. The second term on the right hand side describes the source term that causes $S_{i; \alpha}$ to gradually take on non-zero values. In the absence of the source term $S_{i; \alpha} = 0$ for all time. For a particular direction in parameter space defined by $\eta_\alpha$ we can define $s_i := \sum_\alpha S_{i\alpha} \eta_\alpha$ by projection. Then, 
\begin{equation}
    \dot{s}_i(t) = \sum_j J_{ij}(x) s_j + \sum_\alpha \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial \theta_\alpha} \eta_\alpha\ .
\end{equation}

The noise-free FlyVis ODE \ref{eq:simulated_update} can be rewritten
\begin{equation}
    \dot{x_i} = -\omega_i x_i + U_i + \sum_j G_{ij} \relu(x_j) + \omega_i I_i
\end{equation}
with a suitable redefinition of the parameters (TODO: fix)
\begin{equation}
    \omega_i = 1/\tau_i, \ U_i = \vrest_i \tau_i, \ W_{ij} = G_{ij} \tau_i
\end{equation}
The Jacobian $J(x)$, which appears in \ref{eq:sens-ode}, has the special form $J(x) = \Omega + G\odot H(x)$ in matrix notation where $H(x) = d \relu (x) / dx$ and $\odot$ denotes elementwise multiplication. The Jacobian is a constant matrix in any region of state space where the signs of $x_i$ are unchanged for all $i$, \eg, $J(x) = \Omega$, for $x \in \R_-^n$. We compute the second term in \ref{eq:sens-ode} for each parameter below:
\begin{eqnarray}
    \omega_j & : & \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial \omega_j} = (-x_i + I_i) \delta_{ij} \\
    U_j & : & \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial U_j} = \delta_{ij} \\
    G_{kl} & : & \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial G_{kl}} = \relu(x_l) \delta_{ik}
\end{eqnarray}
Note that $I_i$ is non-zero only for the input neurons. Let $M$ denote the set of neurons such that $x_i(t) >= 0$ and $I_i(t) = 0$ for all $i \in M$. If there exists such a non-empty set, then
\begin{equation}
    \sum_j \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial \omega_j} + \sum_{j, k} \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial G_{kj}} = -x_j \delta_{ij} + x_j \delta_{ik} 
\end{equation}

\section{Agent-in-the-loop parameter optimisation}
\label{app:agent}
The optimisation space combining simulation parameters (noise $\sigma$,
connectivity constraints, $n_{\mathrm{frames}}$) and machine learning parameters
(e.g.\ for GNN, $\lambda_0,\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\lambda_3,\gamma_0,\gamma_1,\mu_0,\mu_1,\mu_2$, learning rates, etc.) is too large for grid search. We instead deploy a closed loop
coupling three components: a GNN training engine, a large language model
agent (Claude Code, Anthropic), and an explicit structured summary. The system
operates through text file exchange. The instruction file defines the inverse
problem, the quantitative score objective, the metrics to track, the parameter
ranges, and the logging format. The structured summary maintains accumulated
understanding in two tiers: established principles and open questions that
persist across blocks, and a working record of current hypotheses and recent
iterations. A configuration file is edited by the agent to set the next
experiment; a results file is written by the experiment engine and read by
the agent.

The primary objective of the agentic loop is to optimise hyperparameters
against a quantitative score ($R^2_{\mathbf{W}}$ or rollout accuracy). But we
also constrain the agent to hypothesis-driven protocol: a testable
hypothesis must be stated before each experiment, and only experimental
outcomes can validate or falsify it. The next experiment is therefore
selected by the hypothesis the agent currently seeks to test rather than by a
numerical exploration/exploitation trade-off.

Experiments are organised into blocks of 8-16 iterations (multiples of 4 to
match the parallel batch size). The agent does not start from scratch, it is initialised with a configuration established from prior works. The simulation
regime of each block can be specified by the user in the instruction file or
chosen by the agent. Within a block, the agent explores GNN training
parameters guided by its current hypothesis. To make the protocol robust, we
run $4$ experiments in parallel and submit the resulting batch of training
outcomes to the agent. Reasoning over a batch rather than a single trial
sharpens both confirmation and falsification. The structured summary also
serves as an explicit long-term memory, overcoming the finite context window
of the agent and enabling accumulation across hundreds of iterations. With
this protocol, convergence to the optimal configuration is reached within
$\sim 100$ GNN training experiments sweeping sometimes more than 10 parameters.

We initially adopted UCB tree search as in AlphaEvolve and FunSearch
\cite{romera-paredes_mathematical_2024,novikov_alphaevolve_2025}, where
parameter mutations are sampled from a tree balancing exploitation and
exploration. Once the hypothesis-driven constraint was introduced, we
found that the agent stopped consulting the UCB scores: parent selection and
parameter mutations were driven entirely by hypotheses formulated from the
structured summary and recent experimental results. We accordingly removed
the UCB component, which had become redundant.

\subsection{Known-ODE baseline} \label{method:known_ode}
The known-ODE baseline fixes the functional form of the dynamics to the ground-truth flyvis equation and learns only the biophysical parameters and the connectivity:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\tau}_i\,\frac{d\hat{v}_i(t)}{dt}
= -\hat{v}_i(t) + \hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_i
+ \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i} \widehat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}\,\mathrm{ReLU}\!\big(v_j(t)\big)
+ I_i(t).
\label{eq:known_ode}
\end{equation}
The learnable parameters are the per-edge weights $\widehat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}$, the per-neuron time constants $\hat{\tau}_i = \mathrm{softplus}(\tilde{\tau}_i)$ (with $\tilde{\tau}_i\in\mathbb{R}$ the underlying optimized parameter), and the per-neuron resting potentials $\hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_i$. During training, inputs $I_i(t)$, adjacency $\mathcal{N}_i$, and activity $v_i(t)$ are given; $\widehat{\mathbf{W}}, \tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}, \hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}$ are optimized by minimizing the difference between simulator $y_i(t)=dv_i(t)/dt$ and known-ODE predictions $\hat{y}_i(t)=d\hat{v}_i(t)/dt$,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}} = \sum_{i,t}\|\hat{y}_i(t) - y_i(t)\|_2.
\label{eq:known_ode_loss_pred}
\end{equation}
Even with the ODE structure fixed, recovery of $\widehat{\mathbf{W}}$ from voltage traces remains non-unique (see Appendix~\ref{app:degeneracy}). To constrain the solution space, we added regularization terms to $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}
+ \gamma_0\|\widehat{\mathbf{W}}\|_1 + \gamma_1\|\widehat{\mathbf{W}}\|_2
+ \eta_0\|\tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}\|_1 + \eta_1\|\tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}\|_2
+ \eta_2\|\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}\|_1 + \eta_3\|\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}\|_2.
\label{eq:known_ode_loss}
\end{equation}
The $\ell_1$ term $\gamma_0$ on $\widehat{\mathbf{W}}$ promotes sparse effective connectivity, while the $\ell_2$ term $\gamma_1$ penalizes large synaptic weights. The $\ell_1$ penalty $\eta_0$ on $\tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}$ shrinks the time constants toward the softplus origin, while the $\ell_2$ penalty $\eta_1$ prevents drift. The $\ell_1$ penalty $\eta_2$ on $\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}$ promotes sparse resting potentials, while the $\ell_2$ penalty $\eta_3$ penalizes large biases. Eq.~\ref{eq:known_ode_loss} is a general form: the coefficients $\gamma_0,\gamma_1,\eta_0,\eta_1,\eta_2,\eta_3$ are hyperparameters tuned via the agentic optimization procedure described in \ref{app:agent} , which can set a subset of them to zero.

The FlyVis model optimizes a biophysically motivated, connectome-constrained model on an optical flow task, which requires the neural activities to encode past visual stimuli. The stimulus baseline model  (Figure \ref{fig:baseline_arch}) predicts the activities $v_i(t)$ of all the neurons using the input stimulus at times $t, t-1, t-(t_w-1)$, where $t_w$ denotes a context window. The stimulus encoder and predictor MLP are jointly trained to minimize the MSE loss between prediction and ground truth voltage. Figure \ref{fig:stimulus_ctx_pearson} shows the prediction performance, quantified using Pearson's r, as a function of $t_w$; at $t_w=1$ the performance is poor and increases monotonically as a function of context length. This baseline serves to demonstrate that . Thus, the performance of any model that predicts the activity $v(t)$ based on the stimulus $s(t)$ and $v(t-1)$ must be bounded from below by the stimulus baseline with $t_w=1$.


\subsection{MLP baseline}

We used a plain MLP to predict the time derivative of neural activity based on the input stimulus and past activity. We begin with the noise free system, where $\sigma = 0$ in \ref{eq:simulated_update}, and use the model
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dv}{dt} = \mathrm{MLP}(s(t), v(t-1))\, .
\end{equation}
We train this model to minimize the MSE loss between the predicted and ground truth $dv/dt$. We observed that by varying the hyperparameters of the MLP we were able to accurately capture the 1-step evolution of neural activity. However, we failed to achieve a stable roll out on validation data beyond a few hundred time steps. When measuring the MSE on an 8000 step rollout on validation data we observed large (RMSE $\sim 1.0$) low frequency oscillations that did not go to zero even after significant increases to the training time [TODO: show this in a figure, or ok to just state?]. To address this, we added ResNet-style skip connections, which enable the network to easily model the low frequency oscillations in the MSE that were missed by the plain MLP. This intervention helps us learn a generalizable model for neural activity evolution and we are able to generate autoregressive rollouts of over 8000 steps on the held-out test data with Pearson's $r > 0.90$ as shown in Table \ref{tab:mlp_results}. While the model faithfully reproduces the rollout, it does not capture the true underlying dynamics. This is best illustrated by plotting the Jacobian of the MLP with respect to the stimuli and the other neuron activities, which shows a large deviation from expectations. The main reason is that the MLP misses the connectome constraint and is therefore free to take any combinations of the current neural activity and the stimulus to predict the next time step. A linear analysis of the MLP shows poor concordance with the true underlying weights.

We found that the addition of stochastic dynamics via an model noise term as in Eq. \ref{eq:simulated_update} with $\sigma > 0$ hurts the ability of the MLP to learn the underlying drift dynamics. At the high noise level of $\sigma=0.5$ the activity at the next time step is dominated by the noise compared to the drift term that is mostly close to zero. To compensate for this, we trained the MLP model to make predictions of the next $R$ time steps and minimized the MSE loss over $t+1, t+2, \cdots, t+R$. The $R$ step rollout training helps average out the uncorrelated noise and the model is able to learn the correct drift dynamics. Additionally, we initialized the weights and biases of the final layer of the MLP to zero, since this starts the optimization at $dv_i/dt = 0$ generating a constant and bounded rollout. We still observe a performance gap between the noise free conditions and those with noise added. It may be possible to bridge this gap with more input data that would allow for further averaging out of the noise term, or other architectural and training innovations. 

\subsection{EED model}

To improve the performance of the MLP we introduce a bottleneck architecture that we call the Encoder-Evolver-Decoder model. This can be viewed as a state space model where the internal dynamics are described by an evolving latent representation, which is then read out by the measured neurons. We were motivated to consider this architecture by the observation that the neural activity matrix can be accurately reconstructed using top $K$ singular values for $K \ll N$ \comment{TODO: add reconstruction error vs K plot or just numbers inline}. The model architecture, depicted in Figure \ref{fig:baseline_arch}C, consists of an auto-encoder that projects to/reconstructs the observed neural activity from a lower dimensional latent space. The visual stimulus is also encoded into a smaller representation and the next latent state is a function of the stimulus latent and the activity latent. Similar to the MLP baseline architecture above we added linear skip connections to each of the encoder, decoder, evolver and stimulus encoder MLPs.
