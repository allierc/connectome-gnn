\begin{abstract}

Synapse-level connectomes describe the structure of circuits, but not the electrical and chemical dynamics.  Conversely, large-scale recordings of neural activity capture these dynamics, but not the circuit structure.  We asked whether combining binary connectivity and recorded neural activity can be used to infer mechanistic models of neural circuits.  We trained a graph neural network model (GNN) to forecast the activity of \emph{Drosophila} visual system simulations.  Trained on activity trajectories in response to visual inputs, the model recovers effective connectivity weights, neuron types, and nonlinear activation functions, even when 400\% random connections are added to the adjacency matrix.  Moreover, it correctly predicts causal effects of connection removal, demonstrating the ability to infer mechanistic dependencies directly from activity data.

\end{abstract}

\section{Related work}


Prior studies were successful in retrieving functional connectivity, functional properties of neurons, or predictive models of neural activity at the population level, but a method to infer an interpretable mechanistic model of complex neural assemblies is yet missing. \citet{mi_connectome-constrained_2021} infer the hidden voltage dynamics and functional connectivity from calcium recordings of parts of the \emph{C. elegans} nervous system using a simple biophysical neuron model.  While its predictive performance is modest, it demonstrates that integrating real activity recordings with connectivity can yield experimentally testable predictions beyond simulation.  \citet{pospisil_fly_2024} used the connectome of \emph{Drosophila melanogaster} \citep{dorkenwald_neuronal_2024} to create simulations of full brain activity and inferred effective connectivity from known perturbations of individual neurons with a simple linear model.  Lacking information about connectivity, \citet{wang_foundation_2025} trained a foundation model of mouse visual cortex activity on real two-photon recordings from 135,000 neurons, which predicts responses to both natural videos and out-of-distribution stimuli across animals.
\citet{prasse_predicting_2022} focus on accurate prediction without recovering connectivity while \citet{yu_discovering_2025} infer symbolic dynamical equations without validating connectivity weights. Both work with simulated data and do not learn neuronal heterogeneity.

Our method learns the functional properties of individual neurons and their relative connectivity weights, enabling transfer of the learned dynamics to different network configurations with modified connectivity or different neuron type compositions.

\section{Methods}

\subsection*{Simulations} \label{method:simulation}
We simulated neural activity in the \emph{Drosophila} visual system using \textit{flyvis} pretrained models \cite{lappalainen_connectome-constrained_2024}. The recurrent neural network contains $13{,}741$ neurons from $65$ cell types and $434{,}122$ synaptic connections. We restricted the original $721$ retinotopic columns to the central subset of $217$. Each neuron is modeled as a non-spiking voltage compartment governed by
\begin{equation}
\tau_i\frac{dv_i(t)}{dt} = -v_i(t) + V_i^{\text{rest}}
+ \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i}\mathbf{W}_{ij}\,\text{ReLU}\!\big(v_j(t)\big)
+ I_i(t) + \sigma\,\xi_i(t),
\label{eq:simulated_update}
\end{equation}
where $v_i$ is the neuron membrane voltage, $\tau_i$ and $V_i^{\mathrm{rest}}$ are cell-type parameters, $\mathbf{W}_{ij}$ is the connectome synaptic weight, $I_i(t)$ is the visual input, and $\xi_i(t)\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ is an independent Gaussian noise scaled by $\sigma$. The later defines an intrinsic noise perturbing the dynamical trajectory of $v_i(t)$. We also added measurement noise to $v_i$(t), an independent Gaussian noise scaled by $\gamma$.

Visual inputs are derived from the DAVIS dataset~\cite{perazzi_benchmark_2016} and YouTube-VOS~\cite{perazzi_benchmark_2016}, both natural video benchmarks. Frames are cropped, converted to grayscale, and resampled
onto the hexagonal photoreceptor lattice of $217$ columns via a Gaussian box-eye filter, giving $1{,}736$ input neurons ($8$ photoreceptor types R1--R8 per column). DAVIS sequences are augmented with horizontal/vertical
flips and four rotations and split $80/20$ into train/validation. YouTube-VOS serves  as the held-out test set, ensuring that no test stimulus is seen during training or hyperparameter
optimisation. Models are additionally retrained on YouTube-VOS to verify that the optimized hyperparameters are not stimulus-dependent.






\subsubsection*{Graph neural network model} \label{method:GNN}
We approximate the simulated voltage dynamics by a message-passing GNN \cite{gilmer_neural_2017}
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\hat{v}_i(t)}{dt}
= f_\theta\!\Big(v_i(t),\,\mathbf{a}_i,\,
\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i} \widehat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}\,
g_\phi\!\big(v_j(t),\mathbf{a}_j\big)^2,\,
I_i(t)\Big).
\label{eq:gnn_ct}
\end{equation}
Nodes of the GNN correspond to neurons and edges correspond to functional synaptic connections. The GNN learns a latent embedding $\mathbf{a}_i\in\mathbb{R}^2$ for each neuron. The learnable vectors $\mathbf{a}_i\in\mathbb{R}^2$ represent compact latent identity used to capture cell-type-specific properties (like time constants and nonlinearities). Neuron update $f_\theta=\text{MLP}_0$ and edge message $g_\phi=\text{MLP}_1$ are three-layer perceptrons (width $64$, ReLU, linear output). $g_\phi$ maps presynaptic inputs $v_j$ to nonnegative messages (via squaring) depending on neural embedding $\mathbf{a}_j$, weighted by $\widehat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}$. $f_\theta$ processes the postsynaptic voltage $v_i$, aggregated input, and external input $I_i(t)$ to predict $d\hat{v}_i(t)/dt$, depending on neural embedding $\mathbf{a}_i$. During training, inputs $I_i(t)$, adjacency $\mathcal{N}_i$, and activity $v_i(t)$ are given. The MLPs, $\widehat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}$, and $\mathbf{a}_i$ are optimized by minimizing difference between simulator $y_i(t)=dv_i(t)/dt$ and GNN predictions $\hat{y}_i(t)=d\hat{v}_i(t)/dt$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}} = \sum_{i,t}\|\hat{y}_i(t) - y_i(t)\|_2
\label{eq:loss_pred}
\end{equation}
Many synaptic weight configurations give rise to identical neural activity, making the recovery of $\widehat{\mathbf{W}}$ from voltage traces fundamentally non-unique (see Appendix \ref{app:degeneracy}). To constrain the solution space, we added regularization terms to $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}$
\begin{eqnarray}
\mathcal{L} &=& \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}
+ \lambda_0\|\theta\|_1 + \lambda_1\|\phi\|_1 + \lambda_2\|\theta\|_2 + \lambda_3\|\phi\|_2
+ \gamma_0\|\widehat{\mathbf{W}}\|_1 + \gamma_1\|\widehat{\mathbf{W}}\|_2 \nonumber\\
&& +\; \mu_0\!\left\|\mathrm{ReLU}\!\left(-\tfrac{\partial\,f_\theta(v,m,\mathbf{a},I)}{\partial m}\right)\right\|_2
+ \mu_1\!\left\|\mathrm{ReLU}\!\left(-\tfrac{\partial\,g_\phi(v,\mathbf{a})}{\partial v}\right)\right\|_2 \nonumber +\; \mu_2\|g_\phi(v_\star,\mathbf{a})-v_\star\|_2.
\label{eq:loss_full}
\end{eqnarray}
The $\ell_1$ penalties $\lambda_0,\lambda_1$ on the MLP weights $\theta$ and $\phi$ promote sparsity, biasing $f_\theta$ and $g_\phi$ toward simpler functions; the $\ell_2$ penalties $\lambda_2,\lambda_3$ prevent weight explosion and stabilize training. The $\ell_1$ term $\gamma_0$ on $\widehat{\mathbf{W}}$ promotes sparse effective connectivity, while the $\ell_2$ term $\gamma_1$ penalizes large synaptic weights. The monotonicity term $\mu_0$ penalizes negative slopes of $f_\theta$ with respect to its aggregated synaptic input $m=\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i}\widehat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}\,g_\phi(v_j,\mathbf{a}_j)$, encoding the prior that the postsynaptic derivative increases with net excitatory drive. The monotonicity term $\mu_1$ penalizes negative slopes of $g_\phi$ with respect to $v$, encoding the prior that synaptic drive increases with presynaptic voltage. The normalization term $\mu_2$ anchors $g_\phi$ at a reference voltage $v_\star$, removing the scale degeneracy between $g_\phi$ and $\widehat{\mathbf{W}}$. Eq.~\ref{eq:loss_full} is a general form: the coefficients $\lambda_0,\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\lambda_3,\gamma_0,\gamma_1,\mu_0,\mu_1,\mu_2$ are hyperparameters tuned via the agentic optimization procedure described below, which can sets a subset of them to zero.


\subsubsection*{Agent-in-the-loop parameter optimisation}
\label{method:agent}
The combined space of simulation and machine learning parameters is too large
for grid search. We instead deploy a closed-loop system in which a large
language model agent (Claude Code, Anthropic) interprets experiment results,
maintains a structured summary, and proposes the next experiment by changing
the simulation regime and/or machine learning parameters
(Appendix~\ref{app:agent}). Each experiment trains the GNN with a new
configuration and collects metrics that the agent can compare to previous
runs. The primary optimisation target is either connectivity identifiability
($R^2_{\mathbf{W}}$) or rollout prediction accuracy. To this aim, the
agent follows a hypothesis-driven protocol. A testable hypothesis must be
stated before each experiment, and only experimental outcomes can validate or
falsify it. Experiments are organised into blocks of $8$--$16$ iterations,
with $4$ runs executed in parallel so that the agent reasons over a batch of
outcomes rather than a single trial, sharpening both confirmation and
falsification.

The resting membrane potential $V_i^{\mathrm{rest}}$ is the hardest parameter to extract. Results obtained with GNN are far below the Known-ODE baseline $R^2_{V^{\mathrm{rest}}}\sim0.9$ (see Table \ref{tab:cv_per_condition} and \ref{tab:cv_known_ode}). It is however a key property of the circuit important to recover as it sets the baseline excitability of each neuron. Notably $V_i^{\mathrm{rest}}$ recovery is made possible by a minimal modification of the stimulus sequences, namely prepending a $10\%$ blank period ($I_i(t)=0$) to each training sequence. During this silent window, each neuron relaxes towards $V_i^{\mathrm{rest}}$, giving the possibility for the GNN to extrapolate the $V_i^{\mathrm{rest}}$ values. This modification alone raises $R^2_{\widehat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}}$ from  $\sim0.1$ to $\sim0.8$. This highlights the impact of the choice of stimulus on parameter recovery; a real experiment on the fly visual system would be potentially unable to recover the resting potential in the absence of a sufficient amount of blank/dark stimulus frames. The solution was not discovered by the agentic loop HPO, which did not explore stimulus structures. Including the stimuli optimization in the agent search space is thus a necessary extension for future work.

\paragraph{Robustness to erroneous adjacency matrices.}
In practice, annotated connectome reconstructions contain both false negatives (missing
connections) and false positives (spurious connections). To test robustness to
both error types, we \textit{(i)} augmented the ground-truth adjacency matrix
with randomly added null edges ($+400\%$; $434{,}112 \to 2{,}170{,}560$ edges)
and \textit{(ii)} randomly removed edges ($-20\%$; $434{,}112 \to 347{,}000$
edges). In both cases the GNN was trained on the corrupted connectivity while
neural activity was simulated with the full ground-truth connectome
($434{,}112$ edges).

\section{Degeneracy of the inverse problem}
\label{app:degeneracy}
In the following, we characterise the dynamical equivalence class of $\mathbf{W}$, the set of all weight matrices that reproduce the observed trajectories. We show that Eq.~\eqref{eq:simulated_update} can be linearised per neuron, that this linear system admits a solution, and finally that it admits many different solutions.

\paragraph{Linearisation of the simulation equation.}
Rearranging Eq.~\eqref{eq:simulated_update} to isolate the incoming synaptic
weights gives
\begin{equation}
  \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i} \mathbf{W}_{ij}\,\text{ReLU}\!\big(v_j(t)\big)
  = \tau_i \frac{dv_i(t)}{dt} + v_i(t) - V_i^{\text{rest}} - I_i(t).
\end{equation}
Defining the presynaptic activity $h_j(t) = \text{ReLU}(v_j(t))$ and the
residual $b_i(t) = \tau_i \frac{dv_i(t)}{dt} + v_i(t) - V_i^{\text{rest}} -
I_i(t)$, and stacking the $T$ time-point constraints into matrix form, yields
an independent linear system for each postsynaptic neuron $i$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{H}_i\,\mathbf{w}_i = \mathbf{b}_i,
  \label{eq:linear_system}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{H}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_i}$ is the presynaptic
activity matrix, $d_i$ is the in-degree of neuron $i$, $\mathbf{w}_i \in
\mathbb{R}^{d_i}$ the incoming weight vector, and $\mathbf{b}_i \in
\mathbb{R}^T$ the stacked residuals. This holds for each postsynaptic neuron
$i = 1, \ldots, N$, where $N = 13{,}697$ and $d_i$ ranges from $1$ to $208$.
The null space $\ker(\mathbf{H}_i) = \{\boldsymbol{\delta} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_i}
: \mathbf{H}_i\boldsymbol{\delta} = \mathbf{0}\}$ characterises the
degeneracy: any $\mathbf{w}_i + \boldsymbol{\delta}$ with $\boldsymbol{\delta}
\in \ker(\mathbf{H}_i)$ satisfies Eq.~\eqref{eq:linear_system} equally and
produces identical dynamics. In the following, we characterise the
dynamical equivalence class of $\mathbf{W}$, the set of all weight
matrices that reproduce the observed trajectories.


\paragraph{Pseudoinverse recovery.}
Assuming $\tau_i$ and $V_i^{\mathrm{rest}}$ are known, Eq.~\eqref{eq:linear_system}
admits many solutions. Table~\ref{tab:pseudoinverse} compares six solvers that use
different SVD variance thresholds (90\%--100\%) or ridge regularisation.
Truncating to 90\% of the variance yields $R^2_{\mathbf{W}} = 0.07$, a weight
matrix almost uncorrelated with the ground truth, yet the recovered $\mathbf{W}$
reproduces neural activity with mean rollout Pearson $r = 0.96$ over 1{,}000 frames.
Solutions keeping $r = 1.00$, obtained with higher threshold or ridge, bring $R^2_{\mathbf{W}}$ to
$0.88$--$0.99$. This empirical results shows that the mapping from dynamics to connectivity is many-to-one.

\paragraph{Structural origin of the null space.}
In fact, the columnar organisation of the connectome directly implies the
existence of $\ker(\mathbf{H}_i)$, without SVD argument. The flyvis circuit
spans 217 retinotopic columns, and each postsynaptic neuron $i$ typically
receives input from $k_{i\alpha} > 1$ neurons of the same cell type $\alpha$
across columns. A moving stimulus crosses the eye so that column $c$ sees
what column $c-1$ saw moments earlier: same-type neurons produce time-shifted
copies of the same signal, giving the corresponding $k_{i\alpha}$ columns of
$\mathbf{H}_i$ rank $\approx 1$ instead of $k_{i\alpha}$. Any sum-zero
perturbation $\sum_{m=1}^{k_{i\alpha}} \delta_{j_m} = 0$ on those weights
leaves $\mathbf{H}_i \mathbf{w}_i$ unchanged, contributing $k_{i\alpha} - 1$
free directions to $\ker(\mathbf{H}_i)$. Summing over all neurons and cell
types:
\begin{equation}
  \dim\ker(\mathbf{H}) = \sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{\alpha:\,k_{i\alpha}>1}(k_{i\alpha}-1)
  = 308{,}160 \quad (71\%\ \text{of edges}).
  \label{eq:null_structural}
\end{equation}

To verify Eq.~\eqref{eq:null_structural}, we generated one single-type variant
per degenerate cell type: a perturbed matrix $\mathbf{W}' = \mathbf{W} +
\boldsymbol{\Delta}$, where $\boldsymbol{\Delta}$ applies sum-zero perturbations
at maximum amplitude ($8\times$ mean group weight) within the degenerate groups
of that type. Of the 65 flyvis cell types, 11 have no degenerate groups and 2
(Lawf2, Tm5b) have no outgoing edges; the remaining 52 types each yield one
variant. For each we computed $R^2_{\mathbf{W}}$ (weight-space agreement with GT)
and rollout Pearson $r$ (ODE trajectory agreement over 1{,}000 frames).
$R^2_{\mathbf{W}}$ ranges from 0.28 to 1.0 while mean rollout Pearson $r$ remains
above 0.94 (min: TmY10). Perturbed weights differ substantially from the ground
truth yet produce nearly identical dynamics (Table~\ref{tab:lambda_8}). Further, we looked for the minimal-edges $\mathbf{W}$ by collapsing each degenerate $(i,\alpha)$ group of $k_{i\alpha}$ edges onto a
single representative edge $e^* = \arg\max_e |W_e^*|$, zeroing the remaining
$k_{i\alpha}-1$ edges. We tested two variants: sum-preserving
($W_{e^*} = \sum_e W_e^*$, exact null-space member) and calibrated
($W_{e^*}$ minimising the per-group dynamics residual via one least-squares
scalar fit). Both zero $308{,}160$ edges ($71\%$ of $434{,}112$). The sum-preserving variant achieves mean rollout Pearson $r = 0.95$ with $R^2_{\mathbf{W}} = 0.10$.
The calibrated variant achieves mean rollout Pearson $r = 0.98$ with $R^2_{\mathbf{W}} = 0.39$.

%%% Goal of this section: explain why the known ODE method does not recover tau %%%

Given an ODE, sensitivity analysis [TODO: right term?][TODO: ref] helps identify directions in parameter space that minimally impact the solution. The solution $x_i(t)$ to a general ODE
\begin{equation}
\dot{x_i} = f_i(x, \theta)    
\end{equation}
with the state $x \in \R^n$ and parameters $\theta \in \R^p$, implicitly depends on $\theta$. The \emph{sensitivity} $S_{i\alpha} := \partial x_i / \partial \theta_\alpha$ of the $i$-th component to the specific parameter direction $\theta_\alpha$ is the solution to another ODE:
\begin{equation}
\dot{S_{i; \alpha}} = \sum_j \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j} S_{j; \alpha} + \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial \theta_\alpha} \label{eq:sens-ode}
\end{equation}
The ODE is initialized $x(t=0) = x_0$ to a value independent of the parameters $\theta$ and therefore we have $S_{i; \alpha}(t=0)= 0$. The second term on the right hand side describes the source term that causes $S_{i; \alpha}$ to gradually take on non-zero values. In the absence of the source term $S_{i; \alpha} = 0$ for all time. For a particular direction in parameter space defined by $\eta_\alpha$ we can define $s_i := \sum_\alpha S_{i\alpha} \eta_\alpha$ by projection. Then, 
\begin{equation}
    \dot{s}_i(t) = \sum_j J_{ij}(x) s_j + \sum_\alpha \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial \theta_\alpha} \eta_\alpha\ .
\end{equation}

The noise-free FlyVis ODE \ref{eq:simulated_update} can be rewritten
\begin{equation}
    \dot{x_i} = -\omega_i x_i + U_i + \sum_j G_{ij} \relu(x_j) + \omega_i I_i
\end{equation}
with a suitable redefinition of the parameters (TODO: fix)
\begin{equation}
    \omega_i = 1/\tau_i, \ U_i = \vrest_i \tau_i, \ W_{ij} = G_{ij} \tau_i
\end{equation}
The Jacobian $J(x)$, which appears in \ref{eq:sens-ode}, has the special form $J(x) = \Omega + G\odot H(x)$ in matrix notation where $H(x) = d \relu (x) / dx$ and $\odot$ denotes elementwise multiplication. The Jacobian is a constant matrix in any region of state space where the signs of $x_i$ are unchanged for all $i$, \eg, $J(x) = \Omega$, for $x \in \R_-^n$. We compute the second term in \ref{eq:sens-ode} for each parameter below:
\begin{eqnarray}
    \omega_j & : & \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial \omega_j} = (-x_i + I_i) \delta_{ij} \\
    U_j & : & \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial U_j} = \delta_{ij} \\
    G_{kl} & : & \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial G_{kl}} = \relu(x_l) \delta_{ik}
\end{eqnarray}
Note that $I_i$ is non-zero only for the input neurons. Let $M$ denote the set of neurons such that $x_i(t) >= 0$ and $I_i(t) = 0$ for all $i \in M$. If there exists such a non-empty set, then
\begin{equation}
    \sum_j \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial \omega_j} + \sum_{j, k} \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial G_{kj}} = -x_j \delta_{ij} + x_j \delta_{ik} 
\end{equation}

\section{Agent-in-the-loop parameter optimisation}
\label{app:agent}
The optimisation space combining simulation parameters (noise $\sigma$,
connectivity constraints, $n_{\mathrm{frames}}$) and machine learning parameters
(e.g.\ for GNN, $\lambda_0,\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\lambda_3,\gamma_0,\gamma_1,\mu_0,\mu_1,\mu_2$, learning rates, etc.) is too large for grid search. We instead deploy a closed loop
coupling three components: a GNN training engine, a large language model
agent (Claude Code, Anthropic), and an explicit structured summary. The system
operates through text file exchange. The instruction file defines the inverse
problem, the quantitative score objective, the metrics to track, the parameter
ranges, and the logging format. The structured summary maintains accumulated
understanding in two tiers: established principles and open questions that
persist across blocks, and a working record of current hypotheses and recent
iterations. A configuration file is edited by the agent to set the next
experiment; a results file is written by the experiment engine and read by
the agent.

The primary objective of the agentic loop is to optimise hyperparameters
against a quantitative score ($R^2_{\mathbf{W}}$ or rollout accuracy). But we
also constrain the agent to hypothesis-driven protocol: a testable
hypothesis must be stated before each experiment, and only experimental
outcomes can validate or falsify it. The next experiment is therefore
selected by the hypothesis the agent currently seeks to test rather than by a
numerical exploration/exploitation trade-off.

Experiments are organised into blocks of 8-16 iterations (multiples of 4 to
match the parallel batch size). The simulation
regime of each block can be specified by the user in the instruction file or
chosen by the agent. Within a block, the agent explores GNN training
parameters guided by its current hypothesis. To make the protocol robust, we
run $4$ experiments in parallel and submit the resulting batch of training
outcomes to the agent. Reasoning over a batch rather than a single trial
sharpens both confirmation and falsification. The structured summary also
serves as an explicit long-term memory, overcoming the finite context window
of the agent and enabling accumulation across hundreds of iterations. With
this protocol, convergence to the optimal configuration is reached within
$\sim 100$ GNN training experiments sweeping sometimes more than 10 parameters.

We initially adopted UCB tree search as in AlphaEvolve and FunSearch
\cite{romera-paredes_mathematical_2024,novikov_alphaevolve_2025}, where
parameter mutations are sampled from a tree balancing exploitation and
exploration. Once the hypothesis-driven constraint was introduced, we
found that the agent stopped consulting the UCB scores: parent selection and
parameter mutations were driven entirely by hypotheses formulated from the
structured summary and recent experimental results. We accordingly removed
the UCB component, which had become redundant.

- Stable:       mean conn_R2 >= 0.85, CV < 10%
- Unstable:     mean < 0.85 OR CV >= 10%
- Catastrophic: any seed conn_R2 < 0.50

## Scientific Method
Strict hypothesize -> test -> validate/falsify cycle:
1. Hypothesize: form a specific, testable prediction
2. Design experiment: change EXACTLY ONE parameter at a time (causality rule)
3. Run training: 4 slots (1 control + 3 experiments in EXPLORATION;

4. Analyze results: use conn_R2 AND rollout_pearson_r to understand convergence
5. Update understanding: revise hypotheses based on evidence

## FlyVis Model
Non-spiking compartment model of the Drosophila optic lobe:
tau_i * dv_i/dt = -v_i + V_rest_i + sum_j W_ij * g(v_j) + I_i(t)
- 13,741 neurons, 65 cell types, 434,112 edges
- 1,736 input neurons (photoreceptors, DAVIS visual input)
- Noise level: sigma=0.05 per time step
- 64,000 frames, delta_t = 0.02

sum_j W_ij * g_phi(v_j) -> agg_i         (weighted aggregation)
f_theta(v_i, agg_i, embed_i) -> dv_i/dt  (node update MLP)

Hyperparameter optimisation hits a ceiling, e.g.\ simulation with measurement
noise $\gamma=0.10$, where both the Known-ODE baseline and the best
agent-optimised GNN plateau near $R^2_{\mathbf{W}} \approx 0.80$. To overcome
this ceiling, we tested an agent promoted from config-only to code-authoring,
but under a discipline stricter than free editing. Each exploration block runs
a five-phase cycle. In a 10-minute \emph{research} (R) phase, the agent reads
the structured summary, consults a curated literature, optionally writes a
standalone analysis function to inspect prior-block results, and states one
falsifiable hypothesis. In a 10-minute \emph{staging} (S) phase, the agent
writes the mechanism function and a matched \texttt{pytest} test under an
isolated staging directory, where no codebase file is touched. In a 5-minute
\emph{wire-up} (C) phase, a 3--10 line edit imports the staged function into
one of four allow-listed code modules. The
\emph{training} (T) phase runs the standard cluster training across the
block's slots and iterations, during which the existing per-iteration HPO
loop still tunes the newly-exposed coefficient. Finally, an automatic
\emph{verdict} (V) phase triple-checks the primary metric across seeds: mean
improvement $\Delta R^2_{\mathbf{W}} \geq 0.005$, at least 75\% of seeds
strictly above the pre-block median, and no seed below the pre-block minimum
minus $0.02$ on any guarded metric. Failing blocks are reverted, preserving the failed diff in the branch history and appending the mechanism to a falsified-idea registry so that future research phases cannot retry it.

\subsection{Known ODE}

The activation functions in a biological neural network are seldom known {\it a priori}, but if they were, the process of parameter estimation is drastically simplified as the exact activation functions provide a powerful prior on the dynamics. Given the observed neural activity the parameters could be determined using gradient descent. The performance of this model serves as an upper bound on the performance of the GNN where we have the task of learning both the weights and the activation functions themselves.

\subsection{MLP baseline}

MLPs are known to be universal function approximators and therefore we fit a model
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dv}{dt} = \mathrm{MLP}(s(t), v(t-1))

We train this model for next time step prediction and show that the trained model generalizes to unseen stimuli producing stable rollouts of over 8000 steps on the test dataset. \subsection{EED model}

To improve the performance of the MLP we introduce a bottleneck architecture that we call the Encoder-Evolver-Decoder model. We were motivated by the observation that the neural activity matrix can be accurately reconstructed using top $K$ singular values for $K \ll N$. The model consists of an auto-encoder that projects to/reconstructs from a lower dimensional latent space. The dynamics is entirely representable in the lower dimensional space. We reasoned that this bottleneck may improve generalization performance.
