\section{Introduction}

Building a mechanistic model of a brain from measurements of its wiring and its activity is a long-standing goal of neuroscience, and one that is becoming concrete: Synapse-resolution connectomes paired with neural activity recordings from the same brain \citep{MICrONS-Consortium2025-pt} [V1DD, Zebrafish] open the prospect of mechanistic, data-constrained models of neural circuits. It is unclear, however, whether such data actually suffice to identify the underlying brain system, and what reliable computational strategies for doing so would look like.

Different threads of prior work bound the answer. 
System identification theory establishes that recovering dynamical systems parameters requires observations rich enough to disambiguate them \citep{Astrom1965-ri, Ljung2010-by}. In the neural circuit setting, this raises two questions: How varied must the observation be, and how can such variation be achieved experimentally? At least the former question can hardly be addressed without computational models.

In computational neuroscience, it is known that different parameter settings of the same circuit architecture can produce indistinguishable activity \citep{Prinz2004-pb, Marder2011-cc}, leaving open the extent to which degenerate circuit parameters can be disambiguated from data.

Machine learning models, trained by backpropagation, provide powerful tools to find circuit parameters that accurately uncover the input-output computation of a system, such as stimulus-response neural time series. Many machine learning architectures exist to solve sequential time-series problems \citep{Elman1990-ar, Vaswani2017-wz, Gu2023-at, Krishnan2015-ri, Chen2018-gd}, but general-purpose models are not designed to function like the brain and uncover its underlying parameters and mechanisms. Using such models as tools for neuroscience shifts the question from \emph{which architecture is most performant in predicting time-series} to \emph{which architecture is most suitable for brain simulation} --- that is, most interpretable and most easily constrained by the heterogenous measurements experimentalists actually collect.

Providing concrete answers to these questions requires access to ground-truth connectivity and dynamics from the same system, which no current experimental dataset fully provides: Available large-scale datasets contain either only partial brain measurements \citep{MICrONS-Consortium2025-pt}, activity but no connectivity measurements \citep{Ahrens2013-rd, lueckmann_zapbench_2025}, or connectivity \citep{Dorkenwald2024-ni} and activity \citep{Currier2025-af} from different individuals. While new datasets are underway, many of these system-identification questions can be addressed today in a fully computational framework built on recent large-scale, connectome-constrained circuit simulations \citep{Lappalainen2024-tq, Shiu2024-rz}. 

We developed a 'teacher--student' setup in which the teacher is a variant of a recent fly visual-system simulation \citep{Lappalainen2024-tq}, a continuous-time recurrent dynamical system defined on the fly connectome graph (45K neurons, 1.5M connections) and capable of detecting optical flow in natural video. We extended the teacher with the most recent whole-eye connectome data \citep{Dorkenwald2024-ni, Schlegel2024-qh} to test counterfactuals such as the convolutional assumption of the original model or connectivity uncertainty from EM measurements, resulting in model variants at varying scales ($13$K or $50$K neurons; $0.4$M or $5$M edges).
As students, we evaluate a broad selection of ML sequence models and propose as our primary model class an interpretable recurrent graph neural network (GNN) that can be flexibly conditioned on neural and synaptic measurements, and that admits learnable latent variables for circuit structure such as cell types.
As a baseline against which to compare the GNN, we use an oracle student that knows the true ODE of the teacher. Computational models in the form of differential equations of neural circuit electrical activity are often designed by tailoring equation components to match specific observed data \citep{Abbott1999-sh, Hodgkin1952-eq}, and an oracle of this form represents the strongest version of that choice. At brain scale, however, it is unlikely that a single ``true'' ODE exists, and models that flexibly parametrize neural and synaptic interactions and absorb measurement constraints become more practical than models hand-designed for a particular system or set of observations.
Finally, for any gradient-based model class, tuning training hyperparameters such that the same model class works equivalently well across data settings is itself nontrivial. We therefore introduce an agentic hyperparameter optimization (HPO) procedure that identifies a single GNN training configuration for robust system identification across conditions such as connectivity and activity completeness, or neuronal noise level.

\section{Related work}


Prior studies were successful in retrieving functional connectivity, functional properties of neurons, or predictive models of neural activity at the population level, but a method to infer an interpretable mechanistic model of complex neural systems is yet missing.

Several lines of work address related but distinct
problems.
A concurrent study by \citet{Lueckmann2026-hl} establishes a similar in silico testbed using a larval zebrafish visuomotor circuit of $\sim$36 units, showing that LLM-guided code search recovers effective connectivity when given structural priors. 
\citet{mi_connectome-constrained_2021} infer the hidden voltage dynamics and functional connectivity from calcium recordings of parts of the \emph{C. elegans} nervous system using a simple biophysical neuron model.  While its predictive performance is modest, it demonstrates that integrating real activity recordings with connectivity can yield experimentally testable predictions beyond simulation.  \citet{pospisil_fly_2024} used the connectome of \emph{Drosophila melanogaster} \citep{dorkenwald_neuronal_2024} to create simulations of full brain activity and inferred effective connectivity from known perturbations of individual neurons with a simple linear model.  Lacking information about connectivity, \citet{wang_foundation_2025} trained a foundation model of mouse visual cortex activity on real two-photon recordings from 135,000 neurons, which predicts responses to both natural videos and out-of-distribution stimuli across animals.
\citet{prasse_predicting_2022} focus on accurate prediction without recovering connectivity while \citet{yu_discovering_2025} infer symbolic dynamical equations without validating connectivity weights. Both work with simulated data and do not learn neuronal heterogeneity.

Our method learns the functional properties of individual neurons and their relative connectivity weights, enabling transfer of the learned dynamics to different network configurations with modified connectivity (or different neuron type compositions).

\section{Methods}

\subsection*{Flyvis simulations} \label{method:simulation}


We simulated neural activity in the \emph{Drosophila} visual system using Flyvis pretrained models \citep{lappalainen_connectome-constrained_2024}. We restricted the original $721$
retinotopic columns to the central subset of $217$
columns, covering the functionally relevant central visual field while substantially reducing memory and runtime requirements. This way, the recurrent neural network contains $13{,}741$ neurons from $65$ cell types and $434{,}122$ synaptic connections. To address different modeling assumptions and scalability, we simulate model variants as specified in Table~\ref{tab:network_variants} -- for instance the full network with  $45{,}669$ neurons  and $1{,}513{,}231$ synaptic connections. 
Each neuron is modeled as a non-spiking voltage compartment governed by
\begin{equation}
\tau_i\frac{dv_i(t)}{dt} = -v_i(t) + V_i^{\text{rest}}
+ \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i}\mathbf{W}_{ij}\,\text{ReLU}\!\big(v_j(t)\big)
+ I_i(t) + \sigma\,\xi_i(t),
\label{eq:simulated_update}
\end{equation}
where $v_i$ is the neuron membrane voltage, $\tau_i$ and $V_i^{\mathrm{rest}}$ are cell-type parameters, $\mathbf{W}_{ij}$ is the connectome synaptic weight, $I_i(t)$ is the visual input. 
Visual inputs are derived from the DAVIS-2017 dataset \citep{Pont-Tuset_arXiv_2017}, a benchmark dataset for annotated natural videos (see detailed in Appendix \ref{app:agent}). $\xi_i(t)\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ is an independent Gaussian noise
scaled by $\sigma$ (model noise), perturbing the dynamical trajectory of $v_i(t)$. We additionally applied a measurement noise directly to $v_i(t)$, Gaussian noise scaled by $\gamma$. See examples of simulated data in \label{fig:stimulus_hexagons}, with our without model and measurement noise in Fig. \ref{fig:simulations}.

\subsubsection*{Graph neural network model} \label{method:GNN}
We approximate the simulated voltage dynamics by a message-passing GNN \citep{gilmer_neural_2017}
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\hat{v}_i(t)}{dt}
= f_\theta\!\Big(v_i(t),\,\mathbf{a}_i,\,
\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i} \hat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}\,
g_\phi\!\big(v_j(t),\mathbf{a}_j\big)^2,\,
I_i(t)\Big).
\label{eq:gnn_ct}
\end{equation}
Nodes of the GNN correspond to neurons and edges correspond to functional synaptic connections. The GNN learns a latent embedding $\mathbf{a}_i\in\mathbb{R}^2$ for each neuron. The learnable vectors $\mathbf{a}_i\in\mathbb{R}^2$ represent compact latent identity, they can map to cell-type-specific properties (like time constants and nonlinearities). Neuron update $f_\theta=\text{MLP}_0$ and edge message $g_\phi=\text{MLP}_1$ are three-layer perceptrons (width $64$, ReLU, linear output). $g_\phi$ maps presynaptic inputs $v_j$ to nonnegative messages (via squaring) depending on latent embedding $\mathbf{a}_j$, weighted by $\hat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}$. $f_\theta$ processes the postsynaptic voltage $v_i$, aggregated input, and external input $I_i(t)$ to predict $d\hat{v}_i(t)/dt$, depending also on latent embedding $\mathbf{a}_i$. During training, inputs $I_i(t)$, adjacency $\mathcal{N}_i$, and activity $v_i(t)$ are given. The MLPs, $\hat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}$, and $\mathbf{a}_i$ are optimized by minimizing difference between simulator $y_i(t)=dv_i(t)/dt$ and GNN predictions $\hat{y}_i(t)=d\hat{v}_i(t)/dt$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}} = \sum_{i,t}\|\hat{y}_i(t) - y_i(t)\|_2
\label{eq:loss_pred}
\end{equation}
Many synaptic weight configurations give rise to identical neural activity, making the recovery of $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ from voltage traces fundamentally non-unique (see Appendix \ref{app:degeneracy}). To constrain the solution space, we added regularization terms to $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}$
\begin{eqnarray}
\mathcal{L} &=& \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}
+ \lambda_0\|\theta\|_1 + \lambda_1\|\phi\|_1 + \lambda_2\|\theta\|_2 + \lambda_3\|\phi\|_2
+ \gamma_0\|\hat{\mathbf{W}}\|_1 + \gamma_1\|\hat{\mathbf{W}}\|_2 \nonumber\\
&& +\; \mu_0\!\left\|\mathrm{ReLU}\!\left(-\tfrac{\partial\,f_\theta(v,m,\mathbf{a},I)}{\partial m}\right)\right\|_2
+ \mu_1\!\left\|\mathrm{ReLU}\!\left(-\tfrac{\partial\,g_\phi(v,\mathbf{a})}{\partial v}\right)\right\|_2 \nonumber +\; \mu_2\|g_\phi(v_\star,\mathbf{a})-v_\star\|_2.
\label{eq:loss_full}
\end{eqnarray}
The $\ell_1$ penalties $\lambda_0,\lambda_1$ on the MLP weights $\theta$ and $\phi$ promote sparsity, biasing $f_\theta$ and $g_\phi$ toward simpler functions; the $\ell_2$ penalties $\lambda_2,\lambda_3$ prevent weight explosion and stabilize training. The $\ell_1$ term $\gamma_0$ on $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ promotes sparse effective connectivity, while the $\ell_2$ term $\gamma_1$ penalizes large synaptic weights. The monotonicity term $\mu_0$ penalizes negative slopes of $f_\theta$ with respect to its aggregated synaptic input $m=\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i}\hat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}\,g_\phi(v_j,\mathbf{a}_j)$, encoding the prior that the postsynaptic derivative increases with net excitatory drive. The monotonicity term $\mu_1$ penalizes negative slopes of $g_\phi$ with respect to $v$, encoding the prior that synaptic drive increases with presynaptic voltage. The normalization term $\mu_2$ anchors $g_\phi$ at a reference voltage $v_\star$, removing the scale degeneracy between $g_\phi$ and $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ in \cref{eq:gnn_ct}. We tuned all the hyperparameters $\lambda_0,\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\lambda_3,\gamma_0,\gamma_1,\mu_0,\mu_1,\mu_2$ and the MLPs structure with an agentic optimization procedure described in Appendix \ref{app:agent}.


\subsubsection*{Benchmark}
We compare GNN results with four baseline models detailed in Appendix~\ref{app:benchmark}: \emph{Known-ODE}, which assumes knowledge of the full functional form of the ODE and learns the circuit parameters $\tau, \vrest, W$; \emph{\mlpbase}, which predicts $dv/dt$ as a function of the current $v(t)$ and $I(t)$ using a 2-layer MLP (640 hidden units) with ResNet-style skip connections; \emph{\eedbase}, which predicts neural activity using a 256D nonlinear dynamical system that is read out by the observed activity; and \emph{Stimulus-driven MLP}, which predicts $v(t)$ based only on the past stimulus $I(t-(w-1)), \cdots, I(t-1), I(t)$. These baselines span the spectrum from full mechanistic knowledge (Known-ODE) to purely data-driven prediction (\stimbase), while \mlpbase{ }and \eedbase{ }adopt distinct dynamical-systems perspectives without incorporating additional inductive bias.

$\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}$ ($13{,}741$ each). The ReLU
activation is shared across neurons; cell-type assignments are not
given. With $\ell_1$/$\ell_2$ regularisation on the parameters and an MSE
one-step prediction loss (Appendix~\ref{app:benchmark}), the Known-ODE
achieved near-perfect activity prediction over $8{,}000$ test frames
and near-perfect parameter recovery over $>4.5\cdot10^5$ values  (Fig.~\ref{fig:know_ode_rollout},
Fig.~\ref{fig:known_ode_params_3col_noise_comparison}). Even at high model noise ($\sigma=0.5$), the model clusters the $65$
cell types correctly and recovers all circuit parameters, with the
exception of a small fraction of neurons ($<10\%$). Notably, the Known-ODE trained on
noisy activity reproduced the noise-free rollout. It cannot
explicitly fit the stochastic fluctuations and instead converges on the
underlying deterministic dynamics. This results sets an upper bound: when the
ODE form is given, the strongly ill-posed inverse problem is well tractable.
Conversely, the GNN does not have access to the update equation. It learns jointly
the connectivity, a per-neuron embedding, and two signalling functions
$f_\theta, g_\phi$ that together express a wide family of update rules
(Eq.~\ref{eq:gnn_ct}). To overcome the degeneracy problem, we optimized the GNN training scheme with an agentic loop (Appendix~\ref{app:agent} and \ref{app:GNN}). As a result, the GNN achieved near-perfect activity prediction (Table~\ref{tab:cv_gnn_vs_baselines},
Fig.~\ref{fig:rollout_3col_noise_comparison}) at all noise model levels, and
likewise reproduces the noise-free rollout when trained on noisy
activity. Strikingly, parameter recovery is comparable to the
Known-ODE upper bound. Connectivity weights, time constants, resting potentials
and cell-type clusters are all recovered with accuracy approaching the
upper bound, despite the GNN having no prior on the form of the dynamics
(Table~\ref{tab:cv_gnn_vs_baselines}).

\paragraph{GNN recovers Flyvis with realistic FlyWire edges}
% This fits into results section
\cite{lappalainen_connectome-constrained_2024} compiles whole-eye connectivity from local reconstructions. The resulting flyvis model connectome is a convolutional (translation-invariant) graph: Every visual column shares the same set of 2,140 edge templates, each parametrized by source type, target type, and column offset. While this assumption is a good approximation for the underlying biology, it discards per-column variation of connectivity that is present in the fly and available in more recent whole-brain reconstructions (FlyWire) \citep{dorkenwald_neuronal_2024, Schlegel2024-qh}. To test the counterfactual whether the GNN would recover a dynamical visual system with real biological synaptic wiring variability -- or in other words, whether the GNN inference capability was limited by the convolutional assumption --  we constructed a hybrid connectome variant that augments the flyvis graph with column-resolved FlyWire connectivity data, while remaining compatible with the trained flyvis model parameters (Fig.~\ref{fig:flywire}a, Methods).

\section{Degeneracy of the inverse problem}
\label{app:degeneracy}


% FROM RESULTS:
% In each case, the model almost perfectly predicts the one-step update and is able to perform an auto-regressive rollout on held out stimuli for 8000 steps. However, despite perfect information about the dynamical equation we find that certain parameters cannot be accurately inferred. Parameter recovery improves upon the addition of a small amount of noise $\sigma=0.05$ and the recovery is perfect at the highest level of $\sigma=0.5$. This is due to a continuous degeneracy in the parameter space of the FlyVis ODE model simulated on naturalistic videos, that is, there is a \emph{degenerate subspace} in parameter space along which different ODEs with the same initial conditions produce the exact same solution. The chosen simulation parameters $(\tau_i, W_{ij}, \vrest_i)$ comprise one point in the degenerate subspace from which we generate a training dataset $\{v_i(t) | \ t \in [0, T]\}$ but gradient descent with random initialization of parameters can converge to any point in this space. Concretely, as a toy example, the parameters $\theta_1, \theta_2$ of the ODE $\dot{x} = -(\theta_1 + \theta_2)x, \ x(0) = 0$, cannot be uniquely determined from the solution $x(t)$ alone, but the combination $\theta_1 + \theta_2$ can be. To understand the degenerate space in equation \ref{eq:simulated_update}, first we observe that while the equation is nonlinear in $v(t)$, it is linear in the parameters $\theta_\alpha := (\tau_i, W_{ij}, \vrest_i)$, and therefore the problem of determining the parameters from given training data $v(t)$ can be written as $A\theta = b$, where $A$ is $(NT - 1) \times (2N + N_E)$ design matrix and $b$ is a vector of length $NT-1$. With this formulation, it is easy to see that if the training data contained a neuron $i$ that was always at its resting potential, \ie, $v_i(t) = \vrest_i \ \forall \ t$, then it is impossible to identify $\tau_i$ because the column multiplying $\tau_i$ in $A$ is identically 0. However, upon the addition of model noise, the stochastic dynamics decreases the likelihood of finding such a neuron. Similarly, if a neuron is in a constantly excited state the training data for that neuron would allow us to probe its presynaptic weights accurately but we would be underpowered to determine $\tau$ and $\vrest$. We do not rule out the possibility that there exists a family of stimuli, perhaps uncorrelated white noise, that would allow for perfect recovery of parameters in the absence of noise. This baseline helps demonstrate that the flyvis in the degenerate subspace can produce perfect one-step predictions and perfect rollouts but fail to recover the true parameters of the system. For further details on the degeneracy and parameter recovery, we refer the interested reader to Appendix \ref{app:degeneracy}.

Recall that the FlyVis stochastic dynamical system in \ref{eq:simulated_update}, in the absence of noise ($\sigma=0$), takes the form:
\begin{equation}
    \tau_i\frac{dv_i(t)}{dt} = -v_i(t) + V_i^{\text{rest}}
+ \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i}\mathbf{W}_{ij}\,\text{ReLU}\!\big(v_j(t)\big)
+ I_i(t) \label{eq:flyvis-app-noise-free-ode}
\end{equation}
Our goal is to infer the circuit parameters $(\tau_i, \vrest_i, W_{ij})$ from one solution of this ODE $\tilde{v}_i(t)$ and the stimulus $I_i(t)$. The ODE parameters can be decomposed into a separate set for each neuron $i$, namely, $\theta_{i\alpha} := (\tau_i, \vrest_i, W_{ij})$ is a vector of length $2 + |\mathcal{N}_i|$ indexed by $\alpha$, where $\mathcal{N}_i$ is the set of presynaptic partners of neuron $i$. We assume the solution $\tilde{v}_i(t)$ was generated by a parameter set $\theta$ and solving the inverse problem gives us an inferred set $\hat{theta}$. We show here that there are \emph{continuous} deformations in parameter space that lead to equivalent solutions, therefore, the system does not admit a unique solution making the inverse problem ill-posed.

\Cref{eq:flyvis-app-noise-free-ode} can be rewritten, for the purposes of parameter inference
\begin{equation}
    -\tau_i \dot{\tilde{v}}_i + \vrest_i + \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}_i} W_{ij}h_j(t) = \tilde{v}_i(t) - I_i(t)
    \label{eq:flyvis-linear-rewrite}
\end{equation}
where $h_j(t) := \relu(\tilde{v}_j(t))$ are the pre-synaptic activities.
Observe that while the ODE is non-linear in the state variable $v$ it is
\emph{linear} in the circuit parameters $\theta_{i\alpha}$ defined above. So,
given a solution $\tilde{v}_i(t)$ sampled at discrete time points $t=0,
\ldots, T-1$, we can rewrite Eq.~\ref{eq:flyvis-linear-rewrite} as a separate
linear system to solve for each neuron $i$,
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{A}_i \, \boldsymbol{\theta}_i = \mathbf{b}_i,
    \label{eq:parameter-linear-system}
\end{equation}
in terms of the design matrix $\mathbf{A}_i$ of dimension $T \times (2 + |\mathcal{N}_i|)$,
and the vectors $\boldsymbol{\theta}_i$ of dimension $2 + |\mathcal{N}_i|$ and
$\mathbf{b}_i$ of dimension $T$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{A}_i := \begin{pmatrix}
        -\dot{\tilde{v}}_i(0) & 1 & h_{j_1}(0) & h_{j_2}(0) & \cdots  \\
        -\dot{\tilde{v}}_i(1) & 1 & h_{j_1}(1) & h_{j_2}(1) & \cdots  \\
        \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots &
    \end{pmatrix},\quad
    \boldsymbol{\theta}_i := \begin{pmatrix}
        \tau_i \\ \vrest_i \\ W_{ij_1} \\ W_{ij_2} \\ \vdots
    \end{pmatrix},\quad
    \mathbf{b}_i := \begin{pmatrix}
        \tilde{v}_i(0) - I_i(0) \\
        \tilde{v}_i(1) - I_i(1) \\
        \vdots
    \end{pmatrix}
    \label{eq:explicit-linear-system}
\end{equation}
where $j_1, j_2, \ldots$ are the pre-synaptic neurons in $\mathcal{N}_i$.
We emphasise that this linear system is not a linearisation or approximation
but a property of the voltage-based firing-rate models used in flyvis
\citep{Lappalainen2024-tq}.

The linear system Eq.~\ref{eq:parameter-linear-system} can be solved to
infer the simulation parameters $\theta_{i\alpha}$. If the column space is
not full rank the system admits a continuous degeneracy defined by the
kernel of $\mathbf{A}_i$. In particular, if there exists a vector
$\boldsymbol{\eta}_i$ satisfying $\mathbf{A}_i \, \boldsymbol{\eta}_i =
\mathbf{0}$ for every $i$, then the family of parameters
$\boldsymbol{\theta}'_i = \boldsymbol{\theta}_i + \lambda \,
\boldsymbol{\eta}_i$ are solutions to the inverse problem for all $\lambda$.
Furthermore, a forward simulation with parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}'$
under the same stimulus $I_i$ and initial condition $\tilde{v}_i(t{=}0)$
would generate the same solution $\tilde{v}_i(t)$.

In the following, we present explicit examples of the degenerate space.
This serves to explain why parameter recovery from neural activity in the
flyvis system \emph{without} intrinsic noise does not work perfectly, even
when the activation functions are known (Known-ODE). The issue is
exacerbated when fitting GNNs without noise, where the activation functions
are estimated simultaneously with the parameters.

\subsubsection*{$\tau$ recovery}

If the network structure and stimulus are such that a neuron $i$ is in a resting state during the entire experiment, then we have $\tilde{v}_i(t) = \vrest_i$ and $\dot{\tilde{v}}_i(t) = 0$ for all observed $t$. In this case, the first column of $A_i$ is identically zero and hence it is impossible to recover $\tau_i$. A neuron can be in constant rest, \eg, if it does not receive an external stimulus and all its pre-synaptic neurons contribute sub-threshold/negative activities, resulting in $h_{j_1}(t), h_{j_2}(t), \cdots = 0$. In this case, the corresponding weights to $W_i$ are also impossible to determine. \subsubsection*{$\vrest$ recovery}

Other scenarios make it impossible to unambiguously recover $\vrest$. 
Let's suppose two neurons $i$ and $j$ are both at rest, and that $j$ is a pre-synaptic partner of $i$. If all incoming connections to $i$ and $j$ are sub-threshold, except for $j\rightarrow i$ and if $\vrest_j > 0$, then
\begin{equation}
    A_i := \begin{pmatrix}
        0 & 1 & \vrest_j & 0 & \cdots  \\
        0 & 1 & \vrest_j & 0 & \cdots  \\
        \vdots & \vdots & \vdots &\vdots & 
    \end{pmatrix} \text{and} \ \mathbf{A_j} := \begin{pmatrix}
        0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots  \\
        0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots  \\
        \vdots & \vdots & \vdots &\vdots & 
    \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
We now have a family of solutions where $\hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_j = \vrest_j $ and $\hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_i + \vrest_j\hat{W}_{ij} = \vrest_j$. Like in the previous example, $\tau_i$ and $\tau_j$ are undetermined because the neurons are at rest, but we have a new degeneracy with a solution $\hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_j = \vrest_j, \hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_i = \vrest_j ( 1 - \hat{W}_{ij} )$ for \emph{any} value of $\hat{W}_{ij}$.% generated by the optimization.

To study degeneracies within the space of weight matrices, we fix the values of $\vrest$ and $\tau$ and rearrange \cref{eq:flyvis-linear-rewrite} yielding

\begin{equation}

\end{equation}
Redefining the residual term $b_i(t) = \tau_i \frac{dv_i(t)}{dt} + v_i(t) - V_i^{\text{rest}} - I_i(t)$, we have 
% Defining the presynaptic activity $h_j(t) = \text{ReLU}(v_j(t))$ and the
% residual $b_i(t) = \tau_i \frac{dv_i(t)}{dt} + v_i(t) - V_i^{\text{rest}} -

  \mathbf{H}_i\,\mathbf{w}_i = \mathbf{b}_i,

\in \ker(\mathbf{H}_i)$ satisfies Eq.~\eqref{eq:linear_system} equally and
produces identical dynamics. In the following, we characterise the
dynamical equivalence class of $\mathbf{W}$, the set of all weight

\section{Agent-in-the-loop parameter optimisation}
\label{app:agent}

The optimisation space combining simulation parameters (noise $\sigma$,
connectivity constraints, $n_{\mathrm{frames}}$) and machine learning parameters
(e.g.\ for GNN, $\lambda_0,\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\lambda_3,\gamma_0,\gamma_1,\mu_0,\mu_1,\mu_2$, learning rates, etc.) is too large for grid search. We instead deploy a closed loop
coupling three components: a GNN training engine, a large language model
agent (Claude Code, Anthropic), and an explicit structured summary. The system
operates through text file exchange. The instruction file defines the inverse
problem, the quantitative score objective, the metrics to track, the parameter
ranges, and the logging format. The structured summary maintains accumulated
understanding in two tiers: established principles and open questions that
persist across blocks, and a working record of current hypotheses and recent
iterations. A configuration file is edited by the agent to set the next
experiment; a results file is written by the experiment engine and read by
the agent.

The primary objective of the agentic loop is to optimise hyperparameters
against a quantitative score ($R^2_{\mathbf{W}}$ or rollout accuracy). But we
also constrain the agent to hypothesis-driven protocol: a testable
hypothesis must be stated before each experiment, and only experimental
outcomes can validate or falsify it. The next experiment is therefore
selected by the hypothesis the agent currently seeks to test rather than by a
numerical exploration/exploitation trade-off.

Experiments are organised into blocks of 8-16 iterations (multiples of 4 to
match the parallel batch size). The agent does not start from scratch, it is initialised with a configuration established from prior works. The simulation
regime of each block can be specified by the user in the instruction file or
chosen by the agent. Within a block, the agent explores GNN training
parameters guided by its current hypothesis. To make the protocol robust, we
run $4$ experiments in parallel and submit the resulting batch of training
outcomes to the agent. Reasoning over a batch rather than a single trial
sharpens both confirmation and falsification. The structured summary also
serves as an explicit long-term memory, overcoming the finite context window
of the agent and enabling accumulation across hundreds of iterations. With
this protocol, convergence to the optimal configuration is reached within
$\sim 100$ GNN training experiments sweeping sometimes more than 10 parameters.

We initially adopted UCB tree search as in AlphaEvolve and FunSearch
\citep{romera-paredes_mathematical_2024,novikov_alphaevolve_2025}, where
parameter mutations are sampled from a tree balancing exploitation and
exploration. Once the hypothesis-driven constraint was introduced, we
found that the agent stopped consulting the UCB scores: parent selection and
parameter mutations were driven entirely by hypotheses formulated from the
structured summary and recent experimental results. We accordingly removed
the UCB component, which had become redundant.

## Scientific Context
Core research question: Can a GNN recover the full synaptic weight matrix
from noisy Drosophila optic lobe activity (13,741 neurons, sigma=0.05)?
The GNN inverts neural dynamics: given activity traces with additive noise,
learn W such that GNN dynamics reproduce observed activity. The inverse
problem is highly underdetermined - the balance between regularization,

- coeff_g_phi_diff prevents trivial g_phi collapse - critical for W recovery
- DAL (aug) may have a cliff: above DAL=35 the model may over-fit to noise

\subsection{Known-ODE} \label{app:known_ode}
The known-ODE model fixes the functional form of the dynamics to the ground-truth flyvis equation and learns only the biophysical parameters and the connectivity:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\tau}_i\,\frac{d\hat{v}_i(t)}{dt}
= -\hat{v}_i(t) + \hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_i
+ \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i} \hat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}\,\mathrm{ReLU}\!\big(v_j(t)\big)
+ I_i(t).
\label{eq:known_ode}
\end{equation}
The learnable parameters are the per-edge weights $\hat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}$, the per-neuron time constants $\hat{\tau}_i = \mathrm{softplus}(\tilde{\tau}_i)$ (with $\tilde{\tau}_i\in\mathbb{R}$ the underlying optimized parameter), and the per-neuron resting potentials $\hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_i$. During training, inputs $I_i(t)$, adjacency $\mathcal{N}_i$, and activity $v_i(t)$ are given; $\hat{\mathbf{W}}, \tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}, \hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}$ are optimized by minimizing the difference between simulator $y_i(t)=dv_i(t)/dt$ and known-ODE predictions $\hat{y}_i(t)=d\hat{v}_i(t)/dt$,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}} = \sum_{i,t}\|\hat{y}_i(t) - y_i(t)\|_2.
\label{eq:known_ode_loss_pred}
\end{equation}
Even with the ODE structure fixed, recovery of $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ from voltage traces remains non-unique (see Appendix~\ref{app:degeneracy}). To constrain the solution space, we added regularization terms to $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}
+ \gamma_0\|\hat{\mathbf{W}}\|_1 + \gamma_1\|\hat{\mathbf{W}}\|_2
+ \eta_0\|\tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}\|_1 + \eta_1\|\tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}\|_2
+ \eta_2\|\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}\|_1 + \eta_3\|\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}\|_2.
\label{eq:known_ode_loss}
\end{equation}
The $\ell_1$ term $\gamma_0$ on $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ promotes sparse effective connectivity, while the $\ell_2$ term $\gamma_1$ penalizes large synaptic weights. The $\ell_1$ penalty $\eta_0$ on $\tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}$ shrinks the time constants toward the softplus origin, while the $\ell_2$ penalty $\eta_1$ prevents drift. The $\ell_1$ penalty $\eta_2$ on $\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}$ promotes sparse resting potentials, while the $\ell_2$ penalty $\eta_3$ penalizes large biases. Eq.~\ref{eq:known_ode_loss} is a general form: the coefficients $\gamma_0,\gamma_1,\eta_0,\eta_1,\eta_2,\eta_3$ are hyperparameters tuned via the agentic optimization procedure described in Appendix \ref{app:agent} , which can set a subset of them to zero. In practice we retain a single regularization profile for every
Known-ODE result reported in the paper, namely the best paremeters found by the
agentic exploration run on noise-free data (Appendix~\ref{app:agent}):
$\gamma_0=10^{-5}$ ($\ell_1$ on $\hat{\mathbf W}$),
$\eta_1=3\!\times\!10^{-3}$ ($\ell_2$ on $\tilde{\boldsymbol\tau}$),
$\eta_3=10^{-3}$ ($\ell_2$ on $\hat{\mathbf V}^{\mathrm{rest}}$), with
the remaining three coefficients pinned to zero. The same coefficients
are applied unchanged to the noisy flyvis conditions and to the
FlyWireRF runs.

\subsection{MLP baseline}

We used a plain MLP to predict the time derivative of neural activity based on the input stimulus and past activity. We begin with the noise free system, where $\sigma = 0$ in \ref{eq:simulated_update}, and use the model
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dv}{dt} = \mathrm{MLP}(s(t), v(t-1))\, .
\end{equation}

We train this model to minimize the MSE loss between the predicted and ground truth $dv/dt$. We observed that by varying the hyperparameters of the MLP we were able to accurately capture the 1-step evolution of neural activity. However, we failed to achieve a stable roll out on validation data beyond a few hundred time steps. When measuring the MSE on an 8000 step rollout on validation data we observed large (RMSE $> 1.0$) low frequency oscillations that did not go to zero even after significant increases to the training time. To address this, we added ResNet-style skip connections, which enable the network to easily model the low frequency oscillations in the MSE that were missed by the plain MLP. This intervention helps us learn a generalizable model for neural activity evolution and we are able to generate autoregressive rollouts of over 8000 steps on the held-out test data with Pearson's $r > 0.90$ (data not shown here). We found that the addition of stochastic dynamics via an model noise term as in Eq. \ref{eq:simulated_update} with $\sigma > 0$ hurts the ability of the MLP to learn the underlying drift dynamics. At the high noise level of $\sigma=0.5$ the activity at the next time step is dominated by the noise compared to the drift term that is mostly close to zero. To compensate for this, we trained the MLP model to make predictions of the next $R$ time steps and minimized the MSE loss over $t+1, t+2, \cdots, t+R$. The $R$ step rollout training helps average out the uncorrelated noise and the model is able to learn the correct drift dynamics. Additionally, we initialized the weights and biases of the final layer of the MLP to zero, since this starts the optimization at $dv_i/dt = 0$ generating a constant and bounded rollout. We still observe a performance gap between the noise free conditions and those with noise added. It may be possible to bridge this gap with more input data that would allow for further averaging out of the noise term, or other architectural and training innovations. 

\subsection{EED model}

To improve the performance of the MLP we introduce a bottleneck architecture that we call the Encoder-Evolver-Decoder model. This can be viewed as a state space model where the internal dynamics are described by an evolving latent representation, which is then read out by the measured neurons. We were motivated to consider this architecture by the observation that the neural activity matrix can be accurately reconstructed using top $K$ singular values for $K \ll N$ \comment{TODO: add reconstruction error vs K plot or just numbers inline}. The visual stimulus is also encoded into a smaller representation and the next latent state is a function of the stimulus latent and the activity latent. \subsection{Agentic HPO and training.}
The optimised networks of the GNN are $g_\phi$ and $f_\theta$ of
Eq.~\ref{eq:gnn_ct}, both modelled as MLPs (ReLU activation,
hidden dimension~$=80$, $3$ layers, output size~$=1$). Other learnables are the two-dimensional
latent vector $\mathbf{a}_i$ associated with each neuron and the
connectivity matrix $\hat{\mathbf W}$. We implemented the GNNs in PyTorch~Geometric
\citep{fey_fast_2019} and used Adam gradient descent with separate
parameter-group learning rates 
$\text{lr}_W\!=\!9{\cdot}10^{-4}$ (connectivity),
$\text{lr}\!=\!1.2{\cdot}10^{-3}$ ($g_\phi$, $f_\theta$),
$\text{lr}_\text{emb}\!=\!1.55{\cdot}10^{-3}$ (embeddings), batch
size $8$, 100 epochs over $64\,000$ training frames. Time points are sampled uniformly at random at each step.

The agentic loop of Appendix~\ref{app:agent} was run independently for
each of the six conditions of Table~\ref{tab:unified_hp} ($\sim$100
iterations of 4 parallel slots per condition, single-parameter
mutations, 4-seed robustness validation of every candidate winner).
The exploration converged on a consistent picture of the role played
by each term of Eq.~\ref{eq:loss_full}. The $\ell_1$ prior on $\hat{\mathbf W}$ ($\gamma_0$) is the only important regulariser: setting $\gamma_0\!=\!0$ leaves rollouts
nearly unchanged ($r\!=\!0.999$) but collapses $R^2_{\hat W}$ from
$0.97$ to $0.39$, a direct manifestation of the degeneracy of
Appendix~\ref{app:degeneracy}. The $\ell_2$ counterpart $\gamma_1$ is
provides no measurable benefit. Among the $g_\phi$ priors, $\mu_1$
prevents the trivial collapse $g_\phi\!\equiv\!0$ but is otherwise
insensitive in $[375,1500]$, $\mu_2$ obeys ``lower is better'' at
noise$=0.05$, and the weight-$\ell_1$ $\lambda_1$ becomes the
dominant lever only in the noise-free condition ($\lambda_1\!=\!0.14$,
$+0.054$ on $R^2_{\hat W}$). The two $f_\theta$ priors are
asymmetric: $\lambda_0$ ($\ell_1$) is harmful and is driven to $0$, whereas $\lambda_2$ ($\ell_2$) acts as the magnitude anchor of $\hat{\mathbf W}$ and must remain at
$\sim\!10^{-3}$.

A clean cross-condition trend emerged: the heavier the measurement
noise, the lighter the explicit regularisation. The $\sigma\!=\!0.05$
winner sits between the two extremes, is the closest single point to
the per-coefficient medians of Table~\ref{tab:unified_hp}, and is
the only one validated under 12-seed robustness with zero
catastrophic failures; we therefore retain it unchanged for every
GNN result reported in the paper, including the FlyWire and
degraded-connectome variants.

\subsection{Joint stimulus recovery with a SIREN.}
To test whether the visual input $I_i(t)$ entering
Eq.~\ref{eq:known_ode} can itself be recovered from activity alone,
we extend the GNN with an implicit neural representation of the
stimulus field $\hat I^*(t,x,y)$, modelled as a coordinate-based
MLP with sine activations \citep[SIREN,][input size~$=3$,
hidden dimension~$=2048$, $4$ hidden layers, output size~$=1$,
$\omega_0\!=\!4096$, outermost-linear head]{sitzmann_implicit_2020}.
The SIREN is queried at the $1\,736$ photoreceptor positions
$(x_i,y_i)$ at every time step and substitutes for $I_i(t)$ in the
GNN forward pass. The GNN and the SIREN are jointly optimised on the
same prediction loss but with strongly asymmetric learning rates
and a two-phase ``alternate-training'' schedule: epoch~$0$ uses the
full GNN learning rates above with
$\text{lr}_\text{SIREN}\!=\!10^{-8}$; epochs~$1$–$2$ scale the GNN
learning rates by $0.05$ while $\text{lr}_\text{SIREN}$ is held
constant, letting the SIREN catch up on a stable graph backbone.
