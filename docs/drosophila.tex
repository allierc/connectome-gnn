\begin{abstract}
Theoretical work on emergent structure in recurrent neural networks
almost never has a ground-truth comparison: discovered modules are
evaluated against themselves. The \emph{Drosophila} central complex
(CX) is the rare exception --- a complete connectome
\citep{hulse2021} pairs with a known biological function
(head-direction integration) and a published RNN model
\citep{vafidis2022}. We use this pairing to ask what optimisation of the path-integration
task identifies when the connectome topology is given. Four constrained
models are trained on the same input--output map:
a Known-ODE RNN, a frozen-connectome control, a fully connected RNN,
and a message-passing GNN with learned neuronal signalling functions.
The frozen-connectome control fails, whereas the three trainable
models recover near-perfect heading accuracy. Among them, the
Known-ODE RNN is the biologically aligned recovery: it preserves the
canonical CX allocation of head-direction information to EPG / EPGt
and $\Delta 7$, with ER6 remaining tonic. The GNN reaches comparable
behavioural performance but implements an alternative circuit,
redistributing head-direction information across cell types and
learning drift/message functions. Thus optimising the path-integration task recovers the
ring-attractor computation, but not a unique circuit implementation. Resolving these degeneracies requires a new methodology: looking for consistency between connectome, neural activity and behavioral task. 
\end{abstract}

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\textbf{Recurrent neural network.}\label{method:RNN}
The RNN model is a sign-locked discrete-time recurrent network whose connectivity is constrained to the \emph{Drosophila} hemibrain central-complex connectome. For each neuron $i\in\{1,\dots,N\}$ the subthreshold activity $\hat{h}_i(t)$ evolves according to
\begin{equation}
\tau\,\frac{d\hat{h}_i(t)}{dt} = -\hat{h}_i(t)
  + \sum_j \hat{W}^{\mathrm{rec}}_{ij}\,\sigma\!\big(\hat{h}_j(t)\big)
  + \sum_l \hat{W}^{\mathrm{in}}_{il}\,u_l(t)
  + \hat{b}_i,
\label{eq:cx_dynamics}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\hat{y}(t) = \big(\cos\hat{\theta}(t),\,\sin\hat{\theta}(t)\big) = \hat{W}^{\mathrm{out}}\sigma\big(\hat{h}(t)\big) + \hat{b}^{\mathrm{out}},
\label{eq:cx_readout}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid, $\tau = 0.1\,\mathrm{s}$, $\Delta t = 0.01\,\mathrm{s}$, and the input $u(t)\in\mathbb{R}^3 = (\omega(t),\,\cos\theta_0\,\delta_{t,0},\,\sin\theta_0\,\delta_{t,0})$ encodes angular velocity and a one-frame initial-heading impulse. The recurrent matrix is sign-locked to the connectome template $W^{\mathrm{con}}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times N}$: the trainable per-edge parameter is the magnitude $\hat{S}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times N}$, and
\begin{equation}
\hat{W}^{\mathrm{rec}}_{ij} = |\hat{S}_{ij}|\,\mathrm{sign}(W^{\mathrm{con}}_{ij})\,(1-\delta_{ij}).
\label{eq:cx_w_rec}
\end{equation}
The angular-velocity input is anatomically restricted to the PEN subpopulations through a strict four-scalar gate on the angular-velocity column of $\hat{W}^{\mathrm{in}}$,
\begin{equation}
\hat{W}^{\mathrm{in}}_{i,\omega} = v_s \quad \text{if } i \in s,\ \ s \in \{\mathrm{PENa}_L,\mathrm{PENa}_R,\mathrm{PENb}_L,\mathrm{PENb}_R\},
\label{eq:cx_pen_gate}
\end{equation}
with four learnable scalars $\{v_{\mathrm{PENa}_L},\,v_{\mathrm{PENa}_R},\,v_{\mathrm{PENb}_L},\,v_{\mathrm{PENb}_R}\}$ initialised antisymmetrically across hemispheres. The remaining two columns of $\hat{W}^{\mathrm{in}}$ --- carrying the initial-heading impulse $(\cos\theta_0,\sin\theta_0)\,\delta_{t,0}$ --- remain free for all neurons. The learnable parameters are
\begin{equation*}
\big\{\hat{S},\;
v_{\mathrm{PENa}_L},\,v_{\mathrm{PENa}_R},\,v_{\mathrm{PENb}_L},\,v_{\mathrm{PENb}_R},\;
\hat{W}^{\mathrm{in}}_{:,\theta_0},\,\hat{b},\,\hat{W}^{\mathrm{out}},\,\hat{b}^{\mathrm{out}}\big\};
\end{equation*}
the connectome template $W^{\mathrm{con}}$, PEN subpopulation membership, neuron-type assignments, $\tau$ and $\Delta t$ are given. Training optimises a per-frame MSE on the heading readout with two connectome-prior regularisers and an $\ell_1$ sparsity prior on the recurrent magnitudes,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}
= \tfrac{1}{BT}\sum_{b,t} \|\hat{y}_{b,t} - y_{b,t}\|_2^2
+ \lambda_{\mathrm{cos}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cos}}
+ \lambda_{\mathrm{nf}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nf}}
+ \lambda_{1}\,\|\hat{S}\|_1,
\label{eq:cx_loss}
\end{equation}
with block losses computed over type-pair masks $\mathcal{B}_p\subset\{1,\dots,N\}^2$ (one block per ordered pre/post neuron-type pair $p\in\mathcal{P}$),
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cos}} &= \tfrac{1}{|\mathcal{P}|}\sum_{p\in\mathcal{P}}\!\left[1 - \frac{\langle \hat{W}^{\mathrm{rec}}_{\mathcal{B}_p},\,W^{\mathrm{con}}_{\mathcal{B}_p}\rangle}{\|\hat{W}^{\mathrm{rec}}_{\mathcal{B}_p}\|\,\|W^{\mathrm{con}}_{\mathcal{B}_p}\|}\right],
\label{eq:cx_cos}\\
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nf}} &= \tfrac{1}{|\mathcal{P}|}\sum_{p\in\mathcal{P}} \relu\!\big(\kappa - \overline{|\hat{W}^{\mathrm{rec}}_{\mathcal{B}_p}|}\big)^2.
\label{eq:cx_nf}
\end{align}
The cosine-distance term $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cos}}$ anchors each block to the connectome template; the norm-floor term $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nf}}$ prevents any type-pair block from collapsing to zero by penalising the (squared) shortfall of the block mean absolute weight $\overline{|\hat{W}^{\mathrm{rec}}_{\mathcal{B}_p}|}$ below a fixed floor $\kappa$. The threshold $\kappa$ is a hyperparameter (typical value $\kappa = 0.05$) that sets the minimum functional magnitude each type-pair block must retain; because $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cos}}$ is scale-invariant, $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nf}}$ is required to prevent a degenerate solution in which the cosine prior is satisfied by an arbitrarily small recurrent magnitude. A linear-reconstruction analysis of the null space of the trained $\hat{W}^{\mathrm{rec}}$ --- which feeds the per-cell-type and per-unit sparsification tests in the Results --- is given in \cref{app:degeneracy_cx}.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Graph neural network model.}\label{method:GNN}
The GNN model replaces the dense recurrent operator $\sum_j \hat{W}^{\mathrm{rec}}_{ij}\sigma(\hat{h}_j)$ of \cref{eq:cx_dynamics} with a message-passing graph neural network \citep{gilmer_neural_2017}, and --- crucially --- does \emph{not} hard-code the leak or the firing-rate nonlinearity. Whereas the RNN imposes the full ODE of \cref{eq:cx_dynamics}, the GNN imposes only the connectome topology and the input/output channels; the leak $-\hat{h}_i$ and the rate nonlinearity $\sigma$ must be \emph{discovered} inside the learned drift. Nodes correspond to neurons and edges to the non-zero entries of the hemibrain connectome template $W^{\mathrm{con}}$. The GNN learns a latent embedding $\mathbf{a}_i\in\mathbb{R}^2$ per neuron, representing compact cell-type-specific identity, and the subthreshold activity evolves as
\begin{equation}
\tau\frac{d\hat{h}_i(t)}{dt} =
  f_\theta\!\Big(\hat{h}_i(t),\,\mathbf{a}_i,\,
       \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i} \hat{W}_{ij}\,g_\phi\!\big(\hat{h}_j(t),\mathbf{a}_j\big)\Big)
  + \sum_l \hat{W}^{\mathrm{in}}_{il}\,u_l(t),
\label{eq:cx_gnn_dynamics}
\end{equation}
with the same readout (\cref{eq:cx_readout}), PEN-gated encoder (\cref{eq:cx_pen_gate}), and dynamics constants ($\tau,\Delta t$) as the RNN. The neighbour set is fixed by the connectome topology, $\mathcal{N}_i = \{j : W^{\mathrm{con}}_{ij}\neq 0\}$. The node update $f_\theta$ and edge message $g_\phi$ are three-layer perceptrons (width 64, ReLU, linear output). $g_\phi$ maps the presynaptic state $\hat{h}_j$ to a per-edge message conditioned on the latent embedding $\mathbf{a}_j$ and weighted by the per-edge gain $\hat{W}_{ij}$; $f_\theta$ then processes the postsynaptic state $\hat{h}_i$, the embedding $\mathbf{a}_i$, and the aggregated synaptic drive. We constrain $g_\phi$ to be monotonically increasing in $\hat{h}_j$ via the prior $\partial g_\phi/\partial \hat{h} \ge 0$ (see \cref{eq:cx_gnn_loss}); combined with the sign-locked $\hat{W}_{ij}$ (\cref{eq:cx_gnn_w}) this restores Dale's law at the per-edge level without introducing a $\pm$-sign degeneracy. The leak, the firing-rate nonlinearity, and any departure from a linear synapse must emerge from $(f_\theta, g_\phi, \mathbf{a})$ themselves; the monotonicity priors below provide the minimum inductive bias for $f_\theta, g_\phi$ to behave like a neuron (postsynaptic drive increases with net excitatory input). The per-edge weight is sign-locked to the connectome as in the RNN,
\begin{equation}
\hat{W}_{ij} = |W_{ij}|\,\mathrm{sign}(W^{\mathrm{con}}_{ij})\,(1-\delta_{ij}),
\label{eq:cx_gnn_w}
\end{equation}
where $W_{ij}\in\mathbb{R}$ is the trainable per-edge magnitude. The learnable parameters are
\begin{equation*}
\big\{\theta,\,\phi,\,\mathbf{a},\,W,\;
v_{\mathrm{PENa}_L},\,v_{\mathrm{PENa}_R},\,v_{\mathrm{PENb}_L},\,v_{\mathrm{PENb}_R},\;
\hat{W}^{\mathrm{in}}_{:,\theta_0},\,\hat{b},\,\hat{W}^{\mathrm{out}},\,\hat{b}^{\mathrm{out}}\big\};
\end{equation*}
the connectome template $W^{\mathrm{con}}$, PEN subpopulation membership, neuron-type assignments, $\tau$, $\Delta t$, and ring orderings are given.

As with the RNN, heading recovery from cos/sin readouts is fundamentally non-unique: many distinct $(\theta,\phi,\mathbf{a},W)$ configurations produce identical readouts. We constrain the solution space with the same connectome-prior regularisers as the RNN ($\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cos}}$, $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nf}}$ from \cref{eq:cx_cos,eq:cx_nf}, computed on the per-edge gain surface $\hat{W}_{ij}$ with floor threshold $\kappa$), together with sparsity and monotonicity priors on the MLPs:
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L} \;=\;& \tfrac{1}{BT}\sum_{b,t}\|\hat{y}_{b,t} - y_{b,t}\|_2^2
+ \lambda_{\mathrm{cos}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cos}}
+ \lambda_{\mathrm{nf}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nf}} \nonumber\\
& +\;\lambda_0\|\theta\|_1 + \lambda_1\|\phi\|_1 + \lambda_2\|\theta\| + \lambda_3\|\phi\|
   \;+\;\gamma_0\|W\|_1 + \gamma_1\|W\| \nonumber\\
& +\;\mu_0\!\left\|\relu\!\left(+\tfrac{\partial f_\theta(\hat{h},\mathbf{a},m)}{\partial \hat{h}}\right)\right\|
   +\;\mu_1\!\left\|\relu\!\left(-\tfrac{\partial f_\theta(\hat{h},\mathbf{a},m)}{\partial m}\right)\right\| \nonumber\\
& +\;\mu_2\!\left\|\relu\!\left(-\tfrac{\partial g_\phi(\hat{h},\mathbf{a})}{\partial \hat{h}}\right)\right\|
   +\;\mu_3\,\big\|g_\phi(h_\star,\mathbf{a})-h_\star\big\|,
\label{eq:cx_gnn_loss}
\end{align}
where $m = \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i}\hat{W}_{ij}\,g_\phi(\hat{h}_j,\mathbf{a}_j)$ is the aggregated synaptic input. The $\ell_1$ penalties $\lambda_0,\lambda_1$ on the MLP weights bias $f_\theta,g_\phi$ toward sparser, simpler functions; the $\ell_2$ penalties $\lambda_2,\lambda_3$ prevent weight explosion. The leak-monotonicity term $\mu_0$ penalises \emph{positive} slopes of $f_\theta$ in the postsynaptic state $\hat{h}$, encoding the prior that the drift contains a leak ($\partial f_\theta/\partial \hat{h} \le 0$); $\mu_1$ penalises negative slopes of $f_\theta$ in the aggregated input $m$, encoding the prior that postsynaptic drive increases with net excitatory input. Together, $\mu_0$ and $\mu_1$ force the discovered drift to be decreasing in self-voltage and increasing in synaptic input --- the two qualitative signatures of \cref{eq:cx_dynamics}. The term $\mu_2$ enforces the analogous monotonicity prior on $g_\phi$ in the presynaptic voltage. The normalisation term $\mu_3$ anchors $g_\phi$ at a reference subthreshold value $h_\star$, removing the multiplicative degeneracy between $g_\phi$ and $\hat{W}$ in \cref{eq:cx_gnn_dynamics}. Hyperparameters $\lambda_{\{0,1,2,3\}},\,\gamma_{\{0,1\}},\,\mu_{\{0,1,2,3\}}$ and the MLP widths/depths are tuned with the agentic optimisation procedure of \citep{allier_connectome_2026}.

\paragraph{Mutual-information estimators.}\label{method:MI}
We use mutual information to quantify how strongly each unit's
firing rate $r_i(t) = \sigma(\hat h_i(t))$ tracks the heading
$\theta(t)$ (or the angular velocity $\omega(t)$) over a held-out
rollout. For a single neuron we discretise $\theta$ into $n_\theta$
equal-width angular bins and $r$ into $n_r$ equal-width bins, then
compute the plug-in estimator in bits,
\begin{equation}
\hat I(r_i;\theta) = \sum_{b,c} \hat p(b,c)\,\log_2
\frac{\hat p(b,c)}{\hat p(b)\,\hat p(c)},
\label{eq:mi_plugin}
\end{equation}
where $\hat p$ is the empirical joint over the $(\theta\!\!-\!\!\mathrm{bin}, r\!\!-\!\!\mathrm{bin})$
histogram. For a group of $K$ neurons we use the cross-validated
lower bound
\begin{equation}
\hat I_{\mathrm{LB}}(\mathbf r_{1:K};\theta) =
H(\theta) - \tfrac{1}{|\mathcal D_{\mathrm{te}}|}
\sum_{(t,\theta)\in\mathcal D_{\mathrm{te}}}
-\log_2 q_\psi(\theta_b\mid \mathbf r(t)),
\label{eq:mi_joint_lb}
\end{equation}
where $q_\psi$ is an $L_2$-regularised multinomial logistic
regression with input $\mathbf r \in \mathbb R^K$ and output the
$n_\theta$ heading bins, trained on a stratified 5-fold split.
$\hat I_{\mathrm{LB}}$ is the population-MI lower bound used for
the per-cell-type and all-neurons joint MIs in
\cref{fig:hd_mi_summary,tab:hd_mi_summary,fig:omega_mi_summary,tab:omega_mi_summary}.
The same estimators apply to $\omega$ by replacing $\theta$ with
$\omega$ and using uniform binning over the 99\textsuperscript{th}
percentile of $|\omega|$ instead of circular wrapping.

\paragraph{Agentic hyperparameter optimisation.}\label{para:agent_gnn}
The GNN hyperparameter space (embedding dimension, MLP widths and depths, the $\lambda$, $\gamma$, $\mu$ coefficients, learning rates, and the curriculum on the rollout length $T$) is too large for grid search; we tune it with the closed-loop LLM-agent protocol of \citep{allier_connectome_2026}, using the deterministic-sweep rollout correlation $r_{\mathrm{roll},1k}$ (Pearson $r$ on the decoded heading at $\omega = 60^\circ/\mathrm{s}$, $T = 1000$ frames, warm-up $10$) as the primary score. The first architectural promotion off the stabilisation winner was $\dim\mathbf{a}_i : 2 \to 4$, with best-seed $r_{\mathrm{roll},1k} = 0.981$ and $5$-seed mean $0.931 \pm 0.061$ versus a seed-fragile $\dim\mathbf{a}_i = 2$ parent. The same four-dimensional latent recurs in the connectome-recovery agentic explorations of \citep{allier_connectome_2026}: the noise-free condition deploys $\dim\mathbf{a} = 4$, the $\sigma = 0.05$ conditions saturate at $2$--$4$, and $\dim\mathbf{a} \ge 6$ falsifies on connectivity-$R^{2}$. A four-dimensional embedding appears to be the smallest representation that separates the dozen-plus presynaptic cell-type identities $g_\phi$ must encode without over-fitting per-edge gains. The runs of \cref{fig:evolution_pi_gnn} predate this promotion and were trained at $\dim\mathbf{a}_i = 2$.

\medskip
\noindent\emph{Why the agentic loop is methodologically primordial.} In high-dimensional recurrent systems, optimisation failure is not itself strong evidence against a mechanistic hypothesis. The non-convergence of a parameterisation may reflect poor hyperparameter selection, unstable training dynamics, or an unfavourable basin of the optimisation landscape rather than the absence of a valid solution. The agentic optimisation loop is therefore not merely a performance tool but a methodological requirement: without extensive closed-loop exploration of the training regime, it is easy to mistake optimisation failure for biological falsification.

\medskip
\noindent\emph{Two-stage agentic protocol.} We run each parameterisation through \emph{two} agentic loops with distinct objectives. The first loop is goal-driven: it searches the hyperparameter space until $r_{\mathrm{roll},1k}$ saturates at the behavioural ceiling, establishing that the parameterisation admits a converging recipe at all. The second loop is knowledge-driven: starting from the first loop's winner as a known floor, it conducts systematic single-axis sweeps across all hyperparameter families — learning rates, stabilisers, curriculum, regularisation, initialisation, architecture — under a strict causality rule (exactly one parameter changes per slot), interleaved with robustness batches (identical configuration, varied seeds) to separate genuine effects from seed variance. The two-stage split is essential because the first loop produces only the existence proof ("there is a recipe that converges") whereas the second produces the causal map the paper's interpretive claims rely on: which hyperparameters explain variance in the converged metric, which failure modes the parameterisation exhibits when training breaks, how robust the converged recipe is across seeds. Collapsing the two stages into a single optimisation pass yields a winning configuration without the mechanistic understanding such conclusions require.


\section{Results}

We compare four recurrent parameterisations on the same path-integration task, each trained on the OU velocity stream of \cref{eq:cx_dynamics} and evaluated on a constant-$\omega = +60^\circ/\mathrm{s}$ deterministic rollout, a held-out OU trial, and an integration-gain sweep over $|\omega| \le 180^\circ/\mathrm{s}$.
\textbf{(i)~Known-ODE RNN} (\cref{fig:evolution_pi}). Connectome topology and Dale signs are imposed via $\hat{W}^{\mathrm{rec}} = |\hat{S}|\odot\mathrm{sign}(W^{\mathrm{con}})$; the per-edge magnitudes $\hat{S}$, the four PEN velocity scalars, the encoder/decoder and biases train end-to-end. Attains $r = 1.00$, $\mathrm{RMSE} = 2.6^\circ$ on the constant-$\omega$ rollout, integration gain $\approx 1$ across $|\omega| \le 120^\circ/\mathrm{s}$, and accurately tracks the OU trial.
\textbf{(ii)~Frozen-$W_{\mathrm{rec}}$ control} (\cref{fig:evolution_pi_frozen}). Same architecture with $\hat{W}^{\mathrm{rec}} \equiv W^{\mathrm{con}}$ held at the raw hemibrain values; only the encoder, decoder, biases, and PEN scalars train. Fails on every readout ($r = -0.37$, $\mathrm{RMSE} = 105^\circ$, integration gain $\approx 0$): the encoder/decoder cannot memorise the path-integration map when the recurrent operator is held at the connectome's nominal values, confirming that the raw weights do not by themselves instantiate a continuous attractor.
\textbf{(iii)~Fully connected RNN} (\cref{fig:evolution_pi_fc}). The connectome topology mask is dropped; only the column-wise Dale sign of each presynaptic neuron is enforced. Recovers heading accuracy ($r = 1.00$, $\mathrm{RMSE} = 12.4^\circ$) but populates off-block entries that are absent from the hemibrain wiring (\cref{fig:evolution_pi_fc}b).
\textbf{(iv)~Message-passing GNN} (\cref{fig:evolution_pi_gnn}). The dense recurrent operator is replaced by $f_\theta, g_\phi$ MLPs over a per-neuron embedding $\mathbf{a}_i$ (\cref{eq:cx_gnn_dynamics}); the leak and firing-rate non-linearity must emerge from these MLPs. The checkpoint shown is the epoch-5 best model of the soft-curriculum tail-loss training, reaching $r_{\mathrm{roll},1k} \approx 0.99$ on the deterministic constant-$\omega$ rollout and tracking the held-out OU trial to within the same band as the RNN variants ($\mathrm{RMSE} = 17.0^\circ$ on the constant-$\omega$ rollout).

\paragraph{Known-ODE RNN: many circuits, same activity.}
The path-integration task fixes only the input--output map: angular
velocity $\omega(t)$ in, decoded heading $\hat \theta(t)$ out. The
Known-ODE RNN (\cref{method:RNN}, \cref{fig:evolution_pi}) imposes
the full ODE of \cref{eq:cx_dynamics} --- linear leak $-\hat h/\tau$,
sigmoid firing-rate non-linearity, hemibrain connectome topology +
Dale signs on $\hat W^{\mathrm{rec}}$ --- and trains only the
per-edge magnitudes $\hat S$. The trained model reaches $r = 1.00$
on a held-out trial. \Cref{fig:function_dynamics}(a, b) shows the
network sweeps the full $|\hat h| \lesssim 10$ domain of the sigmoid
in both tails: behaviour (the bump traversing the EPG / PEN
glomerulus ring as heading rotates) forces every neuron through the
non-linearity many times, so the operating range is determined by
behaviour rather than by the architecture's "capacity". Yet
behaviour does \emph{not} identify the weights: as
\cref{app:degeneracy_cx} establishes, the per-edge recurrent weights
are under-constrained by the input--output map --- a large
equivalence class of $\hat W^{\mathrm{rec}}$ reproduces the same
voltage trajectories, and per-cell-type partitions of the connectome
absorb sum-zero perturbations of up to $50.2\%$ of the edges without
disrupting the heading readout. Many circuits, one activity.
To probe how HD information is parcellated across cell types, we
estimate the mutual information $I(\sigma(\hat h_i); \theta)$
between each neuron's rate and heading along a $T = 10\,000$ frame
OU rollout ($n_\theta = 64$ angular bins; \cref{method:MI},
\cref{fig:hd_mi_summary}, \cref{tab:hd_mi_summary}). The
whole-population joint MI is $I_{\mathrm{pop}} = 3.43$ bits,
predicting a Gaussian-channel per-step posterior
$\sigma_{\theta\mid\hat h} \approx \sigma_\theta\,2^{-I_{\mathrm{pop}}}
\approx 9.6^\circ$ --- in the same band as the directly measured
circular RMSE ($2.5^\circ$ constant-$\omega$, $25.8^\circ$ OU; the
rollout error compounds integration drift on top of the per-step
decoding floor), confirming the MI estimator tracks the network's
actual heading precision. The per-neuron champion is EPGt
($\bar I = 1.54$ bits) and ER6 carries essentially no HD
($\bar I < 0.01$ bits), so in this solution ER6 acts as a tonic
inhibitor without $\theta$-dependent modulation. Per-cell-type
cloning is summarised by the redundancy factor
$\rho_t = \sum_{i \in t} I_i / I_{\mathrm{joint},t}$
(\cref{tab:hd_mi_summary}); large $\rho$ means same-type cells carry
near-identical $\theta$-tuning, so per-neuron MIs sum sub-additively
into the joint. The EPG block has $\rho \approx 18$
(46 cells $\to$ 16 effective glomeruli, joint saturating against
$H(\theta)$), which also explains why the all-neurons joint MI
($\sim 3.4$ bits) is so much smaller than $\sum_{t,i} I_i$
($\sim 150$ bits naively): the code is heavily cloned and capped near
the angular-entropy ceiling rather than scaling with neuron count.
The remaining three trained parameterisations
(\cref{fig:evolution_pi_frozen},\cref{fig:evolution_pi_fc},\cref{fig:evolution_pi_gnn})
expose three further layers of this degeneracy, addressed in turn
below.

\paragraph{Frozen-$W^{\mathrm{rec}}$ control: the raw connectome alone does not explain it.}
A natural reading of the Known-ODE RNN's success is that the
connectome itself --- not the trained magnitudes --- carries the
path-integration computation. The frozen-$W^{\mathrm{rec}}$ control
(\cref{method:RNN} with $\hat W^{\mathrm{rec}} \equiv W^{\mathrm{con}}$
held at the raw hemibrain values throughout training,
\cref{fig:evolution_pi_frozen}) falsifies that reading. Only the
encoder $\hat W^{\mathrm{in}}$, the decoder $\hat W^{\mathrm{out}}$,
the biases, and the four PEN velocity scalars are trainable; the
recurrent operator is locked. The model fails on every readout
($r = -0.37$, $\mathrm{RMSE} = 105^\circ$, integration gain $\approx
0$), the decoded heading is flat, and no bump forms. The raw
hemibrain weights therefore do not by themselves instantiate a
continuous attractor in this task and training regime: the learned
per-edge magnitudes of the Known-ODE RNN are functionally
load-bearing. This rules out the cleanest "connectome alone explains
the behaviour" narrative and shifts the question to what the
\emph{learned} magnitudes are doing --- which the next two
parameterisations probe.

\paragraph{Fully connected RNN: connectome zeros are not required for the computation.}
Replacing the topology mask with a column-wise Dale sign constraint
($\hat W^{\mathrm{rec}} = |\hat S| \odot
\mathrm{sign}\big(\sum_i W^{\mathrm{con}}_{i,:}\big)$,
\cref{fig:evolution_pi_fc}) allows the recurrent matrix to populate
every off-block entry absent from the hemibrain wiring. Despite
this, the model still reaches $r = 1.00$ and exhibits the same
ring-attractor bump migration as the topology-locked Known-ODE RNN
(\cref{fig:kinographs}). In this task and training regime, the
connectome's pattern of missing edges is therefore not required for
the recovered heading computation: the absent edges behave as free
parameters of the inverse problem.
The per-cell-type HD MI (\cref{fig:hd_mi_summary}c, d) reaches
$I_{\mathrm{pop}} = 3.05$ bits ($\sigma_{\theta\mid\hat h} \approx
12.5^\circ$, again consistent with the rollout RMSE), but the
internal allocation diverges from the Known-ODE solution: the
optimiser drives the 4 EPGt cells into deep negative saturation
($\bar h \approx -8$, $\sigma(\hat h) < 0.05$, $\bar I = 0.01$ bits)
and $\Delta 7$ takes over as the dominant per-neuron encoder
($\bar I = 1.15$ bits).

\paragraph{GNN with learned neuronal dynamics: the implementation is not uniquely identified by the task.}
The GNN (\cref{method:GNN}, \cref{fig:evolution_pi_gnn}) keeps the
connectome topology mask but replaces the hardcoded leak and
sigmoid of the Known-ODE RNN with learned functions
$f_\theta, g_\phi$. Despite this much weaker dynamical prior, the
trained GNN still reaches
$r_{\mathrm{roll},1k} \approx 0.99$ and reproduces the same
ring-attractor behaviour. The recovered dynamics differ
substantially from the reference Known-ODE model. The learned drift
$f_\theta$ (\cref{fig:evolution_pi_gnn}k) is not the linear leak
$-h/\tau$, and the learned signalling function $g_\phi$
(\cref{fig:evolution_pi_gnn}l) does not reproduce the canonical
sigmoid except at a coarse qualitative level enforced by the
monotonicity prior. Yet the resulting network still performs path
integration with near-identical behavioural accuracy. Moreover, the rollout probes only a narrow operating region of the
learned functions (\cref{fig:function_dynamics}g, h): most neurons
remain near a low-activity plateau while only a small
bump-encoding sub-population traverses the strongly nonlinear
regime. Large regions of the learned dynamical surfaces are
therefore effectively unconstrained by the task. The GNN thus exposes a deeper layer of degeneracy than the
per-weight or connectome-zero ambiguities of the previous models.
The path-integration task does not uniquely identify the
neuronal dynamics themselves: multiple drift and signalling
functions can realise the same ring-attractor computation.
The same degeneracy reaches the per-cell-type HD code
(\cref{fig:hd_mi_summary}e, f; $I_{\mathrm{pop}} = 3.35$ bits): the
learned $g_\phi$ permits the network to suppress $\Delta 7$
($\bar I = 0.11$ bits) and route HD through ER6 ($\bar I = 1.18$
bits) instead --- the exact mirror image of the Known-ODE allocation.
This $\Delta 7 \leftrightarrow$ ER6 exchange is not a random
redistribution: $\Delta 7$ and ER6 are the \emph{only} two inhibitory
cell types in the model (\cref{fig:connectome_summary}d, with both
populations carrying sign-flipped, $5\times$-amplified outgoing
weights per the Hulse-2025 preparation; see \cref{method:RNN}), and
their postsynaptic targets nest --- $96\%$ of ER6's targets are also
targeted by $\Delta 7$, both blanketing the EPG / PEN / PEG ring. The
two populations are therefore approximately interchangeable as sources
of ring inhibition, and the GNN exploits this structural symmetry to
satisfy the task by carrying HD on ER6 instead of $\Delta 7$. What
ER6 cannot replace is $\Delta 7$'s recurrent self-inhibition (the
$\Delta 7 \to \Delta 7$ block is by far the largest single signed
drive in $W^{\mathrm{con}}$; \cref{fig:connectome_summary}a), and
consistent with this the silenced $\Delta 7$ population collapses
into 42 redundant flat traces ($\rho \approx 30$;
\cref{tab:hd_mi_summary}). We can directly test this swap only
because $\Delta 7$ and ER6 are the two inhibitory types in the CX
preparation; whether analogous exchanges hold among the excitatory
populations (EPG / EPGt / PEN$_a$ / PEN$_b$ / PEG) is not resolved
by the present analysis, and the same identifiability gap should be
expected there until ruled out.

\paragraph{Identifiable computation, non-identifiable implementation.}
Three of the four parameterisations (Known-ODE RNN, fully connected
RNN, GNN) reach essentially identical heading accuracy on path
integration but realise the computation through qualitatively
different machinery: different per-edge weights
(\cref{app:degeneracy_cx}), different presence or absence of
off-block edges (Known-ODE vs.\ fully connected), and different
per-neuron intrinsic functions (RNN's hardcoded $-h/\tau,\sigma$
vs.\ GNN's learned $f_\theta, g_\phi$). The fourth, frozen-$W^{\mathrm{rec}}$
control fails outright (\cref{fig:evolution_pi_frozen}), ruling out
the simplest reading that the raw connectome alone instantiates the
algorithm. What remains \emph{identifiable} across the three
that converge?
\textbf{(i)} The functional architecture: all three implement a
ring-attractor with a bump that propagates as heading changes,
visible as a diagonal bump migration in \cref{fig:kinographs}.
\textbf{(ii)} The input--output routing: in all three trained
networks, the EPG / PEN / $\Delta 7$ neurons participate in the
bump while ER6 / PEG cells do not --- a consequence of the
$\hat W^{\mathrm{in}}$ row structure they share (stimulus routed
to PEN sub-populations only, initial-heading impulse free for all).
What is \emph{not} identifiable: per-edge $\hat W^{\mathrm{rec}}$
(\cref{app:degeneracy_cx}), the form of the per-neuron drift, the
form of the firing-rate non-linearity, and the anatomical-neuron
$\to$ preferred-phase mapping (different trained networks assign
different anatomical neurons to the same phase $\varphi$ along the
ring). Optimising the path-integration input--output map fixes
the ring-attractor computation; the implementation remains
under-determined at every level we probe.

\paragraph{Biologically aligned vs alternative cell-type allocations.}
The three models reach statistically equivalent heading precision
($I_{\mathrm{pop}} = 3.43 / 3.05 / 3.35$ bits; per-step posterior
$\sigma_{\theta\mid\hat h} \sim 10^\circ$) but parcellate that
information across cell types in mutually inconsistent ways
(\cref{fig:hd_mi_summary}, \cref{tab:hd_mi_summary}). The Known-ODE
RNN is the most biologically aligned: EPG / EPGt and $\Delta 7$
carry the dominant HD information while ER6 remains essentially
tonic, matching the canonical CX picture
\citep{seelig2015,kim2017,green2017} in which EPG neurons encode
heading and $\Delta 7$ provides the recurrent ring-inhibitory
scaffold. The fully connected RNN saturates EPGt and shifts the
per-neuron champion to $\Delta 7$; the GNN goes further and
implements a $\Delta 7 \leftrightarrow$ ER6 swap that nonetheless
preserves the ring-attractor computation. The biological division
of labour is therefore not forced by the path-integration task
alone: the same input--output map admits alternative circuit
implementations that reallocate HD information away from the
canonical $\Delta 7$-centred inhibitory pathway.


\paragraph{Relation to Hulse et~al.\ 2026.}
The close companion to our analysis is the
\emph{hidden-symmetries} framework of \citet{hulse2024}, which
shows how ring-attractor dynamics can be preserved under
heterogeneous connectivity through hidden symmetries embedded in
cloned network architectures. Their study combines analytically
constructed symmetry-preserving networks with unconstrained
optimisation results, arguing that the CX attractor is robust to
substantial microscopic heterogeneity. Our analysis asks a
complementary question: given successful behavioural recovery on
the same path-integration task, which aspects of the recovered
implementation are actually identifiable? Our
per-$(i,\alpha,\mathrm{instance})$ null directions
(\cref{app:degeneracy_cx}) overlap closely with the clone structure
highlighted in their framework, but we further show that the
degeneracy extends beyond connectivity to the intrinsic neuronal
dynamics themselves.

\textbf{(i)~Per-cell-type perturbations are locally null but do not
remain invariant under full collapse.}
Mixing clones across PB glomeruli (the per-$(i,\alpha)$ partition)
breaks the readout at $r = 0.36$. Per-cell-type groupings are not
identified as clones by \citet{hulse2024} --- their analysis works
at the per-glomerulus instance level --- and our empirical test
confirms it: lumping same-type neurons together is too coarse to
saturate the null space.
\textbf{(ii)~Per-unit clones more closely track the invariant
subspace.} Our
per-$(i,\alpha,\mathrm{instance})$ partition (\cref{app:degeneracy_cx})
groups the same-type, same-PB-glomerulus neurons that Hulse
et~al.\ identify as clones, and sum-preserving collapses inside
each group leave the rollout at $r = 0.83$ even after zeroing
$50.2\%$ of the edges --- the structural symmetry is operationally
real. The natural unit of redundancy is the glomerulus, not the
cell type, exactly as the symmetry framework predicts.
\textbf{(iii)~The connectome's wiring is necessary
but not sufficient.} The frozen-$W^{\mathrm{rec}}$ control
(\cref{fig:evolution_pi_frozen}) fails to integrate, so the
symmetry classes of the raw connectome \emph{cannot} on their own
instantiate the attractor: learned per-edge magnitudes inside those
classes are doing real work. This is the complementary statement
to \citet{hulse2024}: their robustness proof assumes a working
attractor; we show that training, not topology, supplies it.
\textbf{(iv)~The functional form is also non-unique.} The
hidden-symmetries analysis fixes a sigmoidal $\sigma$ and a linear
leak as part of the model; our GNN result
(\cref{fig:evolution_pi_gnn}) shows that behaviourally indistinguishable
dynamics arise from a learned $(f_\theta, g_\phi)$ pair that
\emph{neither} reduces to $-h/\tau$ nor to the canonical sigmoid.
The symmetry classes therefore explain one layer of degeneracy ---
hidden symmetries within a fixed neuronal model --- whereas our GNN
result adds a second layer: the neuronal dynamics themselves are
also non-identifiable. The per-cell-type HD MI analysis
(\cref{fig:hd_mi_summary}, \cref{tab:hd_mi_summary}) sharpens this
point: alternative signalling functions $(f_\theta, g_\phi)$ do
recover the behaviour, but they do so by routing HD information
through cell types that disagree with the biological prior
(EPGt silent in the fully connected RNN, $\Delta 7$ silent in the
GNN, while in calcium imaging
\citep{seelig2015,kim2017,green2017} EPG, EPGt, PEN, PEG, and
$\Delta 7$ are all HD-tuned). Other signalling functions therefore
recover the function but not the biological cell-type assignment.

\section*{Appendix A: Degeneracy of the inverse problem}
\label{app:degeneracy_cx}

Above, we demonstrated that the trained recurrent neural network with imposed connectome topology and Dale signs reproduces the path-integration task at near-perfect accuracy (\cref{fig:evolution_pi}). Here, we ask how degenerate the inverse problem is: how many distinct recurrent weight matrices $\hat{W}^{\mathrm{rec}}$ reproduce the same voltage trajectories?

We fix the values of the bias $\hat{b}$, the time constant $\tau$, and the input weights $\hat{W}^{\mathrm{in}}$ at their trained values, and rearrange \cref{eq:cx_dynamics} to isolate the incoming synaptic weights:
\begin{equation}
\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i} \hat{W}^{\mathrm{rec}}_{ij}\,\sigma\!\big(\hat{h}_j(t)\big)
\;=\; \tau\,\frac{d\hat{h}_i(t)}{dt} + \hat{h}_i(t) - \hat{b}_i - I_i(t),
\end{equation}
where $I_i(t) = \sum_l \hat{W}^{\mathrm{in}}_{il}\,u_l(t)$ is the per-neuron projected input from \cref{eq:cx_pen_gate}. Defining the presynaptic gated activity $h_j(t) = \sigma(\hat{h}_j(t))$ and the residual $b_i(t) = \tau\,d\hat{h}_i(t)/dt + \hat{h}_i(t) - \hat{b}_i - I_i(t)$, and stacking the $T$ time-point constraints into matrix form, yields an independent linear system for each postsynaptic neuron $i$:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{H}_i\,\mathbf{w}_i = \mathbf{b}_i,
\label{eq:cx_linear_system}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{H}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_i}$ is the presynaptic activity matrix, $d_i$ is the in-degree of neuron $i$, $\mathbf{w}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d_i}$ the incoming recurrent weight vector, and $\mathbf{b}_i \in \mathbb{R}^T$ the stacked residuals. This holds for each postsynaptic neuron $i = 1, \ldots, N$, where $N = 156$ and $d_i$ ranges from $1$ to $98$. The null space $\ker(\mathbf{H}_i) = \{\boldsymbol{\delta} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_i} : \mathbf{H}_i\boldsymbol{\delta} = \mathbf{0}\}$ characterises the degeneracy: any $\mathbf{w}_i + \boldsymbol{\delta}$ with $\boldsymbol{\delta} \in \ker(\mathbf{H}_i)$ satisfies \cref{eq:cx_linear_system} equally and produces identical dynamics. In the following, we characterise the dynamical equivalence class of $\hat{W}^{\mathrm{rec}}$, the set of all recurrent weight matrices that reproduce the trained model's voltage trajectories.

\paragraph{Two SVD-based bounds on the null space (inconclusive).}
We solve \cref{eq:cx_linear_system} per neuron via column-equilibrated normal equations and the eigendecomposition of $\mathbf{H}_i^{\top}\mathbf{H}_i$, and quantify $\dim\ker(\mathbf{H})$ in two complementary ways. \emph{Strict per-neuron SVD:} the eigendecomposition flags directions as \emph{null} (relative eigenvalue $\le T \cdot 10^{-22}$) or \emph{sloppy} ($\le T \cdot 10^{-12}$); summed over $i$ this counts only $6.4\%$ of edges ($439$ null + $214$ sloppy). \emph{Global-SVD coarse bound:} by rank--nullity, $d_i = \mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{H}_i) + \dim\ker(\mathbf{H}_i)$. Each per-neuron activity matrix $\mathbf{H}_i$ is a column-submatrix of the global activity matrix $\mathbf{H} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times N}$ stacking sigmoid gated activities $\sigma(\hat h_j(t))$ of all $156$ neurons over $T = 8{,}000$ subsampled frames; therefore $\mathrm{rank}(\mathbf{H}_i) \le r$, where $r$ is the effective rank of $\mathbf{H}$ at a chosen variance threshold, and
\begin{equation}
\dim\ker(\mathbf{H}) \;\approx\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} \max\!\big(0,\; d_i - r\big).
\label{eq:cx_null_globalsvd}
\end{equation}
At $99\%$ variance, $r = 9$ --- only nine modes carry $99\%$ of the population variance, reflecting the low-dimensional ring-attractor manifold --- and the bound spans $8{,}859$ edges ($86.3\%$). Tightening the threshold to $99.9\%$ raises $r$ to $34$ and shrinks the bound to $5{,}015$ edges ($48.9\%$); the null space remains large at every threshold. The two estimates disagree by an order of magnitude and neither is conclusive on its own. To pin down which directions are actually invariant for the trained model, we apply controlled perturbations to specific groups of edges and roll out the network. We test two partition schemes in the next two paragraphs.

\paragraph{Per cell type.}
For each postsynaptic neuron $i$ and presynaptic cell type $\alpha$, the $k_{i,\alpha}$ same-type incoming edges form a candidate $(i,\alpha)$ group; pairs with $k_{i,\alpha} \ge 2$ account for $9{,}400$ edges ($91.6\%$). The sum-zero perturbation is the natural test for this partition: when the $k_{i,\alpha}$ same-type presynaptic neurons carry correlated subthreshold activity, the recurrent drive $\sum_m W_{i,j_m}\,\sigma(\hat h_{j_m}(t)) \approx \sigma(\hat h(t))\,\sum_m W_{i,j_m}$ depends only on the group sum, so any perturbation on the same-type weights $W_{i,j_m} \to W_{i,j_m} + \delta_{j_m}$ with $\sum_m \delta_{j_m} = 0$ leaves the dynamics invariant under the lockstep assumption \citep{allier_connectome_2026}. We test this candidate null direction empirically by perturbing each type's groups by a sum-zero vector at amplitude $\lambda \cdot \mathrm{mean}(|W_{\mathrm{group}}|)$ and rolling out for $1{,}000$ frames (\cref{tab:variants_cx}). At $\lambda = 1$, all seven cell types preserve the dynamics ($R^{2}_{\mathbf{W}} \in [0.986, 0.996]$, Pearson $r \in [0.926, 1.000]$): the predicted directions are real \emph{locally} around the trained $\hat{W}^{\mathrm{rec}}$. At $\lambda = 2$ all $r$ remain above $0.90$; at $\lambda = 4$, $R^{2}_{\mathbf{W}}$ drops to $[0.781, 0.964]$ and $\Delta 7$/PEN$_a$ rollouts deteriorate to $r = 0.70$ / $0.79$. Pushing all the way to the extreme by collapsing each $(i,\alpha)$ group of $k_{i,\alpha}$ edges onto a single representative edge $e^{\star} = \arg\max_e |W_e|$ --- setting $W_{e^{\star}} = \sum_{e \in \mathrm{group}} W_e$ (the sum-preserving variant) or $W_{e^{\star}}$ chosen by a one-scalar least-squares fit that minimises the per-group dynamics residual (the calibrated variant), and zeroing the other $k_{i,\alpha} - 1$ edges in the group --- removes $91.6\%$ of the edges. This is the analogue of the operation that preserves dynamics at $r \approx 0.95$ on the visual-system biomodel of \citep{allier_connectome_2026}, but it breaks the CX heading readout: sum-preserving rollout $r = 0.36$, calibrated $r = 0.17$. The per-cell-type null space is therefore approximately null around the trained weights but cannot be saturated.

\paragraph{Per unit.}
The per-$(i,\alpha)$ partition lumps same-type neurons regardless of which PB glomerulus they innervate, but in the CX two EPGs in different glomeruli encode different headings and are not clones. The natural refinement groups same-type neurons by their hemibrain instance, the per-cell annotation that identifies a neuron's anatomical computational unit: for EPG/EPGt/PEG/PEN it is the PB glomerulus (e.g., \emph{EPG(PB08)\_L3} marks an EPG neuron in glomerulus L3); for $\Delta 7$ it is the set of glomeruli the neuron spans (e.g., \emph{Delta7(PB15)\_L1L9R8\_R}); for ER6 it is the hemisphere. Same-type neurons sharing an instance are clones as discussed in \citet{hulse2024}. The $(i,\alpha,\mathrm{instance})$ partition retains $5{,}150$ candidate edges ($50.2\%$ of $10{,}263$). Applying the same collapse defined above to each $(i,\alpha,\mathrm{instance})$ group --- zeroing $50.2\%$ of the edges instead of $91.6\%$ --- yields sum-preserving rollout $r = 0.83$, against $r = 0.36$ for the analogous operation under the per-$(i,\alpha)$ partition. The per-unit null space is therefore approximately saturable: the finer partition is a tight predictor of the truly invariant subspace. In a retinotopic visual system \citep{allier_connectome_2026}, the $(i,\alpha)$ partition saturates because columns of the same type encode the same scene; the CX requires the finer $(i,\alpha,\mathrm{instance})$ partition because heading is encoded per-glomerulus, not per-type.
