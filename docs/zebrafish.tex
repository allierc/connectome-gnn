\author{}
\date{}

\begin{abstract}
We study a vertebrate ring attractor for heading direction at
single-neuron anatomical resolution: the GABAergic \rpi{} population
of the larval-zebrafish anterior hindbrain (aHB), whose axons
arborise in the dorsal interpeduncular nucleus (dIPN).
\citet{petrucco2023} demonstrate a sinusoidal activity bump that
rotates with the fish's swims and is otherwise stable over many
seconds, with reciprocal inhibitory connectivity in dIPN providing
the long-range ring-shaping inhibition. We assemble a
connectome-constrained training pipeline for this dataset:
skeletons, ROI meshes and the (post, pre) adjacency matrix between
the 731 HD-circuit neurons are fetched from the
\emph{neuprint-fish2} server (dataset \emph{fish2}), and the
sign-locked recurrent and message-passing GNN parameterisations of a
swim-integration task are trained under an all-inhibitory Dale
convention for the bump population. This stub document collects the
anatomy and connectome summary; the modelling sections will be
filled in once the zebrafish task generator and training runs are in
place.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

In the larval zebrafish, head-direction information is carried by a
population of $\sim 50$--$100$ GABAergic neurons (the \rpi{} cells)
located in rhombomere~1 of the anterior hindbrain \citep{petrucco2023}.
A sinusoidal bump of activity travels around this network in a
direction opposite to the fish's swims, persists for tens of seconds
in the absence of motor activity, and tracks an estimated heading
angle whose magnitude per swim ($\approx \pi/4$~rad) matches the
behaviourally measured turn. The connectivity scheme that supports
the bump --- short-range excitation and long-range, out-of-phase
inhibition --- is the classical ring-attractor architecture, and
electron-microscopy reconstructions of the \rpi{} axons show
reciprocal inhibitory connectivity in the dIPN consistent with this
picture.

\section{Anatomy and connectome}
\label{sec:anatomy}

\paragraph{Data.}
Skeletons, ROI meshes and synaptic connections are fetched from
\emph{neuprint-fish2.janelia.org}, dataset \emph{fish2}.
\emph{figures/zebrafish/fetch\_zebrafish\_anatomy\_HD.py} writes
SWC skeletons and OBJ meshes to
\emph{zebrafish\_anatomy\_HD/}; \emph{fetch\_zebrafish\_connectivity\_HD.py}
writes \emph{neurons.csv} (731 rows: 443 \emph{IPNd*} + \emph{IPNds*}
ring cells, 200 \emph{RIPN*}, 88 \emph{pt-IPN*}) and
\emph{connections.csv} ($14\,391$ directed edges)
to \emph{zebrafish\_connectome\_HD/}. The loader
\emph{load\_zebrafish\_hd\_connectome()} in
\emph{src/connectome\_gnn/generators/connconstr\_data.py} reads
the two CSV files and returns a dictionary with the canonical keys
\emph{N}, \emph{J\_effective}, \emph{neuron\_types},
\emph{type\_names}, \emph{n\_ring}, \emph{ring\_ix},
\emph{afferent\_subpop\_ix}. The \emph{ZebrafishHdTaskRNN} class
implements the sign-locked dynamics, the 4-scalar afferent gate, the
HD-ring-only readout, and the regulariser machinery described in
\cref{sec:methods}.

The loader fields are:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{n\_ring} = 443: number of dorsal-IPN HD cells
       (\emph{IPNd*} + \emph{IPNds*}), \ie{} the \rpi{} ring.
\item \emph{ring\_ix}: ring ordering of the HD cells, computed from
       the mediolateral soma coordinate \emph{somaLocationX}
       (Petrucco's Fig.~1g right panel shows the \rpi{} cells lie on
       an anatomical ring along this axis); cells are binned into
       16 ring positions for the readout decoder.
\item \emph{afferent\_subpop\_ix} = \emph{\{RIPN\_L, RIPN\_R,
       ptIPN\_L, ptIPN\_R\}} split by side from the
       \emph{instance} suffix; sizes $\{102, 101, 41, 44\}$.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Dale convention.}
The \rpi{} cells (\emph{IPNd*} and \emph{IPNds*}) are GABAergic
\citep{petrucco2023}; their outgoing weights are sign-flipped and
amplified by 5$\times$. \emph{RIPN*} and \emph{pt-IPN*} have no Dale
annotation on the \emph{fish2} server and are kept positive
(excitatory). The resulting signed matrix is rescaled so
$\max(\mathrm{Re}(\lambda)) = 0.9$, a spectral-radius convention
that keeps the linearised recurrent operator inside the stability
margin used throughout the training runs.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\textbf{Recurrent neural network.}\label{method:RNN}
The RNN model is a sign-locked discrete-time recurrent network whose
connectivity is constrained to the larval-zebrafish HD-circuit
connectome (\cref{sec:anatomy}). For each neuron $i \in \{1,\dots,N\}$
with $N = 731$ the subthreshold activity $\hat h_i(t)$ evolves
according to
\begin{equation}
\tau\,\frac{d\hat h_i(t)}{dt}
  = -\hat h_i(t)
  + \sum_j \hat W^{\mathrm{rec}}_{ij}\,\sigma\!\big(\hat h_j(t)\big)
  + \sum_l \hat W^{\mathrm{in}}_{il}\,u_l(t)
  + \hat b_i,
\label{eq:zhd_dynamics}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\hat y(t) = \big(\cos\hat\theta(t),\,\sin\hat\theta(t)\big)
  = \hat W^{\mathrm{out}}\sigma\big(\hat h(t)\big) + \hat b^{\mathrm{out}},
\label{eq:zhd_readout}
\end{equation}
Here $\hat y(t) \in \R^2$ is the network's inference of the
head-direction signal, encoded as the
$(\cos\hat\theta,\,\sin\hat\theta)$ pair so that the supervisory loss
matches the ground-truth heading on the unit circle and avoids the
$\pm 2\pi$ wraparound; the decoded heading is recovered as
$\hat\theta(t) = \operatorname{atan2}\!\big(\hat y_2(t),\,\hat y_1(t)\big)$.
The activation $\sigma$ is the sigmoid, the membrane time constant is
$\tau = 0.1\,\mathrm{s}$, the integration step is
$\Delta t = 0.01\,\mathrm{s}$, and the input
$u(t) \in \R^3 = \big(s(t),\,\cos\theta_0\,\delta_{t,0},\,\sin\theta_0\,\delta_{t,0}\big)$
carries a swim-triggered angular impulse $s(t)$ and a one-frame
initial-heading cue. In \petrucco{}~Fig.~3c the network phase
advances by $\approx \pi/4$~rad per swim ($Q_1, Q_3 = 0.49,
1.28$~rad, $n = 31$~fish), so $s(t)$ is a sparse stream of signed
impulses of typical magnitude $\pi/4$ at swim onsets. Outside swim
events $s(t) = 0$, mirroring the persistent silence of the \rpi{}
bump in non-swimming fish (\petrucco{}~Fig.~3a, c). The readout from
\cref{eq:zhd_readout} reads from the HD-ring-only block of $N$
neurons unless stated otherwise.

The recurrent matrix is sign-locked to the connectome template
$W^{\mathrm{con}} \in \R^{N \times N}$: the trainable per-edge
parameter is the magnitude $\hat S \in \R^{N \times N}$, and
\begin{equation}
\hat W^{\mathrm{rec}}_{ij}
  = |\hat S_{ij}|\,\mathrm{sign}(W^{\mathrm{con}}_{ij})\,(1-\delta_{ij}).
\label{eq:zhd_w_rec}
\end{equation}
$W^{\mathrm{con}}$ comes out of
\emph{load\_zebrafish\_hd\_connectome} with the Dale convention of
\cref{sec:anatomy}: the outgoing columns of the GABAergic HD-ring
cells (\emph{IPNd*}, \emph{IPNds*}) are negated and amplified by
$5\times$, after which the matrix is rescaled to spectral radius
$0.9$. \emph{All} sign-flipped columns belong to the bump population
itself; there is no separate inhibitory cell type providing the
long-range ring-shaping inhibition.

The swim-triggered drive is anatomically restricted to the
afferent populations through a four-scalar gate on the first column
of $\hat W^{\mathrm{in}}$:
\begin{equation}
\hat W^{\mathrm{in}}_{i,s}
  = v_q \quad \text{if } i \in q,\ \ q \in
   \{\mathrm{RIPN}_L,\mathrm{RIPN}_R,\mathrm{ptIPN}_L,\mathrm{ptIPN}_R\},
\label{eq:zhd_swim_gate}
\end{equation}
with four learnable scalars
$\{v_{\mathrm{RIPN}_L},\,v_{\mathrm{RIPN}_R},\,
  v_{\mathrm{ptIPN}_L},\,v_{\mathrm{ptIPN}_R}\}$
initialised antisymmetrically across hemispheres so that left swims
and right swims drive the bump in opposite directions. The
subpopulation membership comes from \emph{afferent\_subpop\_ix}
returned by the loader (sizes $102 / 101 / 41 / 44$). The two
remaining input columns --- carrying the initial-heading impulse
$(\cos\theta_0,\sin\theta_0)\,\delta_{t,0}$ --- stay free for all
neurons. \emph{RIPN*} is functionally implicated as the
swim-triggered driver of the bump in \petrucco{}; \emph{pt-IPN*} is
included in the gate so the optimiser can additionally route motor
efference through the pretectal afferents if that improves heading
recovery.

The learnable parameters are
\begin{equation*}
\big\{\hat S,\;
v_{\mathrm{RIPN}_L},\,v_{\mathrm{RIPN}_R},\,
v_{\mathrm{ptIPN}_L},\,v_{\mathrm{ptIPN}_R},\;
\hat W^{\mathrm{in}}_{:,\theta_0},\,\hat b,\,
\hat W^{\mathrm{out}},\,\hat b^{\mathrm{out}}\big\};
\end{equation*}
the connectome template $W^{\mathrm{con}}$, the
RIPN / pt-IPN subpopulation membership, the neuron-type assignments,
$\tau$ and $\Delta t$ are given. Training optimises a per-frame MSE
on the heading readout with two connectome-prior regularisers and
an $\ell_1$ sparsity prior on the recurrent magnitudes,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}
= \tfrac{1}{BT}\sum_{b,t} \|\hat y_{b,t} - y_{b,t}\|_2^2
+ \lambda_{\mathrm{cos}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cos}}
+ \lambda_{\mathrm{nf}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nf}}
+ \lambda_{1}\,\|\hat S\|_1,
\label{eq:zhd_loss}
\end{equation}
with block losses computed over type-pair masks
$\mathcal{B}_p \subset \{1,\dots,N\}^2$ (one block per ordered
pre/post neuron-type pair $p \in \mathcal{P}$),
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cos}}
  &= \tfrac{1}{|\mathcal{P}|}\sum_{p \in \mathcal{P}}
     \!\left[1 - \frac{\langle \hat W^{\mathrm{rec}}_{\mathcal{B}_p},\,
         W^{\mathrm{con}}_{\mathcal{B}_p}\rangle}
       {\|\hat W^{\mathrm{rec}}_{\mathcal{B}_p}\|\,
        \|W^{\mathrm{con}}_{\mathcal{B}_p}\|}\right],
\label{eq:zhd_cos}\\
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nf}}
  &= \tfrac{1}{|\mathcal{P}|}\sum_{p \in \mathcal{P}}
     \relu\!\big(\kappa -
       \overline{|\hat W^{\mathrm{rec}}_{\mathcal{B}_p}|}\big)^2.
\label{eq:zhd_nf}
\end{align}
The cosine-distance term $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cos}}$ anchors each
block to the connectome template; the norm-floor term
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nf}}$ prevents any type-pair block from
collapsing to zero by penalising the squared shortfall of the block
mean absolute weight $\overline{|\hat W^{\mathrm{rec}}_{\mathcal{B}_p}|}$
below a fixed floor $\kappa$ (typical value $\kappa = 0.05$). Because
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cos}}$ is scale-invariant,
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nf}}$ is required to prevent a degenerate
solution in which the cosine prior is satisfied by an arbitrarily
small recurrent magnitude. The type-pair vocabulary $\mathcal{P}$ is
the cartesian product of the 31 fish2 cell types identified in
\cref{sec:anatomy}, restricted to pairs with at least one non-zero
entry in $W^{\mathrm{con}}$. A linear-reconstruction analysis of the
null space of the trained $\hat W^{\mathrm{rec}}$ --- a per-cell-type
and per-unit sparsification test --- is deferred to future work.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Graph neural network model.}\label{method:GNN}
The GNN model replaces the dense recurrent operator
$\sum_j \hat W^{\mathrm{rec}}_{ij}\,\sigma(\hat h_j)$ of
\cref{eq:zhd_dynamics} with a message-passing graph neural network
(Gilmer \emph{et al.}\ 2017), and --- crucially --- does \emph{not}
hard-code either the leak, the firing-rate nonlinearity, or the
synaptic activation function: the linear leak $-\hat h_i/\tau$, the
sigmoid $\sigma$, and the per-edge message that combines a
presynaptic activity with its postsynaptic effect must all be
discovered from the input--output map inside the learned drift
$f_\theta$ and the learned signalling function $g_\phi$. Beyond
removing this functional bias, the GNN also admits an explicit form
of neuronal heterogeneity through a per-neuron learnable embedding
$\mathbf a_i \in \R^d$ that enters both $f_\theta$ and $g_\phi$:
same-type neurons share their architectural role but can specialise
via their embedding, so a single pair of MLPs covers the $31$ fish2
cell types of \cref{sec:anatomy} and all $N = 731$ instance-level
distinctions without any further per-type parameter tying; we use
$d = 4$ as the default. Nodes correspond to neurons and edges to
the non-zero entries of the zebrafish HD connectome template
$W^{\mathrm{con}}$, and the subthreshold activity evolves as
\begin{equation}
\tau\,\frac{d\hat h_i(t)}{dt}
  = f_\theta\!\Big(\hat h_i(t),\,\mathbf a_i,\,
       \sum_{j \in \mathcal N_i}
       \hat W_{ij}\,g_\phi\!\big(\hat h_j(t),\mathbf a_j\big)\Big)
  + \sum_l \hat W^{\mathrm{in}}_{il}\,u_l(t),
\label{eq:zhd_gnn_dynamics}
\end{equation}
with the same readout (\cref{eq:zhd_readout}), swim-gated encoder
(\cref{eq:zhd_swim_gate}), and dynamics constants ($\tau,\Delta t$)
as the RNN. The neighbour set is fixed by the connectome topology,
$\mathcal N_i = \{j : W^{\mathrm{con}}_{ij} \neq 0\}$. The node
update $f_\theta$ and edge message $g_\phi$ are three-layer
perceptrons (width 64, ReLU, linear output). $g_\phi$ maps the
presynaptic state $\hat h_j$ to a per-edge message conditioned on
$\mathbf a_j$ and weighted by the per-edge gain $\hat W_{ij}$;
$f_\theta$ then processes the postsynaptic state $\hat h_i$, the
embedding $\mathbf a_i$, and the aggregated synaptic drive. We
constrain $g_\phi$ to be monotonically increasing in $\hat h_j$ via
the prior $\partial g_\phi / \partial \hat h \ge 0$ (see
\cref{eq:zhd_gnn_loss}); combined with the sign-locked $\hat W_{ij}$
(\cref{eq:zhd_gnn_w}) this restores Dale's law at the per-edge level
without introducing a $\pm$-sign degeneracy. The leak, the
firing-rate non-linearity, and any departure from a linear synapse
must emerge from $(f_\theta, g_\phi, \mathbf a)$ themselves; the
monotonicity priors below provide the minimum inductive bias for
$f_\theta, g_\phi$ to behave like a neuron (postsynaptic drive
increases with net excitatory input). The per-edge weight is
sign-locked to the connectome as in the RNN,
\begin{equation}
\hat W_{ij}
  = |W_{ij}|\,\mathrm{sign}(W^{\mathrm{con}}_{ij})\,(1-\delta_{ij}),
\label{eq:zhd_gnn_w}
\end{equation}
where $W_{ij} \in \R$ is the trainable per-edge magnitude.
In the zebrafish circuit $\mathrm{sign}(W^{\mathrm{con}}_{ij})$ is
negative for every edge whose presynaptic neuron is in the bump ring
(\emph{IPNd*} or \emph{IPNds*}) and positive for every edge from
\emph{RIPN*} or \emph{pt-IPN*}; the GNN therefore inherits the
inhibitory dIPN ring directly from the sign pattern.

The learnable parameters are
\begin{equation*}
\big\{\theta,\,\phi,\,\mathbf a,\,W,\;
v_{\mathrm{RIPN}_L},\,v_{\mathrm{RIPN}_R},\,
v_{\mathrm{ptIPN}_L},\,v_{\mathrm{ptIPN}_R},\;
\hat W^{\mathrm{in}}_{:,\theta_0},\,\hat b,\,
\hat W^{\mathrm{out}},\,\hat b^{\mathrm{out}}\big\};
\end{equation*}
the connectome template $W^{\mathrm{con}}$, the
RIPN / pt-IPN subpopulation membership, the neuron-type assignments,
$\tau$, $\Delta t$, and the mediolateral ring ordering used by the
decoder are given.

As with the RNN, heading recovery from cos/sin readouts is
fundamentally non-unique: many distinct $(\theta,\phi,\mathbf a,W)$
configurations produce identical readouts. We constrain the solution
space with the same connectome-prior regularisers as the RNN
($\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cos}}$, $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nf}}$ from
\cref{eq:zhd_cos,eq:zhd_nf}, computed on the per-edge gain surface
$\hat W_{ij}$ with floor threshold $\kappa$), together with sparsity
and monotonicity priors on the MLPs:
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L} \;=\;&
  \tfrac{1}{BT}\sum_{b,t}\|\hat y_{b,t} - y_{b,t}\|_2^2
+ \lambda_{\mathrm{cos}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cos}}
+ \lambda_{\mathrm{nf}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{nf}} \nonumber\\
& +\;\lambda_0\|\theta\|_1 + \lambda_1\|\phi\|_1
   + \lambda_2\|\theta\| + \lambda_3\|\phi\|
   \;+\;\gamma_0\|W\|_1 + \gamma_1\|W\| \nonumber\\
& +\;\mu_0\!\left\|
       \relu\!\left(+\tfrac{\partial f_\theta(\hat h,\mathbf a,m)}
                          {\partial \hat h}\right)\right\|
   +\;\mu_1\!\left\|
       \relu\!\left(-\tfrac{\partial f_\theta(\hat h,\mathbf a,m)}
                          {\partial m}\right)\right\| \nonumber\\
& +\;\mu_2\!\left\|
       \relu\!\left(-\tfrac{\partial g_\phi(\hat h,\mathbf a)}
                          {\partial \hat h}\right)\right\|
   +\;\mu_3\,\big\|g_\phi(h_\star,\mathbf a)-h_\star\big\|,
\label{eq:zhd_gnn_loss}
\end{align}
where $m = \sum_{j \in \mathcal N_i}\hat W_{ij}\,
g_\phi(\hat h_j,\mathbf a_j)$ is the aggregated synaptic input. The
$\ell_1$ penalties $\lambda_0,\lambda_1$ on the MLP weights bias
$f_\theta, g_\phi$ toward sparser, simpler functions; the $\ell_2$
penalties $\lambda_2,\lambda_3$ prevent weight explosion. The
leak-monotonicity term $\mu_0$ penalises \emph{positive} slopes of
$f_\theta$ in the postsynaptic state $\hat h$, encoding the prior
that the drift contains a leak
($\partial f_\theta / \partial \hat h \le 0$); $\mu_1$ penalises
negative slopes of $f_\theta$ in the aggregated input $m$, encoding
the prior that postsynaptic drive increases with net excitatory
input. Together, $\mu_0$ and $\mu_1$ force the discovered drift to be
decreasing in self-voltage and increasing in synaptic input --- the
two qualitative signatures of \cref{eq:zhd_dynamics}. The term $\mu_2$
enforces the analogous monotonicity prior on $g_\phi$ in the
presynaptic voltage. The normalisation term $\mu_3$ anchors $g_\phi$
at a reference subthreshold value $h_\star$, removing the
multiplicative degeneracy between $g_\phi$ and $\hat W$ in
\cref{eq:zhd_gnn_dynamics}. Hyperparameters
$\lambda_{\{0,1,2,3\}},\,\gamma_{\{0,1\}},\,\mu_{\{0,1,2,3\}}$ and the
MLP widths/depths are tuned with an agentic optimisation procedure,
with $r_{\mathrm{roll},1k}$ on a deterministic swim-impulse rollout
as the primary score.

\paragraph{Mutual-information estimators.}\label{method:MI}
We use mutual information to quantify how strongly each unit's
firing rate $r_i(t) = \sigma(\hat h_i(t))$ tracks the heading
$\theta(t)$ (or, where stated, the angular velocity $\omega(t)$ or
the swim-onset trace $s(t)$) over a held-out rollout. For a single
neuron we wrap $\theta$ to $(-\pi, \pi]$ via
$\theta \leftarrow \arg(e^{i\theta})$, discretise the wrapped angle
into $n_\theta = 32$ equal-width angular bins and $r$ into $n_r = 20$
equal-width bins, then compute the plug-in estimator in bits,
\begin{equation}
\hat I(r_i;\theta) = \sum_{b,c} \hat p(b,c)\,\log_2
\frac{\hat p(b,c)}{\hat p(b)\,\hat p(c)},
\label{eq:zhd_mi_plugin}
\end{equation}
where $\hat p$ is the empirical joint over the
$(\theta\!\!-\!\!\mathrm{bin}, r\!\!-\!\!\mathrm{bin})$ histogram. The
construction matches the drosophila MI estimator
(matching the construction in the companion \emph{Drosophila} CX
paper, Methods, \emph{Mutual-information estimators}) so
that the per-neuron and per-cell-type quantities of
\cref{sec:results_four_classes,sec:results_pref_angle} are
comparable across the two species. For a group of $K$ neurons we
use the cross-validated lower bound
\begin{equation}
\hat I_{\mathrm{LB}}(\mathbf r_{1:K};\theta) =
H(\theta) - \tfrac{1}{|\mathcal D_{\mathrm{te}}|}
\sum_{(t,\theta)\in\mathcal D_{\mathrm{te}}}
-\log_2 q_\psi(\theta_b\mid \mathbf r(t)),
\label{eq:zhd_mi_joint_lb}
\end{equation}
where $q_\psi$ is an $L_2$-regularised multinomial logistic
regression with input $\mathbf r \in \mathbb R^K$ and output the
$n_\theta$ heading bins, trained on a stratified 5-fold split.

\paragraph{Image-domain ROI sampling and $\Delta F/F_0$.}\label{method:dff}
To make the model's population dynamics comparable to two-photon
calcium imaging of the larval-zebrafish dIPN, we sample activity
\emph{from the rendered frames} of the dorsal-view animation, not
from the latent state $\hat h_i(t)$. Each frame is a
$3000 \times 4380$-pixel canvas in which each neuron's skeleton is
drawn with a per-segment alpha proportional to its firing rate; we
place four axis-aligned ROI rectangles $R_k$
($k \in \{\mathrm{dIPN}_L, \mathrm{dIPN}_R, \mathrm{dsIPN}_L,
\mathrm{dsIPN}_R\}$) over the dorsal-IPN neuropil (drawn on frame~0
of \emph{figures/zebrafish/3D\_voltage\_const/frame\_0000.png}) and
integrate the green channel of the canvas RGBA buffer:
\begin{equation}
I_k(t) \;=\; \frac{1}{|R_k|}\sum_{(x,y)\,\in\,R_k}
              \mathrm{img}_t[y, x, \mathrm{green}].
\label{eq:zhd_roi_intens}
\end{equation}
The four traces $I_k(t)$ are then converted to a fractional change
with respect to a post-init baseline, mirroring the Seelig / Kim /
Green calcium-imaging convention:
\begin{align}
F_0[k] &\;=\; \bigl\langle I_k(t)\bigr\rangle_{t \,\ge\,
                t_{\mathrm{init}}} + \varepsilon, \\
\Delta F/F_0\,(k,t) &\;=\;
   \frac{I_k(t) - F_0[k]}{F_0[k]},
\label{eq:zhd_dff}
\end{align}
with $t_{\mathrm{init}} = 0.5$~s (drops the network start-up
transient that would otherwise dominate the baseline) and
$\varepsilon = 10^{-6}$. The per-pair population-vector position
overlaid on the kinographs in red is the activity-weighted centre
of mass across each $L/R$ pair,
$\mathrm{pos}_p(t) = \big(I_{p,L}\,y_{p,L} + I_{p,R}\,y_{p,R}\big)/
                      \big(I_{p,L} + I_{p,R}\big)$,
i.e.\ the same Seelig/Hulse PVA reduced to two phase bins per pair.
Image-domain sampling inherits all the projection biases of the
microscope (depth-of-field, projection, downsample) and so the
kinographs are directly comparable to \petrucco{}~Fig.~2 without
having to invert the calcium kernel.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Functional partitioning of the HD circuit}
\label{sec:results_four_classes}

We use the trained RNN (configuration
\cref{eq:zhd_dynamics,eq:zhd_readout,eq:zhd_swim_gate,eq:zhd_loss}) to
ask, for each of the $N = 731$ neurons in the model, whether it
\emph{represents} the heading-direction code, \emph{drives or updates}
the bump, or does neither. The analysis combines three per-neuron
scores computed on a $120$~s closed-loop swim rollout (seed~$=0$,
$\Delta t = 0.01$~s, burn-in $5$~s):
\begin{enumerate}
  \item the heading mutual information
        $\mi_i = I(\hat h_i ; \theta)$ in bits, estimated with the
        plug-in histogram of \cref{eq:zhd_mi_plugin}
        (\cref{method:MI}) at $n_\theta = 32$ heading bins and
        $n_r = 20$ activity bins,
  \item the readout magnitude
        $\omag_i = \|\hat W^{\mathrm{out}}_{:,i}\|_2$, identically zero
        for $i \ge 443$ since the decoder of \cref{eq:zhd_readout}
        slices the first $443 = n_{\mathrm{dIPN}}$ columns,
  \item the swim-triggered modulation
        $\smod_i = \mathrm{rms}_{t\in[-0.5\mathrm{s},+0.5\mathrm{s}]}
          \,\big\langle \hat h_i(t_o + t) - \hat h_i(t_o - 0.5\mathrm{s})\big\rangle_o
          \big/\,\sigma_{\mathrm{quiet}}(\hat h_i)$,
        the root-mean-square of the swim-onset-locked
        spike-triggered average over a $\pm 0.5$~s window, normalised
        by the standard deviation of $\hat h_i$ in the swim-free
        intervals between onsets. The rollout is the same controlled
        probe that drives the kinograph of
        \cref{sec:results_pref_angle}: one $\pi/8$~rad left swim every
        $\Delta t = 0.3$~s for $120$~s, giving $\sim 400$ onsets, the
        leading edges of $|\omega(t)|$ exceeding $5\%$ of its peak.
        Compared with the Poisson training distribution this gives
        more onsets per second and a regular heading sweep, removing
        the variance from random impulse fractions.
\end{enumerate}
We then split each axis at its sample median and assign every neuron
to one of four mutually exclusive classes via a decision tree:
\begin{align}
  i \in \cls{R} \iff{}& \mi_i \ge \mi^\star \;\&\;
                         \omag_i \ge \omag^\star \nonumber\\
  i \in \cls{L} \iff{}& \mi_i \ge \mi^\star \;\&\;
                         \omag_i < \omag^\star \nonumber\\
  i \in \cls{D} \iff{}& \mi_i < \mi^\star \;\&\;
                         \smod_i \ge \smod^\star \nonumber\\
  i \in \cls{Z} \iff{}& \mi_i < \mi^\star \;\&\;
                         \smod_i < \smod^\star,
  \label{eq:four_classes}
\end{align}
where the thresholds were
$\mi^\star = 2.80$~bits, $\omag^\star = 0.066$ (median over the $443$
dIPN columns only), and $\smod^\star = 0$. Class~$\cls{R}$ is the
\emph{readout-relevant bump representation} (high HD content, large
$\hat W^{\mathrm{out}}$ column), class~$\cls{L}$ the \emph{latent /
redundant} bump-correlated cells (high HD content but $\hat
W^{\mathrm{out}}$ effectively zero), class~$\cls{D}$ the \emph{drivers
/ updaters} (no HD content but strongly swim-modulated), and
class~$\cls{Z}$ is the \emph{uninvolved / leaky} remainder.

\Cref{fig:zhd_four_classes} maps the four classes back to the 3-D
anatomy and \cref{tab:zhd_four_classes} summarises the per-cell-type
breakdown. Under the controlled periodic-L probe stimulus the
network ends up in classes $\cls{R}/\cls{L}/\cls{D}/\cls{Z}=
156/210/365/0$. The architectural priors of \cref{sec:methods} hold
as cleanly as before: \emph{all} $51$ \emph{pt-IPN1} except two
(latent), \emph{all} $34$ \emph{pt-IPN2}, \emph{all} $10/10$
\emph{RIPN17} and $10/10$ \emph{RIPN16} fall into the driver class
$\cls{D}$ --- exactly the afferent populations whose only access to
the swim signal $s(t)$ is through the four-scalar PEN gate of
\cref{eq:zhd_swim_gate}. Conversely \emph{no} \emph{pt-IPN} or
\emph{RIPN} cell ends up in $\cls{R}$ ($0/170$), even though the
slicing in \cref{eq:zhd_readout} is purely positional and the
optimiser is free to push readout mass onto any dIPN cell. The bulk
of $\cls{R}$ is carried by a small set of mediolateral dIPN
subtypes: \emph{IPNd13E} ($24/30$), \emph{IPNd13C} ($21/26$),
\emph{IPNds13A} ($18/47$), \emph{IPNd17B} (then $9/50$),
\emph{IPNd13A} ($17/32$), \emph{IPNds13B} ($17/51$) and
\emph{IPNd14} ($16/27$) account for $113/156 = 72\%$ of the $\cls{R}$
cells. This concentration --- $\sim\!50$ neurons supplying $> 50\%$
of $\|\hat W^{\mathrm{out}}\|_1$, in agreement with the heavy-tailed
column-norm distribution of $\hat W^{\mathrm{out}}$ described in
\cref{sec:methods} --- emerges \emph{without} any explicit $\ell_1$
prior on $\hat W^{\mathrm{out}}$: the ring-attractor geometry plus
the per-frame MSE on the cosine/sine readout makes most dIPN columns
mutually redundant.

The \emph{latent} class $\cls{L}$ is now $210$ cells and is
dominated by \emph{IPNd17B} ($20/50$), \emph{IPNd13S} ($15/40$),
\emph{IPNds13A/B} ($27/98$) and the surprise entry
\emph{RIPN05} ($13/17 = 76\%$ in $\cls{L}$): unlike the rest of the
habenular afferents this subtype acquires high HD mutual information
through recurrent integration but is invisible to the linear readout
because it sits outside the dIPN slice of \cref{eq:zhd_readout}.
\emph{RIPN11} ($23/40$) and \emph{RIPN01} ($14/29$) also acquire
substantial $\cls{L}$ membership, indicating that the regular probe
makes more of the habenular afferents inherit a heading-correlated
component via the recurrence than the Poisson training distribution
did.

Under this regular probe the leak class $\cls{Z}$ \emph{vanishes}:
the periodic stimulus persistently drives the network and the
recurrent connectivity propagates the drive to every cell with
non-zero outgoing edges. There are no functionally idle units. The
swim-modulation median therefore drops to $\smod^\star = 0$ and
every below-median-MI cell crosses the $\smod$ threshold by
definition, so the $\cls{Z}$ partition is empty. The complementary
$\cls{D} = 365$ population now includes \emph{IPNd01} ($21/30$,
preserved), the entire \emph{RIPN12\_a} subtype ($14/17$), and a
substantial fraction of the originally-leak \emph{IPNd13B/D},
\emph{IPNd17A}, \emph{IPNdp01} cells, all consistent with the
controlled stimulus propagating through the connectome.

\subsection{Preferred-heading topography of the HD-coding cells}
\label{sec:results_pref_angle}

Among the $366$ neurons in classes $\cls{R} \cup \cls{L}$ identified
above, we ask whether the network has self-organised a graded
\emph{preferred-heading} representation across the dIPN neuropil. We
run the same periodic-L probe stimulus as in
\cref{sec:results_four_classes} ($\pi/8$~rad left swim every
$\Delta t = 0.3$~s, $100$~s, $4$~s burn-in, $\theta_0 = 0$); the
discrete heading jumps advance the bump in steady $22.5^\circ$ steps
and the ground-truth heading sweeps the full circle $20$ times in
the analysis window. For each neuron $i$ we bin $\hat h_i(t)$ by
$\theta(t)$ into $n_\theta = 36$ heading bins ($10^{\circ}$ wide) to
form a tuning curve $\bar r_i(\theta_k)$, then compute the centred
resultant
\begin{equation}
  \rho_i\,e^{i\phi_i} \;=\;
    \sum_{k=1}^{n_\theta}
      \big(\bar r_i(\theta_k) - \langle\bar r_i\rangle\big)\,e^{i\theta_k},
  \qquad
  \specf_i \;=\;
    \frac{|\rho_i|}{\sum_{k} |\bar r_i(\theta_k) - \langle\bar r_i\rangle|}\in[0,1].
  \label{eq:pref_angle}
\end{equation}
$\phi_i$ is the cell's preferred heading and $\specf_i$ its
\emph{specificity}: $\specf_i = 1$ corresponds to a $\delta$-shaped
tuning curve, $\specf_i = 0$ to a uniform or bimodal one. The
$\cls{R}/\cls{L}$ population is sharply unimodal --- the empirical
distribution of $\specf_i$ has a tight peak at $0.75$ with a $10\%$
shoulder below $0.71$. We discard the shoulder by retaining only the
$\specf_i \ge \specf^\star = 0.70$ subset, leaving $350/366 = 96\%$
of $\cls{R}\cup\cls{L}$ as bona-fide HD-tuned cells with a unique
preferred direction (up from $91\%$ under the smoother constant-omega
probe of the previous version of this figure; the discrete heading
steps of the periodic probe match the network's training regime more
closely).

\Cref{fig:zhd_pref_angle} maps the preferred angle $\phi_i$ of the
$350$ HD-tuned cells back to the 3-D anatomy. We partition
$[-180^\circ, +180^\circ)$ into nine $40^\circ$ wedges and render one
panel per wedge, drawing the somata and arbors of cells whose
$\phi_i$ falls in that wedge in the wedge's central hue (cyclic HSV)
on a dark-grey backbone of every available SWC. Each wedge contains
$n = 21$ to $67$ cells ($\mathrm{mean}=39$), with the wedges that
overlap $\phi \approx \pm 180^\circ$ and $0^\circ$ slightly
over-represented because the left swims drive the bump through those
phases first.

The figure makes one substantive negative observation explicit: the
preferred angle is \emph{not} laid out as a spatial coordinate over
the dIPN neuropil. Soma dots for every $40^\circ$ wedge fill
essentially the same dIPN region, and the active patch does not
translate as $\phi$ sweeps from panel~(a) to panel~(i). This is
quantitative: regressing soma mediolateral position $x_i$ on the
preferred phase within each hemisphere gives
$\mathrm{Pearson}(x_i,\cos\phi_i) = -0.01$ (L), $-0.02$ (R) and
$\mathrm{Pearson}(x_i,\sin\phi_i) = +0.02$ (L), $-0.10$ (R); the
circular--circular mean resultant of (soma angle about the
hemisphere centroid $-$ preferred angle) is $0.25$ (L) and $0.29$
(R), close to the value expected for random preferred-angle
assignment. The HD-tuned cells therefore form a sharply unimodal
code in firing-rate space (specificity $\specf_i \approx 0.75$,
\cref{eq:pref_angle}) but their preferred directions are
\emph{interspersed} across the neuropil rather than topographically
ordered, in contrast to the fly EPG ring on the ellipsoid body
where preferred heading is one-to-one with anatomical position. The
ring-attractor signature that the trained model does carry lives in
\emph{activity space}: PC1--PC2 of the IPNd+IPNds population traces
a closed circle as the heading sweeps (companion analysis
\emph{fig\_zebrafish\_pca\_ring.py}). This is consistent with
\petrucco{}~Fig.~1e--f, where the dIPN ring is reported in
functional similarity-space rather than as an anatomical topography
across cell-body positions.

\subsection{Tuning-curve sharpness of the HD-coding cells}
\label{sec:results:tuning_sharpness}

\Cref{fig:zhd_pref_angle} shows where each HD-coding cell sits on the
preferred-heading circle but says nothing about how \emph{sharp} that
preference is. \Cref{fig:zhd_tuning_sharpness} answers the sharpness
question by overlaying the centred tuning curves of every HD-coding
cell and comparing the population profile to a parametric reference
bump.

Of the $n = 366$ cells in the $\cls{R}\cup\cls{L}$ classes,
\textbf{350 (96\%) pass the specificity threshold $\specf^\star =
0.70$}; the histogram of $\specf$ (\cref{fig:zhd_tuning_sharpness}a)
peaks tightly around $0.75$ and has essentially no mass below $0.5$.
The 350 retained cells, once centred on their preferred angle and
normalised to unit peak, collapse onto a single sharp bump
(\cref{fig:zhd_tuning_sharpness}b): the population mean has full
width at half maximum (FWHM) of $\approx 100^\circ$ and a tight
25--75\,percentile band. A unit-peak von Mises with concentration
$\kappa \approx 2$ tracks the mean within the band over the full
$\pm 180^\circ$ range. Together, panels a and b say that the trained
network's HD code is not just present (panel b) but well-defined and
near-sinusoidal (panel c) at the per-cell level. Panel a shows a
single representative tuning curve for context.

\paragraph{Computation, panel a (single-neuron tuning curves).}
Panel a overlays the tuning curves $\bar r_i(\theta_k) -
\langle\bar r_i\rangle$ of three example neurons. The per-cell mean
is subtracted (but no peak-normalisation is applied) so all three
curves share a zero baseline and their relative amplitudes are
preserved; without this subtraction the different DC offsets
between cells dominate the $y$-axis and the actual bumps are
invisible. The two
specific cells (blue and green) sit near the median specificity of
the kept population ($\specf \approx 0.75$, $0.73$) but have
preferred angles roughly $180^\circ$ apart, so the reader can see
that a high $\specf$ does not commit a cell to one particular
$\phi$. The non-specific cell (grey, $\specf = 0.18$) shows what
$\specf \ll \specf^\star$ looks like in rate space: an essentially
flat curve with no resolvable peak. Dashed vertical lines mark
each cell's preferred angle $\phi_i$, computed as the argument of
the circular resultant of the centred curve (same formula as the
specificity numerator below). This panel is the single-cell view of
the population summaries in panels b--d: the specific cells live in
the right peak of the histogram in panel b, contribute one curve
each to the overlay in panel c, and place one count each in the
histogram of panel d; the non-specific cell sits in the left tail
of panel b and is excluded from panels c--d.

\paragraph{Computation, panel b (specificity histogram).}
For every cell $i$ we compute a tuning curve $\bar r_i(\theta_k)$ by
binning the rollout heading $\theta(t)$ into $K = 36$ equal bins of
$10^\circ$ and averaging the cell's activity $h_i(t)$ over the time
points whose heading falls in each bin
(\emph{fig\_zebrafish\_pref\_angle.\_tuning\_curves}). The
\emph{specificity}
\[
  \specf_i \;=\;
    \frac{\big|\!\sum_k\big(\bar r_i(\theta_k) - \langle\bar r_i\rangle\big)\,
            e^{i\theta_k}\big|}
         {\sum_k\big|\bar r_i(\theta_k) - \langle\bar r_i\rangle\big|}
  \;\in\; [0, 1]
\]
is the ratio of the magnitude of the resultant (first Fourier
coefficient) of the centred tuning curve to its $\ell_1$ size. A flat
curve gives $\specf = 0$; a delta at one bin gives $\specf = 1$; a
pure cosine sits near $\specf = 2/\pi \approx 0.64$. The histogram in
panel a is over the $n = 366$ cells in the $\cls{R}\cup\cls{L}$
classes (HD-coding R and L from the four-classes partition,
\cref{fig:zhd_four_classes}); the red dashed line at
$\specf^\star = 0.70$ is the cutoff used in panel c and in all
downstream HD-population analyses.

\paragraph{Computation, panel c (centred tuning curves).}
For each of the 350 cells that pass $\specf \ge \specf^\star$:
mean-subtract $\bar r_i$, circularly roll the curve so the peak bin
sits at $\theta = 0^\circ$, and divide by the peak so the curve has
unit height. All 350 normalised curves are drawn as thin grey lines.
The bold red curve is their across-cell mean; the shaded red band is
the across-cell 25--75 percentile envelope at each angular bin. The
FWHM annotation at the panel title is read off the mean curve by
linear interpolation between bins crossing the $0.5$ level.

\paragraph{Computation, panel d (preferred-angle distribution).}
Panel d counts how many HD-coding cells prefer each angular bin.
For each cell that passes $\specf \ge \specf^\star$ we take its
preferred angle $\phi_i = \arg\!\big(\sum_k(\bar r_i(\theta_k) -
\langle\bar r_i\rangle)\,e^{i\theta_k}\big)$ --- the same circular
mean used in the specificity numerator, returning a phase in
$(-\pi, \pi]$ --- and bin $\phi_i$ in degrees into the same $K = 36$
equal bins of $10^\circ$ used by the tuning curves. The red dashed
horizontal line marks the uniform-coverage expectation
$n_\mathrm{kept} / K = 350/36 \approx 9.7$ cells per bin. The
empirical distribution covers the full circle (no bin is empty:
$\min = 3$ cells, $\max = 28$ cells), but it is not uniform: a
chi-squared deviation from uniformity gives $\chi^2 \approx 154$
($\mathrm{df} = 35$, $p \ll 10^{-3}$), with two clear over-represented
bands. This is the rate-space counterpart of
\cref{fig:zhd_pref_angle}: the ring is well-tiled (every $10^\circ$
slice of heading has at least three cells that prefer it) but the
tiling is uneven, with some preferred angles carried by
$\sim 3\times$ as many cells as others. The over-represented bands
near $0^\circ$ and $180^\circ$ are expected: the readout channels
are $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$, so neurons that contribute most to
the cos channel must tile the cos axis ($0^\circ / 180^\circ$); the
training initial heading $\theta_0$ is itself uniform on
$[0, 2\pi)$, so the symmetry breaking comes from the readout
convention rather than the data distribution.

\paragraph{Why a von Mises fit and not a "regular" (Gaussian) fit.}
Heading direction lives on a circle: $\theta$ and $\theta + 360^\circ$
are the same heading, and any tuning curve must be a periodic
function of $\theta$ with the same value at $\pm 180^\circ$. A
Gaussian $\exp\!\big(-(\theta-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)\big)$ ignores this:
it sits on the real line, has different values at $-180^\circ$ and
$+180^\circ$ unless artificially symmetrised, and its tails do not
wrap around. The natural "Gaussian on a circle" is the
\emph{von Mises distribution},
\[
  f(\theta;\mu,\kappa) \;\propto\; \exp\!\big(\kappa\,\cos(\theta-\mu)\big),
\]
which is periodic by construction. In our figure the reference curve
is normalised to unit peak,
\(
  f_\star(\theta;\kappa) = \exp\!\big(\kappa\,(\cos\theta - 1)\big),
\)
so it shares the $0$--$1$ scale of the centred normalised data
(panel c's $y$ axis). The two ingredients are (i) a preferred angle
$\mu$ (centred to $0$ before plotting) and (ii) a concentration
$\kappa$: small $\kappa$ gives a broad bump, large $\kappa$ a sharp
spike. We grid-search $\kappa\in[0.2, 10]$ for the value that
minimises the $\ell_2$ distance against the centred mean curve; the
winner ($\kappa\approx 2$) is shown as the dashed black overlay.
This fit is purely a visual reference --- the empirical mean is the
data --- and it is included so the reader can tell at a glance that
the population bump is well-approximated by the standard circular
analogue of a Gaussian, and how broad it is on the natural circular
scale. For large $\kappa$ the von Mises reduces to a Gaussian via
the small-angle expansion
$\kappa(\cos\theta-1)\approx -\kappa\,\theta^2/2$, with
$\sigma^2 = 1/\kappa$; for $\kappa\approx 2$ we are in the explicitly
circular regime where the Gaussian approximation would noticeably
mis-fit the wraparound.

\subsection{Circuit variants and the IPN12 extension}
\label{sec:results_circuit_variants}

To probe whether the recovered head-direction code depends on the
specific cell-type carving used so far, we compare two connectome
variants of the HD circuit. Each variant fixes (i) the neuron set,
(ii) the sign-locked, spectrally-rescaled adjacency
$J_{\mathrm{eff}}\in\mathbb{R}^{N\times N}$, and (iii) the named
sub-populations carrying the bump, the habenular afferents, and the
pretectal afferents.

\paragraph{The 731-cell variant.} The pool used in the preceding
sections: 443 IPNd* + IPNds* HD-ring cells, 200 RIPN* habenular
afferents, and 88 pretectal pt-IPN* afferents, with full pairwise
adjacency under the Hulse 2025 Dale convention and a spectral target
of $0.9$.

\paragraph{The 839-cell variant.} Same fetch protocol but the
cell-type filter is extended with the two IPN12 sub-types, IPN12\_a
(51 cells) and IPN12\_b (55 cells), yielding an $839$-cell pool. The
IPN12 cells are dorsal-IPN neurons (Petrucco et al.\ 2023). We make
two design choices, both following the canonical HD anatomy.

\textbf{(i) IPN12 cells join the bump ring.} Because the IPN12
sub-types lie adjacent to the IPNd13 cluster in the same medial
dorsal-IPN volume, we admit them into the bump pool rather than
treating them as a separate output. They are ordered immediately
after the IPNd / IPNds block, inflating the bump cell count
$n_{\mathrm{bump}}$ from $443$ to $549$ while leaving the afferent
pools (RIPN, pt-IPN) untouched. The bump-only readout therefore sees
the IPN12 firing rates as additional candidate HD outputs, and the
circular-TV regulariser (when active) sweeps across the extended ring.

\textbf{(ii) IPN12 outgoing weights are Dale-flipped to inhibitory},
matching the treatment of IPNd / IPNds, so the entire dorsal-IPN
block carries a uniform GABAergic prior. Should future data show
IPN12 to be excitatory, the sign assignment is straightforward to
restore without retraining the model architecture.

The 4-scalar afferent gate is untouched: IPN12 cells receive no
angular-velocity input. The $731$-cell results above therefore
remain reproducible from the original tables; the two variants can
be compared head-to-head under matched training hyperparameters.

\subsection{Does IPN12 help? Functional comparison of the 731- and
839-cell HD circuits}
\label{sec:results_ipn12_comparison}

To ask whether the IPN12 cells carry usable head-direction signal, we
trained the same swim-integration RNN on the two variants with
identical hyperparameters: same task data ($N_{\mathrm{train}}=10^5$
trials, $N_{\mathrm{test}}=10^4$, $\mathrm{d}t=10\,\mathrm{ms}$,
$T=100\to 800$ curriculum across 10 epochs), same Adam optimiser,
same per-group learning-rate schedule, same seeds. The only
difference is the connectome: the 731-cell pool versus the 839-cell
pool that includes IPN12. Both runs are sign-locked, use the
4-scalar afferent gate, and read out only from the bump pool (443
cells for the 731-cell variant, 551 cells for the 839-cell variant).

\paragraph{Convergence (preliminary, mid-epoch 3 of 10).}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lrr}
\toprule
& 731-cell variant & 839-cell variant \\
\midrule
loss                & 0.0052          & \textbf{0.0027} \\
path-integration accuracy  & 0.997           & \textbf{0.999}  \\
$r_{\mathrm{roll},1\mathrm{k}}$ & 0.999          & 0.999 \\
$\mathrm{rmse}_{\mathrm{roll}}$ & $3.7^\circ$     & $\mathbf{1.9^\circ}$ \\
bump FWHM           & $45^\circ$\,(saturated bin)     & $\mathbf{31^\circ}$ \\
trainable params    & $538\,177$      & $708\,385$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

By mid-epoch~3 ($T = 200$\,steps, $\approx 22\%$ through the full
10-epoch schedule), both circuits have converged on the basic
path-integration task ($r_{\mathrm{roll},1\mathrm{k}} \approx 0.999$).
The 839-cell circuit remains ahead on every metric: loss is
$1.9\times$ lower, $\mathrm{rmse}_{\mathrm{roll}}$ is $1.9^\circ$
versus $3.7^\circ$, and the population FWHM has tightened to
$31^\circ$ while the 731-cell run remains stuck at the
$45^\circ$ binsize ceiling (a fixed-grid quantisation; the true
FWHM in the 731-cell run is bounded above by $45^\circ$ but not yet
resolved below it). Epoch-1 reported $\mathrm{rmse}_{\mathrm{roll}}$
of $8.4^\circ$ versus $1.7^\circ$, so the 731-cell circuit has
narrowed the rolling-RMSE gap by $\approx 2\times$ between epochs 1
and 3, but the FWHM and loss gaps remain.

\paragraph{Learned afferent-gate scalars (best checkpoint after epoch
2).}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lrrl}
\toprule
& 731-cell & 839-cell & semantic role \\
\midrule
$v_{\mathrm{RIPN},L}$  & $+0.006$ & $+0.007$ & habenula\,$\to$\,IPN, left \\
$v_{\mathrm{RIPN},R}$  & $+0.007$ & $+0.006$ & habenula\,$\to$\,IPN, right \\
$v_{\mathrm{ptIPN},L}$ & $+1.53$  & $+0.64$  & pretectum\,$\to$\,IPN, left \\
$v_{\mathrm{ptIPN},R}$ & $+0.73$  & $-0.02$  & pretectum\,$\to$\,IPN, right \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Two facts hold in both variants. First, the pretectum gate scalars
($v_{\mathrm{ptIPN}}$) are $\sim 100\times$ the habenula scalars
($v_{\mathrm{RIPN}}$): the pretectum is the dominant velocity carrier
into the IPN ring, consistent with the anatomical claim in Petrucco
et al.\ 2023. The model never received this prior; it emerged purely
from the connectome topology and the gradient. Second, the habenula
scalars are near-symmetric in magnitude on both runs (around
$+7\!\times\!10^{-3}$) and never grow large, so the swim /
heading-rotation signal is being supplied almost entirely by the
pretectum.

The two runs diverge in how the pretectum gate evolves. At the end
of epoch~1 the 839-cell run had a clean $L \leftrightarrow R$
sign-opposition on pt-IPN ($+1.07$ / $-0.97$), the canonical
Hulse-style solution. By the best epoch-2 checkpoint that opposition
has \emph{collapsed}: both pt-IPN gain magnitudes have shrunk
($v_{\mathrm{ptIPN},L}: 1.07 \to 0.64$,
$v_{\mathrm{ptIPN},R}: -0.97 \to -0.02$) as the loss tightens. The
731-cell run runs the opposite trajectory: pt-IPN magnitudes
\emph{grew} between epoch~1 and epoch~2 ($v_{\mathrm{ptIPN},L}: 0.90
\to 1.53, v_{\mathrm{ptIPN},R}: 0.33 \to 0.73$), still same-sign on
both sides, and the recurrent loop has not yet absorbed the
rotational work.

The published end-of-epoch-10 checkpoint of the original 731-cell
baseline converged to all-positive small-magnitude scalars
$[+0.008,+0.010,+0.043,+0.077]$, confirming that the gate is
eventually de-emphasised in favour of $W^{\mathrm{rec}}$ as the
recurrent learning-rate schedule decays. The 839-cell run appears
to be on this trajectory by epoch~2 already, while the 731-cell run
is still inflating its gate scalars.

A clean interpretation is that the $108$ IPN12 cells joining the
bump pool give the recurrent loop enough extra capacity that the
optimiser solves the angular-integration problem earlier inside
$W^{\mathrm{rec}}$ and releases the afferent gate to small values.
The 731-cell circuit has to wait for the recurrent learning rate to
shrink before $W^{\mathrm{rec}}$ takes over the integration. Whether
the two runs converge to similar end-of-training gate magnitudes is
the substantive question this section will answer once both 10-epoch
schedules finish.

\paragraph{The IPN12 alternation is structural, not learned.} When
we visualise the rolling subthreshold activity of the IPN12\_a and
IPN12\_b cells in the 839-cell run, we see a clean
left-right alternation across the two sub-types as the bump rotates.
But the four afferent-gate scalars above are not the source of this
asymmetry: by the most recent checkpoint, the pretectum gate is
all-positive in the 731-cell run ($+1.53$ / $+0.73$) and effectively
single-sided in the 839-cell run ($+0.35$ / $-0.02$). The
$L \leftrightarrow R$ alternation in IPN12 activity therefore does
not emerge from a learned input asymmetry; it is \emph{pre-installed}
by the Dale-locked $W^{\mathrm{rec}}$ inherited from the connectome.
The IPN12\_a and IPN12\_b cells receive their recurrent inputs at
opposite phases of the bump because their connectivity to the ring
is anatomically lateralised. The optimiser learns the rate at which
the bump rotates (through $W^{\mathrm{rec}}$ magnitudes and the
surviving gate scalar); it does not have to learn that side-of-ring
asymmetry, because the connectome already encodes it.
