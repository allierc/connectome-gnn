\section{Introduction}

Building a mechanistic model of a brain from measurements of its wiring and its activity is a long-standing goal of neuroscience, and one that is becoming concrete: Synapse-resolution connectomes paired with neural activity recordings from the same brain \citep{MICrONS-Consortium2025-pt} [V1DD, Zebrafish] open the prospect of mechanistic, data-constrained models of neural circuits. It is unclear, however, whether such data actually suffice to identify the underlying brain system, and what reliable computational strategies for doing so would look like.

Different threads of prior work bound the answer. 
System identification theory establishes that recovering dynamical systems parameters requires observations rich enough to disambiguate them \citep{Astrom1965-ri, Ljung2010-by}. In the neural circuit setting, this raises two questions: How varied must the observation be, and how can such variation be achieved experimentally? At least the former question can hardly be addressed without computational models.

In computational neuroscience, it is known that different parameter settings of the same circuit architecture can produce indistinguishable activity \citep{Prinz2004-pb, Marder2011-cc}, leaving open the extent to which degenerate circuit parameters can be disambiguated from data.

Machine learning models, trained by backpropagation, provide powerful tools to find circuit parameters that accurately uncover the input-output computation of a system, such as stimulus-response neural time series. Many machine learning architectures exist to solve sequential time-series problems \citep{Elman1990-ar, Vaswani2017-wz, Gu2023-at, Krishnan2015-ri, Chen2018-gd}, but general-purpose models are not designed to function like the brain and uncover its underlying parameters and mechanisms. Using such models as tools for neuroscience shifts the question from \emph{which architecture is most performant in predicting time-series} to \emph{which architecture is most suitable for brain simulation} --- that is, most interpretable and most easily constrained by the heterogenous measurements experimentalists actually collect.

Providing concrete answers to these questions requires access to ground-truth connectivity and dynamics from the same system, which no current experimental dataset fully provides: Available large-scale datasets contain either only partial brain measurements \citep{MICrONS-Consortium2025-pt}, activity but no connectivity measurements \citep{Ahrens2013-rd, lueckmann_zapbench_2025}, or connectivity \citep{Dorkenwald2024-ni} and activity \citep{Currier2025-af} from different individuals. While new datasets are underway, many of these system-identification questions can be addressed today in a fully computational framework built on recent large-scale, connectome-constrained circuit simulations \citep{Lappalainen2024-tq, Shiu2024-rz}. 

We developed a 'teacher--student' setup in which the teacher is a variant of a recent fly visual-system simulation \citep{Lappalainen2024-tq}. The original model, a continuous-time recurrent dynamical system defined on the fly connectome graph (45K neurons, 1.5M connections) and trained on the computer vision dataset Sintel \citep{Butler2012-sj} detects optical flow in naturalistic video and recapitulates single-neuron response properties across three decades of experimental research, without knowledge of that data. We extended the teacher with the most recent whole-eye connectome data \citep{Dorkenwald2024-ni, Schlegel2024-qh} to test counterfactuals for identifiability such as the convolutional assumption of the original model or connectivity uncertainty from EM measurements, resulting in model variants at varying scales ($13$K or $50$K neurons; $0.4$M or $5$M edges).
As students, we evaluate a broad selection of ML sequence models and propose as our primary model class an interpretable recurrent graph neural network (GNN) that can be flexibly conditioned on neural and synaptic measurements, and that admits learnable latent variables for circuit structure such as cell types.
As a baseline against which to compare the GNN, we use an oracle student that knows the true ODE of the teacher. Computational models in the form of differential equations of neural circuit electrical activity are often designed by tailoring equation components to match specific observed data \citep{Abbott1999-sh, Hodgkin1952-eq}, and an oracle of this form represents the strongest version of that choice. At brain scale, however, it is unlikely that a single ``true'' ODE exists, and models that flexibly parametrize neural and synaptic interactions and absorb measurement constraints become more practical than models hand-designed for a particular system or set of observations.
Finally, for any gradient-based model class, tuning training hyperparameters such that the same model class works equivalently well across data settings is itself nontrivial. We therefore introduce an agentic hyperparameter optimization (HPO) procedure that identifies a single GNN training configuration for robust system identification across conditions such as connectivity and activity completeness, or neuronal noise level.

    \item We find that the GNN recovers effective connectivity weights, neuron parameters and types, and nonlinear activation functions. %, even with 400\% random connections are added to the given connectivity.

\section{Related work}


Prior studies were successful in retrieving functional connectivity, functional properties of neurons, or predictive models of neural activity at the population level, but a method to infer an interpretable mechanistic model of complex neural systems is yet missing.

Several lines of work address related but distinct
problems.
A study by \citet{Lueckmann2026-hl} establishes a similar in silico testbed using a larval zebrafish visuomotor circuit of $\sim$36 units, showing that LLM-guided code search recovers effective connectivity when given structural priors. 
\citet{mi_connectome-constrained_2021} infer the hidden voltage dynamics and functional connectivity from calcium recordings of parts of the \emph{C. elegans} nervous system using a simple biophysical neuron model.  While its predictive performance is modest, it demonstrates that integrating real activity recordings with connectivity can yield experimentally testable predictions beyond simulation.  \citet{pospisil_fly_2024} used the connectome of \emph{Drosophila melanogaster} \citep{dorkenwald_neuronal_2024} to create simulations of full brain activity and inferred effective connectivity from known perturbations of individual neurons with a simple linear model.  Lacking information about connectivity, \citet{wang_foundation_2025} trained a foundation model of mouse visual cortex activity on real two-photon recordings from 135,000 neurons, which predicts responses to both natural videos and out-of-distribution stimuli across animals.
\citet{prasse_predicting_2022} focus on accurate prediction without recovering connectivity while \citet{yu_discovering_2025} infer symbolic dynamical equations without validating connectivity weights. Both work with simulated data and do not learn neuronal heterogeneity.

Our method learns the functional properties of individual neurons and their relative connectivity weights, enabling transfer of the learned dynamics to different network configurations with modified connectivity (or different neuron type compositions).

\section{Methods}

\subsection*{Flyvis simulations} \label{method:simulation}


We simulated neural activity in the \emph{Drosophila} visual system using Flyvis pretrained models \citep{lappalainen_connectome-constrained_2024}.  Each neuron is modeled as a non-spiking voltage compartment governed by the ordinary differential equation (ODE)
\begin{equation}
\tau_i\frac{dv_i(t)}{dt} = -v_i(t) + V_i^{\text{rest}}
+ \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i}\mathbf{W}_{ij}\,\text{ReLU}\!\big(v_j(t)\big)
+ I_i(t) + \sigma\,\xi_i(t),
\label{eq:simulated_update}
\end{equation}
where $v_i$ is the neuron membrane voltage, $\tau_i$ and
$V_i^{\mathrm{rest}}$ are cell-type parameters, $\mathbf{W}_{ij}$ is the
connectome synaptic weight, and $I_i(t)$ is the visual input. Visual
inputs are derived from the DAVIS-2017 dataset
\citep{perazzi_benchmark_2016}, a benchmark dataset for annotated
natural videos (see details in Appendix~\ref{app:agent}). A null
stimulus ($I_i(t)=0$) is prepended to every sequence so that neurons
relax toward $V_i^{\mathrm{rest}}$ during the first half of each clip,
which is essential for parameter identifiability
(Appendix~\ref{app:stimulus}). $\xi_i(t)\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ is an
independent Gaussian noise scaled by $\sigma$ (model noise), perturbing
the dynamical trajectory of $v_i(t)$. We additionally apply a
measurement noise directly to $v_i(t)$, Gaussian noise scaled by
$\gamma$. See examples of simulated data in
Fig.~\ref{fig:stimulus_hexagons}, with and without model and
measurement noise in Fig.~\ref{fig:simulations}.  We first restricted the original $721$
retinotopic columns to the central subset of $217$
columns, covering the functionally relevant central visual field while substantially reducing memory and runtime requirements during tets and optimization. The simulated recurrent neural network contains then $13{,}741$ neurons from $65$ cell types and $434{,}122$ synaptic connections. Next, we expanded the simulations to a hybrid connectome that retains the trained flyvis dynamical model but inherits per-column wiring heterogeneity and connectivity uncertainty from FlyWire \citep{dorkenwald_neuronal_2024, Schlegel2024-qh}.

\paragraph{Coregistration}

\cite{lappalainen_connectome-constrained_2024} compiles whole-eye connectivity from local reconstructions. The resulting flyvis model connectome is a convolutional (translation-invariant) graph: Every visual column shares the same set of $2,355$ edge templates, each parametrized by source type, target type, and column offset. This reductionist approach discards real synaptic connectivity variability across columns and anatomical location information of neurons and synaptic connections.

To recover real synaptic connectivity variability and anatomical location information, we coregistered the flyvis connectome to FlyWire (FAFB-v783) with a pipeline including several phases: Cell type matching ($65$ flyvis types),  hex-grid orientation, per-column node registration of flyvis $(\text{type}, u, v)$ tuples to FlyWire \texttt{root\_id}s, edge registration via the FlyWire synapse table aggregated by source/target identity, synapse-coordinate attribution, node column inference based on connectivity, and unmatched cell type matching through connectivity.

We tiled the flyvis convolutional template ($65$ cell types, $2{,}355$ edge templates) across every reconstructed column of the right-optic-lobe in FlyWire. This resulted in $50{,}412$ candidate nodes and $1{,}650{,}671$ candidate edges for coregistration. Of these, $64.9\%$ of nodes register to a FlyWire \texttt{root\_id} and $48.5\%$ of edges have both endpoints registered, jointly covering $93.5\%$ of the $2{,}355$ templates and matching $3{,}800{,}254$ FlyWire synapses. The hybrid graph keeps FlyWire-observed edges wherever the postsynaptic neuron's anatomical receptive field was reconstructed in FlyWire and falls back to the flyvis template edges elsewhere. It adds novel edges present only in FlyWire for type pairs that already exist in the flyvis template. Restricted to the central $217$-column crop used in our experiments, this results in a graph of $327{,}358$ edges across $13{,}741$ nodes (Suppl. Tab.~\ref{tab:hybrid_pipeline_stats}). 

Because FlyWire synapse counts were systematically higher than flyvis template values (median ratio $~ 1.39\times$ at shared offsets), we applied a multiplicative per-type-pair mean-ratio correction to each FlyWire edge, so that edge weights remain compatible with the trained flyvis scaling parameters and therefore ODE for simulation.

\paragraph{Connectivity uncertainty augmentation}

To model false-positive connectivity from electron-microscopy reconstruction errors, with anatomical location available from the coregistration, we constructed an augmented variant of the hybrid connectome in which every neuron pair within $\approx 6\,\mu\text{m}$ ($\sim$one column in the optic lobe) inherits
each other's outgoing edges with synapse count zero, across all type
pairs. These zero-weight edges are spatially plausible but
unmeasured connections that the GNN must learn to leave at zero or
activate. The augmentation expands the 217 column hybrid graph from
$327{,}358$ to $2{,}418{,}403$ edges and represents the worst case in
which every confusable edge is included. In our simulations, false positive edges carry no synapse counts, i.e., the data-generating flyvis ODE assigns them zero edge strength and simulated trajectories are unchanged by the augmentation. The GNN student instead places a learnable edge weight (initialised at zero) on every edge, and must learn from the dynamics whether each null edge should remain inactive or carry signal.

\subsubsection*{Graph neural network model} \label{method:GNN}
We approximate the simulated voltage dynamics by a message-passing GNN \citep{gilmer_neural_2017}
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\hat{v}_i(t)}{dt}
= f_\theta\!\Big(v_i(t),\,\mathbf{a}_i,\,
\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i} \hat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}\,
g_\phi\!\big(v_j(t),\mathbf{a}_j\big)^2,\,
I_i(t)\Big).
\label{eq:gnn_ct}
\end{equation}
Nodes of the GNN correspond to neurons and edges correspond to functional synaptic connections. The GNN learns a latent embedding $\mathbf{a}_i\in\mathbb{R}^2$ for each neuron. The learnable vectors $\mathbf{a}_i\in\mathbb{R}^2$ represent compact latent identity, they can map to cell-type-specific properties (like time constants and nonlinearities). Neuron update $f_\theta=\text{MLP}_0$ and edge message $g_\phi=\text{MLP}_1$ are three-layer perceptrons (width $64$, ReLU, linear output). $g_\phi$ maps presynaptic inputs $v_j$ to nonnegative messages (via squaring) depending on latent embedding $\mathbf{a}_j$, weighted by $\hat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}$. $f_\theta$ processes the postsynaptic voltage $v_i$, aggregated input, and external input $I_i(t)$ to predict $d\hat{v}_i(t)/dt$, depending also on latent embedding $\mathbf{a}_i$. During training, inputs $I_i(t)$, adjacency $\mathcal{N}_i$, and activity $v_i(t)$ are given (Appendix \ref{app:GNN}). The MLPs, $\hat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}$, and $\mathbf{a}_i$ are optimized by minimizing difference between simulator $y_i(t)=dv_i(t)/dt$ and GNN predictions $\hat{y}_i(t)=d\hat{v}_i(t)/dt$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}} = \sum_{i,t}\|\hat{y}_i(t) - y_i(t)\|_2
\label{eq:loss_pred}
\end{equation}
Many synaptic weight configurations give rise to identical neural activity, making the recovery of $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ from voltage traces fundamentally non-unique (see Appendix \ref{app:degeneracy}). To constrain the solution space, we added regularization terms to $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}$
\begin{eqnarray}
\mathcal{L} &=& \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}
+ \lambda_0\|\theta\|_1 + \lambda_1\|\phi\|_1 + \lambda_2\|\theta\|_2 + \lambda_3\|\phi\|_2
+ \gamma_0\|\hat{\mathbf{W}}\|_1 + \gamma_1\|\hat{\mathbf{W}}\|_2 \nonumber\\
&& +\; \mu_0\!\left\|\mathrm{ReLU}\!\left(-\tfrac{\partial\,f_\theta(v,m,\mathbf{a},I)}{\partial m}\right)\right\|_2
+ \mu_1\!\left\|\mathrm{ReLU}\!\left(-\tfrac{\partial\,g_\phi(v,\mathbf{a})}{\partial v}\right)\right\|_2 \nonumber +\; \mu_2\|g_\phi(v_\star,\mathbf{a})-v_\star\|_2.
\label{eq:loss_full}
\end{eqnarray}
The $\ell_1$ penalties $\lambda_0,\lambda_1$ on the MLP weights $\theta$ and $\phi$ promote sparsity, biasing $f_\theta$ and $g_\phi$ toward simpler functions; the $\ell_2$ penalties $\lambda_2,\lambda_3$ prevent weight explosion and stabilize training. The $\ell_1$ term $\gamma_0$ on $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ promotes sparse effective connectivity, while the $\ell_2$ term $\gamma_1$ penalizes large synaptic weights. The monotonicity term $\mu_0$ penalizes negative slopes of $f_\theta$ with respect to its aggregated synaptic input $m=\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i}\hat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}\,g_\phi(v_j,\mathbf{a}_j)$, encoding the prior that the postsynaptic derivative increases with net excitatory drive. The monotonicity term $\mu_1$ penalizes negative slopes of $g_\phi$ with respect to $v$, encoding the prior that synaptic drive increases with presynaptic voltage. The normalization term $\mu_2$ anchors $g_\phi$ at a reference voltage $v_\star$, removing the scale degeneracy between $g_\phi$ and $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ in \cref{eq:gnn_ct}. We tuned all the hyperparameters $\lambda_0,\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\lambda_3,\gamma_0,\gamma_1,\mu_0,\mu_1,\mu_2$ and the MLPs structure with an agentic optimization procedure described in Appendix \ref{app:agent}.


\subsubsection*{Benchmark}
We compare GNN results with four baseline models detailed in Appendix~\ref{app:benchmark}: Known-ODE, which assumes knowledge of the full functional form of the ODE and learns the circuit parameters $\tau, \vrest, W$; \mlpbase, which predicts $dv/dt$ as a function of the current $v(t)$ and $I(t)$ using a 2-layer MLP (640 hidden units) with ResNet-style skip connections; \eedbase, which predicts neural activity using a 256D nonlinear dynamical system that is read out by the observed activity; and Stimulus-driven MLP, which predicts $v(t)$ based only on the past stimulus $I(t-(w-1)), \cdots, I(t-1), I(t)$. These baselines span the spectrum from full mechanistic knowledge (Known-ODE) to purely data-driven prediction (\stimbase), while \mlpbase{ }and \eedbase{ }adopt distinct dynamical-systems perspectives without incorporating additional inductive bias.

\section{Results}


We report four quantities throughout the results section: the one-step and
$8{,}000$-frame auto-regressive rollout Pearson~$r$ on neural activity
(per neuron, Fisher-$z$ pooled), and the identity-line $R^2$ on
$\hat{\mathbf{W}}$, $\hat{\boldsymbol{\tau}}$, and
$\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}$. The two parameter $R^2$'s are
computed on the inlier set defined by hard residual thresholds, with
the outlier fraction shown in parentheses. Full definitions are given
in Appendix~\ref{app:metrics}.


\paragraph{Identifiability of parameters.} 
% Vijay's rewrite
The FlyVis model has a columnar structure containing 65 cell types that are repeated over a hexagonal lattice. This symmetric structure allows the weight parameters to be permuted or modified in specific combinations that leave the simulated activity unchanged (see Appendix \ref{app:degeneracy}). For instance, we found a variant circuit with $125{,}952$ edges only that generates the exact same activity as the the complete 434{,}112 edges FlyVis model (rollout $r = 0.95$ over $1{,}000$ frames). Recovering circuit parameters from Flyvis model activity is therefore ill-posed, many distinct circuits produce the same activity. To better asses the identifiability problem, we rewrote the governing equation into a linear form and analysed its design matrix. The latter present singular values equal to zero HOW MUCH ? (symmetries or neurons that are never stimulated) or $\ll 1e-6$ HOW MUCH ? causing null or sloppy directions in parameter space (see Figure \ref{fig:lstsq_param_recovery}). \subparagraph{Results with and without the ODE given.}
Predicting activity alone does not yield understanding of the underlying
circuit. Mechanistic interpretation requires recovering the governing
equation of the neural signal together with the per-neuron and
connection parameters. We separate this inverse problem into two cases
according to whether the form of the governing equation is given or not.
When the equation is given, we train a Known-ODE model ($0.46M$ parameters), the
differentiable version of Eq.~\ref{eq:simulated_update}, whose learnable
parameters are the connectivity weights $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ and the per-neuron $\hat{\boldsymbol{\tau}}$, $\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}$. The shared ReLU
activation is given but cell-type assignments are not
given. With $\ell_1$/$\ell_2$ regularisation on the parameters and an
MSE one-step prediction loss (Appendix~\ref{app:benchmark}), the trained 
Known-ODE models achieves near-perfect activity prediction (one-step $r$ and rollout $r$ are close to $1.00$ over $8{,}000$ frames. Notably, the Known-ODE trained on noisy activity reproduces the noise-free rollout: it cannot explicitly fit the stochastic fluctuations and instead converges on the underlying
deterministic dynamics. The parameter recovery over $> 4.5\cdot 10^5$
values is comparably accurate even at high model noise ($R^2>0.95$, outliers $<10\%$, Fig.~\ref{fig:know_ode_rollout}, Fig.~\ref{fig:known_ode_params_3col_noise_comparison}).
Although the inverse problem is strongly ill-posed, full knowledge
of the equation form combined with $\ell_1$/$\ell_2$ regularisation
makes it tractable.
In contrast to the Know-ODE, the GNN does not have access to the update equation. It
learns jointly the connectivity, a per-neuron embedding, and two
signalling functions $f_\theta, g_\phi$ that together express a wide
family of update rules (Eq.~\ref{eq:gnn_ct}). The training scheme involves a large hyperparameter set
(architecture, learning rates, regularisation coefficients) too large
for grid search, so we tuned it with an agentic loop
(Appendix~\ref{app:agent}, Appendix~\ref{app:GNN}). At low and high
model noise ($\sigma \in \{0.05,\,0.5\}$), the GNN results closely matches the
Known-ODE upper bound. The deterministic
$\sigma=0$ regime is the harder case ($r=0.90\pm 0.21$,
$R^2_{\hat{\mathbf{W}}}=0.89$, $R^2_{\hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}}=0.78$). This is consistent with our identifiability analysis. Per-neuron
noise breaks the degenerate directions of $\mathbf{A}_i$, and is
what enables the GNN to match the Known-ODE upper bound at
$\sigma > 0$. The learned latent embedding $\mathbf{a}_i$ alone separates the $65$
cell types, and combining it with $(\hat{\boldsymbol{\tau}},
\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}})$ and summary statistics of
$\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ matches the clustering accuracy of the same
procedure applied to ground-truth parameters

Fig.~\ref{fig:clustering_appendix},

\paragraph{Edge ablation as a mechanistic test.}
We propose an edge-ablation test to certify that a learned model is
mechanistic, beyond merely predicting activity. For each of the three training-noise regimes ($\sigma \in \{0,\,0.05,\,0.5\}$), we randomly zero $50\%$ of the
$434{,}112$ edges, regenerate $8{,}000$ frames of held-out test
activity with the perturbed connectome, and apply the same edge mask
to the trained GNN's learned weights $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ before
running the autoregressive rollout. No retraining is performed, the
test is whether $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$, $\hat{\boldsymbol{\tau}}$,
$\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}$, $f_\theta$, $g_\phi$
extrapolate to a circuit with half its edges removed. The GNN passes this test at all model noise levels. The per-neuron Pearson correlation between the ablated rollout and the noise-free ablated simulation is $r \geq 0.99$ across all three noise levels
(Fig.~\ref{fig:rollout_3col_noise_comparison_ablation50}). The
agreement holds for individual cell-type traces (panels a--c) and
across the full voltage range (panels d--f). The high cross-condition
agreement indicates that the learned $f_\theta$ and $g_\phi$ encode
the local update rule rather than memorising trajectories, satisfying
our mechanistic criterion and complementing the parameter-recovery
results of Fig.~\ref{fig:gnn_params_3col_noise_comparison}.

\paragraph{Data degradation (measurement noise, partial data, unknown stimulus).}
We evaluate the GNN ($\sigma=0.05$) on a grid of degradations expected in real experiments. Namely measurement noise ($\gamma\in\{0.1,0.2\}$), connectome errors as false positives ($+400\%$ random null edges, $5\times$ inflated graph) and false negatives ($-20\%$, $-50\%$ edges removed), partial temporal sampling ($1/5$ frames), partial spatial sampling ($10\%$, $20\%$ neurons hidden), and unknown stimulus jointly recovered with a SIREN network (Appendix \ref{app:SIREN}) (Table~\ref{tab:cv_cross_noise}). Activity prediction is robust across all conditions (one-step $r\geq0.95$ except $1/5$ frames at $0.86$). Parameter recovery is most resilient to false-positive edges. The GNN correctly identifies the $80\%$ of inflated edges as zero-weight and recovers the true $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ at $R^2=0.96$ even at $5\times$ the true edge count. It degrades under $-20\%$ edge removal ($R^2_{\hat{W}}=0.78$, rollout $r=0.99$), but breaks down at $-50\%$ removal ($R^2_{\hat{W}}=0.19$, rollout $r=0.56$). When half the true edges are missing from the prior, the remaining edges cannot absorb their contribution. Measurement noise primarily corrupts $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ ($R^2=0.64$ at $\gamma=0.1$, $0.39$ at $\gamma=0.2$) while leaving rollout largely intact. Partial observation of frames or neurons preserves $\tau$ recovery ($R^2_{\hat\tau}\geq0.83$) but undermines $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$. As a first attempt to close this gap, we couple the GNN with an Instant\nobreakdash-NGP coordinate network~\citep{muller_instant_2022} that learns to fill in the unobserved neuron activity. Preliminary results recover part of the lost $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ signal but do not yet match the fully observed baseline (Appendix~\ref{app:NGP}). When the input stimulus is itself hidden, we couple the GNN with a SIREN coordinate network~\citep{sitzmann_implicit_2020} that learns the photoreceptor drive jointly with the dynamics (Fig: \ref{fig:stim_rollout_inr}, Appendix \ref{app:SIREN}). The recovered stimulus matches ground truth (rollout $r=0.92$ over $64{,}000 frames)$, while $\hat{\boldsymbol{\tau}}$ ($R^2=0.93$) and $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ ($R^2=0.81$) remain close to ground truth values. Note that the stimulus is not truly unobserved. It is largely present in the activity of the $1{,}736$ retinal photoreceptors (R1--R8 across $217$ columns), which themselves also
receive feedback from downstream neurons. The task is therefore one of
disentangling the feedforward stimulus drive from the recurrent feedback, rather than recovering an unknown input from scratch.

\paragraph{GNN dynamical system inference overcomes false connectivity measurements}

Electron microscopy based reconstruction of brain wiring requires nanometer precision for accurate synaptic resolution of network connectivity. Precision limits of slice alignment and reconstruction errors create false measurements of connectivity, for instance when neuron identity of neurites and synapses is confused among tightly clustered neurons. In such instances, a synaptic connection may be attributed to the wrong pair of neurons.

To address the question whether the GNN's ability to infer the groundtruth system would be impacted by connection measurement errors, we induced false positive connectivity into the graph: Based on spatial proximity (within $6 \mu$m, approximately one-column in the fly visual system) of synapse clouds in the FlyWire anatomical coordinates, we estimated edges that were realistically confusable and included them into the connectivity measurement. The resulting connectivity graph augments the FlyWire-based  connectome with realistic zero-weight edges \comment{how would we know in practice that we need to give them zero weight?}, increasing edge-count from $327,358$ to $2,418,403$. This case represents the most extreme case: All edges that are realistically confusable are confused. In reality, the set of confused edges would likely be a random subsample of the full set of confusable edges.

We again generated simulated data from the flyvis ODE with neuronal noise ($\sigma=0.05$) on this zero-edge augmented, FlyWire-based connectome and trained the GNN and known-ODE control to recover the dynamical system (2,418,403 edges; Table~\ref{tab:hybrid_recovery}). The GNN accurately predicts rollouts and system parameters \comment{please UPDATE parameters}, again on par with the known-ODE model. These results suggest that false positive connectivity induced by measurement uncertainty and errors can be overcome with our system identification framework.

\section{Video stimulus in simulations}

Visual inputs are derived from the DAVIS-2017 dataset

\paragraph{Null stimulus injection.}
For the simulation conditions reported in the main results, we prepend
a zero-stimulus window to every sequence (see \cref{fig:simulations}
for an example trace, in which the null window is highlighted in

\section{Evaluation metrics}
\label{app:metrics}

We define here the four metrics reported throughout the paper: the
one-step and auto-regressive rollout Pearson~$r$ on neural activity,
and the parameter-recovery $R^2$ on the synaptic weights
$\hat{\mathbf{W}}$, the membrane time constants
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\tau}}$, and the resting potentials
$\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}$.

\paragraph{One-step Pearson $r$.}
On a held-out trajectory of $T$ frames, at every time step
$t = 0, \ldots, T-2$ the model is fed the ground-truth voltage
$\tilde v(t)$ together with the stimulus $I(t)$ and predicts the next
state $\hat v(t+1)$. For each neuron $i$ we compute the Pearson
correlation between the predicted and the ground-truth trace,
\begin{equation}
r_i^{\mathrm{1\text{-}step}} = \mathrm{corr}\!\big(\hat v_i(\,\cdot\,),\,\tilde v_i(\,\cdot\,)\big)
\end{equation}
Neurons whose predicted or ground-truth trace is flat
($\sigma < 10^{-8}$) are dropped. The per-neuron correlations are
then pooled in Fisher-$z$ space,
\begin{equation}
    \bar z \;=\; \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\mathrm{arctanh}\,r_i,
    \qquad
    \bar r \;=\; \tanh\bar z,
    \label{eq:fisher_pool}
\end{equation}
and we report $\bar r \pm (r_+ - r_-)/2$ with
$r_\pm = \tanh(\bar z \pm \sigma_z)$ and $\sigma_z$ the standard
deviation of $\mathrm{arctanh}\,r_i$ across the $N$ neurons.

\paragraph{Auto-regressive rollout Pearson $r$.}
For the rollout the model receives only the initial voltage
$\tilde v(0)$ and the full stimulus stream
$\{I(0),\ldots,I(T-1)\}$; from $t = 1$ onwards it is fed back its own
prediction $\hat v(t-1)$ to produce $\hat v(t)$, with no resetting to
ground truth. The trajectory length is $T = 8{,}000$ frames
($160$~s at $\Delta t = 20$~ms) for every flyvis evaluation. The
rollout trace is compared against the noise-free ground truth
$\tilde v_i(\cdot)$ on a per-neuron basis, and the Fisher-$z$ pooling
of Eq.~\ref{eq:fisher_pool} is applied unchanged.

\paragraph{Synaptic-weight identity-line $R^2$.}
The recovery of the connectome is quantified by the
Nash--Sutcliffe efficiency on the edge weights,
\begin{equation}
    R^2_{\widehat{\mathbf{W}}}
        \;=\; 1 \;-\;
        \frac{\frac{1}{|\mathcal{E}|}\sum_{(i,j)\in\mathcal{E}}
              \big(W_{ij} - \widehat{W}_{ij}\big)^2}
             {\mathrm{Var}_{(i,j)}\!\big(W_{ij}\big)},
    \label{eq:r2_identity}
\end{equation}
pooled over all edges $\mathcal{E}$ of the prior graph. The numerator
is the raw mean squared error, so under a linear miscalibration
$\widehat{W}_{ij} = a\,W_{ij} + b$ both a wrong slope $a \neq 1$
\emph{and} a non-zero offset $b$ inflate the residual: the metric
penalises noise, scale, and bias errors. The range is
$(-\infty, 1]$, with $R^2 = 1$ requiring $\widehat{W}_{ij} = W_{ij}$
exactly. We additionally report the diagnostic slope $a$ from a
linear fit of $\widehat{W}$ against $W$, which disambiguates scale
miscalibration from random scatter when $R^2$ is low (e.g.\
Fig.~\ref{fig:known_ode_params_3col_noise_comparison}). For models
trained with extra null edges (e.g.\ the $+400\%$ condition) the sum
runs over the augmented graph, and recovering
$\widehat{W}_{ij} \approx 0$ on the null edges is part of the
reported number. No outlier filtering is applied to
$R^2_{\widehat{\mathbf{W}}}$.

\paragraph{Parameter $R^2$ for $\hat{\boldsymbol{\tau}}$ and
$\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}$ with outlier filtering.}
For the per-neuron parameters $\tau_i$ and $\vrest_i$ we use the same
identity-line statistic of Eq.~\ref{eq:r2_identity}, restricted to an
inlier set. A neuron $i$ is flagged as an outlier when its absolute
residual exceeds a hard, parameter-specific threshold,
\begin{equation}
    |\hat\theta_i - \theta_i| \;>\; \delta_\theta,
    \qquad
    \delta_\tau = 0.1,\;\;
    \delta_{\vrest} = 0.2,
    \label{eq:outlier_threshold}
\end{equation}
in the same units as the corresponding ground-truth parameter. The
thresholds are fixed once and held constant across every condition in
the paper, so that outlier counts are directly comparable across rows
of any cross-noise table. The filtering is motivated by
how $\tau$ and $\vrest$ are extracted: both are read off from the
slope and offset of the learned function
$f_\theta$ around the neuron's working point, and a small number of
inactive or saturated neurons can produce extreme estimates that
would otherwise dominate the variance in
Eq.~\ref{eq:r2_identity}. The reported $R^2_{\hat{\boldsymbol{\tau}}}$
and $R^2_{\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}}$ are therefore
inlier-set values, with the outlier fraction
$n_{\text{out}}/n_{\text{total}}$ (in percent) in parentheses next to
each value, e.g.\ $0.94 \pm 0.01\,(3.3)$ in
Table~\ref{tab:cv_cross_noise}.
\paragraph{Cross-validation aggregation.}
For five-fold CV results, every metric above is computed independently
in each fold; the table value is the mean across folds and the
$\pm$ figure is one standard deviation across folds. Pearson~$r$
values are averaged in Fisher-$z$ space across folds as well
(Eq.~\ref{eq:fisher_pool}, with the $N$ now being the total number of
finite per-neuron entries pooled over all folds).


\section{Degeneracy of the inverse problem}
\label{app:degeneracy}


% FROM RESULTS:
% In each case, the model almost perfectly predicts the one-step update and is able to perform an auto-regressive rollout on held out stimuli for 8000 steps. Parameter recovery improves upon the addition of a small amount of noise $\sigma=0.05$ and the recovery is perfect at the highest level of $\sigma=0.5$. This is due to a continuous degeneracy in the parameter space of the FlyVis ODE model simulated on naturalistic videos, that is, there is a \emph{degenerate subspace} in parameter space along which different ODEs with the same initial conditions produce the exact same solution. The chosen simulation parameters $(\tau_i, W_{ij}, \vrest_i)$ comprise one point in the degenerate subspace from which we generate a training dataset $\{v_i(t) | \ t \in [0, T]\}$ but gradient descent with random initialization of parameters can converge to any point in this space. Concretely, as a toy example, the parameters $\theta_1, \theta_2$ of the ODE $\dot{x} = -(\theta_1 + \theta_2)x, \ x(0) = 0$, cannot be uniquely determined from the solution $x(t)$ alone, but the combination $\theta_1 + \theta_2$ can be. To understand the degenerate space in equation \ref{eq:simulated_update}, first we observe that while the equation is nonlinear in $v(t)$, it is linear in the parameters $\theta_\alpha := (\tau_i, W_{ij}, \vrest_i)$, and therefore the problem of determining the parameters from given training data $v(t)$ can be written as $A\theta = b$, where $A$ is $(NT - 1) \times (2N + N_E)$ design matrix and $b$ is a vector of length $NT-1$. With this formulation, it is easy to see that if the training data contained a neuron $i$ that was always at its resting potential, \ie, $v_i(t) = \vrest_i \ \forall \ t$, then it is impossible to identify $\tau_i$ because the column multiplying $\tau_i$ in $A$ is identically 0. However, upon the addition of model noise, the stochastic dynamics decreases the likelihood of finding such a neuron. Similarly, if a neuron is in a constantly excited state the training data for that neuron would allow us to probe its presynaptic weights accurately but we would be underpowered to determine $\tau$ and $\vrest$. We do not rule out the possibility that there exists a family of stimuli, perhaps uncorrelated white noise, that would allow for perfect recovery of parameters in the absence of noise. This baseline helps demonstrate that the flyvis in the degenerate subspace can produce perfect one-step predictions and perfect rollouts but fail to recover the true parameters of the system. Recall that the FlyVis stochastic dynamical system in \ref{eq:simulated_update}, in the absence of noise ($\sigma=0$), takes the form:
\begin{equation}
    \tau_i\frac{dv_i(t)}{dt} = -v_i(t) + \vrest_i
+ \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i} W_{ij}\,\text{ReLU}\!\big(v_j(t)\big)
+ I_i(t) \label{eq:flyvis-app-noise-free-ode}
\end{equation}
Our goal is to infer the circuit parameters $(\tau_i, \vrest_i, W_{ij})$ given a solution of the ODE $\tilde{v}_i(t)$ and the stimulus $I_i(t)$ sampled at $T$ discrete points $t=0, \cdots T-1$. The ODE parameters can be decomposed into a separate set for each neuron $i$, namely, $\theta_{i\alpha} := (\tau_i, \vrest_i, W_{ij})$ is a vector of length $2 + d_i$ indexed by $\alpha$, where $\mathcal{N}_i$ is the set of presynaptic partners of neuron $i$ and $d_i := |\mathcal{N}_i|$ is the in-degree. We assume that the solution $\tilde{v}_i(t)$ was generated by a parameter set $\theta$ and solving the inverse problem gives us an inferred set $\hat{\theta}$. We show here that, in the absence of noise, the parameter determination is ill-posed and that there are \emph{continuous} families of learned $\hat{\theta}$ consistent with the given $\tilde{v}_i$ and $I_i$.
% We show here that there are \emph{continuous} deformations in parameter space that lead to equivalent solutions, therefore, the system does not admit a unique solution making the inverse problem ill-posed.

\Cref{eq:flyvis-app-noise-free-ode} can be rewritten, for the purposes of parameter inference
\begin{equation}
    -\tau_i \dot{\tilde{v}}_i + \vrest_i + \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}_i} W_{ij}h_j(t) = \tilde{v}_i(t) - I_i(t)
    \label{eq:flyvis-linear-rewrite}
\end{equation}
where $h_j(t) := \relu(\tilde{v}_j(t))$ are the pre-synaptic activities.
Observe that while the ODE is non-linear in the state variable $v$ it is
\emph{linear} in the circuit parameters $\theta_{i\alpha}$ defined above. So, 
we can rewrite Eq.~\ref{eq:flyvis-linear-rewrite} as a separate
linear system to solve for each neuron $i$,
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{A}_i \, \boldsymbol{\theta}_i = \mathbf{b}_i,
    \label{eq:parameter-linear-system}
\end{equation}
in terms of the design matrix $\mathbf{A}_i$ of dimension $T \times (2 + d_i)$,
and the vectors $\boldsymbol{\theta}_i$ of dimension $2 + d_i$ and
$\mathbf{b}_i$ of dimension $T$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{A}_i := \begin{pmatrix}
        -\dot{\tilde{v}}_i(0) & 1 & h_{j_1}(0) & h_{j_2}(0) & \cdots  \\
        -\dot{\tilde{v}}_i(1) & 1 & h_{j_1}(1) & h_{j_2}(1) & \cdots  \\
        \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots &
    \end{pmatrix},\quad
    \boldsymbol{\theta}_i := \begin{pmatrix}
        \tau_i \\ \vrest_i \\ W_{ij_1} \\ W_{ij_2} \\ \vdots
    \end{pmatrix},\quad
    \mathbf{b}_i := \begin{pmatrix}
        \tilde{v}_i(0) - I_i(0) \\
        \tilde{v}_i(1) - I_i(1) \\
        \vdots
    \end{pmatrix}
    \label{eq:explicit-linear-system}
\end{equation}
where $j_1, j_2, \ldots$ are the pre-synaptic neurons in $\mathcal{N}_i$.
We emphasise that this linear system is not a linearisation or approximation
but a property of the voltage-based firing-rate models used in flyvis
\citep{Lappalainen2024-tq}.

The linear system Eq.~\ref{eq:parameter-linear-system} can be solved to
infer the simulation parameters $\theta_{i\alpha}$. If the column space is
not full rank the system admits a continuous degeneracy defined by the
kernel $\mathrm{ker}(\mathbf{A}_i)$. In particular, if there exists a vector
$\boldsymbol{\eta}_i$ satisfying $\mathbf{A}_i \, \boldsymbol{\eta}_i =
\mathbf{0}$ for every $i$, then the family of parameters
$\boldsymbol{\theta}'_i = \boldsymbol{\theta}_i + \lambda \,
\boldsymbol{\eta}_i$ are solutions to the inverse problem for all $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$.
We solve the linear system using least squares, using float64 arithmetic,
column normalization of $\mathbf{A}_i$. We compute the singular value decomposition
of the $(2+d_i) \times (2+d_i)$ Gram matrix $\mathbf{A}_i^T\mathbf{A}_i$

This serves to explain why parameter recovery from neural activity in the

\subsubsection*{$\vrest$ recovery}

Other scenarios make it impossible to unambiguously recover $\vrest$. 
Let's suppose two neurons $i$ and $j$ are both at rest, and that $j$ is a pre-synaptic partner of $i$. If all incoming connections to $i$ and $j$ are sub-threshold, except for $j\rightarrow i$ and if $\vrest_j > 0$, then
\begin{equation}
    A_i := \begin{pmatrix}
        0 & 1 & \vrest_j & 0 & \cdots  \\
        0 & 1 & \vrest_j & 0 & \cdots  \\
        \vdots & \vdots & \vdots &\vdots & 
    \end{pmatrix} \text{and} \ \mathbf{A_j} := \begin{pmatrix}
        0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots  \\
        0 & 1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots  \\
        \vdots & \vdots & \vdots &\vdots & 
    \end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
We now have a family of solutions where $\hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_j = \vrest_j $ and $\hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_i + \vrest_j\hat{W}_{ij} = \vrest_j$. \subsection{Training and agentic HPO.}


The optimised networks of the GNN are $g_\phi$ and $f_\theta$ of
Eq.~\ref{eq:gnn_ct}, both modelled as MLPs (ReLU activation, hidden
dimension $=80$, $3$ layers, output size $=1$). Other learnables are
the two-dimensional latent vector $\mathbf{a}_i$ associated with each
neuron and the connectivity matrix $\hat{\mathbf W}$. We implemented
the GNNs in PyTorch~Geometric \citep{fey_fast_2019} and used Adam
gradient descent with separate parameter-group learning rates
$\text{lr}_W\!=\!9{\cdot}10^{-4}$ (connectivity),
$\text{lr}\!=\!1.8{\cdot}10^{-3}$ ($g_\phi$, $f_\theta$),
$\text{lr}_\text{emb}\!=\!2.3{\cdot}10^{-3}$ (embeddings), batch size
$4$, $1$ nominal epoch with
\texttt{data\_augmentation\_loop}$=500$ over $64\,000$ training
frames (${\approx}5{\cdot}10^{5}$ to $2{\cdot}10^{6}$ gradient steps
across conditions). Time points are sampled uniformly at random at
each step.

The agentic loop of Appendix~\ref{app:agent} was run independently
for each of the six conditions of Table~\ref{tab:unified_hp}
($\sim$100 iterations of 4 parallel slots per condition,
single-parameter mutations, 4-seed robustness validation of every
candidate winner). The exploration converged on a consistent picture
of the role played by each term of Eq.~\ref{eq:loss_full}. The
$\ell_1$ prior on $\hat{\mathbf W}$ ($\gamma_0$) is the only
important regulariser: setting $\gamma_0\!=\!0$ leaves rollouts
nearly unchanged ($r\!=\!0.999$) but collapses $R^2_{\hat W}$ from
$0.97$ to $0.39$, a direct manifestation of the degeneracy of
Appendix~\ref{app:degeneracy}. The $\ell_2$ counterpart $\gamma_1$
provides no measurable benefit. Among the $g_\phi$ priors, $\mu_1$
prevents the trivial collapse $g_\phi\!\equiv\!0$ and was selected in
$[750, 2000]$ across conditions; $\mu_2$ stabilises at $0.9$ for the
$\sigma=0.05$ sub-conditions and is driven to 0 only in the noise-free condition;
the weight-$\ell_1$ $\lambda_1$ takes a distinct value in the
noise-free condition ($\lambda_1\!=\!0.14$, $+0.054$ on
$R^2_{\hat W}$) versus a uniform $0.28$ across the $\sigma=0.05$ 
sub-conditions. The two $f_\theta$ priors split by noise regime:
$\lambda_0$ ($\ell_1$) is needed at the $\sigma=0.05$  working point
($\lambda_0\!=\!0.05$, identical across the four $\sigma=0.05$ 
sub-conditions) but is driven to $0$ in the noise-free and $\sigma=0.5$ 
extremes; $\lambda_2$ ($\ell_2$) acts as the magnitude anchor of
$\hat{\mathbf W}$ and stays at $10^{-3}$ for every noisy condition.

A clean cross-condition pattern emerged: the four $\sigma{=}0.05$
sub-conditions share an identical regulariser block, with the
noise-free and $\sigma{=}0.5$ winners deviating at opposite poles.
The $\sigma{=}0.05$ winner sits between the two extremes, is the
closest single point to the per-coefficient medians of
Table~\ref{tab:unified_hp}, and is the only one validated under
12-seed robustness with zero catastrophic failures; we therefore
retain it unchanged for every GNN result reported in the paper,
including the FlyWire and degraded-connectome variants.

\subsection{Joint stimulus recovery with SIREN.}
\label{app:SIREN}
The unkown-stimulus results of Table ~\ref{tab:cv_cross_noise} is obtained by hiding the visual stimulus over the $1{,}736$ retina neurons. To test whether the visual input $I_i(t)$ entering Eq.~\ref{eq:known_ode} can itself be recovered from activity alone, we extend the GNN with an implicit neural representation of the stimulus field $\hat I^*(t,x,y)$, modelled as a coordinate-based
MLP with sine activations \citep[SIREN,][input size~$=3$,
hidden dimension~$=2048$, $4$ hidden layers, output size~$=1$,
$\omega_0\!=\!4096$, outermost-linear head]{sitzmann_implicit_2020}.
The SIREN is queried at the $1\,736$ photoreceptor positions
$(x_i,y_i)$ at every time step and substitutes for $I_i(t)$ in the
GNN forward pass. The GNN and the SIREN are jointly optimised on the
same prediction loss but with strongly asymmetric learning rates
and a two-phase ``alternate-training'' schedule: epoch~$0$ uses the
full GNN learning rates above with
$\text{lr}_\text{SIREN}\!=\!10^{-8}$; epochs~$1$–$2$ scale the GNN
learning rates by $0.05$ while $\text{lr}_\text{SIREN}$ is held
constant, letting the SIREN catch up on a stable graph backbone.


\subsection{Joint hidden-activity recovery with Instant-NGP}
\label{app:NGP}

The hidden-neuron results of Table~\ref{tab:cv_cross_noise} are obtained by
hiding $10\%$ or $20\%$ of the $12{,}005$ non-retinal neurons and asking
the GNN to recover its parameters without ever seeing those voltage
traces. The unobserved voltages are filled in by a coordinate-based
network trained jointly with the GNN, in the same spirit as the
SIREN-based stimulus recovery of Appendix~\ref{app:SIREN}
\citep{sitzmann_implicit_2020}, but with an Instant-NGP-style
multi-resolution feature grid \citep{muller_instant_2022} in place of the sine MLP
. Because the input is one-dimensional (time), each level of the grid is an embedding of size  $(R_\ell+1) \times d$ with linear interpolation between neighbouring cells. Locality is what motivates this architecture over a SIREN:
updating the prediction for one frame modifies only a small
neighbourhood of grid cells, avoiding the global coupling we observed
when training a time-only SIREN over $64{,}000$ frames. The grid
features are decoded by an MLP into hidden-neuron voltages and a set of
anchor voltages supervised against ground truth on a held-out subset of
visible neurons; the anchor term is necessary to give the time field a
stable temporal scaffold, without which the hidden-neuron output
collapses onto a low-frequency mean.

\paragraph{Spatio-temporal architecture.}
We add a 2-D companion grid indexed by the per-neuron retinotopic
position $(u_i, v_i)$ — the columnar coordinates flyvis attaches to
every node — mapped to Cartesian $(x_i, y_i) = (u_i + 0.5\,v_i,\
v_i\sqrt{3}/2)$ following the hex convention used elsewhere in the
paper. Temporal and spatial grid features are concatenated with the
GNN's learned latent $\mathbf{a}_i$ and decoded by a shared MLP into a
per-neuron scalar voltage; feeding $\mathbf{a}_i$ directly into the
decoder lets the MLP combine it non-linearly with the spatio-temporal
features and makes per-neuron identity available to the NGP
throughout the network. The latent $\mathbf{a}_i$ of a hidden neuron
is itself learned through $g_\phi$: when its NGP-imputed voltage is
sent through outgoing edges to a visible postsynaptic neighbour, the
visible neuron's prediction loss back-propagates through $g_\phi$ to
update $\mathbf{a}_i$, so cell-type structure is inherited from
neighbouring observed neurons of the same type. The ground-truth cell
type is never an input — only the GNN's co-trained latent is fed in,
so cell type remains an unknown of the inverse problem.

\paragraph{Training schedule.}
The GNN and the NGP are co-trained in two phases. During warmup (the
first third of training) hidden voltages are silenced ($v_i = 0$),
so the GNN learns $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ on the visible $90\%$ identically
to a no-NGP run, while the NGP backbone trains via an anchor loss on
a held-out subset of visible neurons. At the end of warmup the
injection switches on hard ($v_i \leftarrow
\mathrm{NGP}(t, x_i, y_i, \mathbf{a}_i)$, with no scalar
ramp). To absorb the resulting input-distribution shift in the GNN
forward, the learning rates of $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$, $f_\theta$ and
$g_\phi$ are damped by a factor of $100$ at the injection switch and
recover linearly back to nominal over an absorption window of roughly
$20\%$ of training; the embedding $\mathbf{a}_i$ and the NGP weights
stay at full learning rate throughout. We deliberately do not add a
self-consistency term between $\mathrm{GNN}(v_h{=}0)_h$ and
$\mathrm{NGP}(t)_h$: an earlier variant of this loss collapsed onto
the trivial zero fixed point, with the consistency residual reducing
to an $L_2$ ridge $\lVert\mathrm{NGP}\rVert$ once the GNN was
consistent with $v_h{=}0$, and pinning the hidden NGP output at zero
throughout training. NGP-hidden is therefore supervised only
through (i) the shared backbone via the anchor loss and (ii)
back-propagation through the injection during phase~2 — both
gradients point away from zero.

This extension is still in development. The architectural and
training-schedule changes described above remove the zero-attractor
failure mode of an earlier temporal-only variant, but the resulting
joint scheme has not yet produced a measurable improvement in
$R^2_{\hat{\mathbf{W}}}$ recovery over the no-NGP baseline on the
$10\%$ / $20\%$ hidden conditions of Table~\ref{tab:cv_cross_noise}.
Final numerical results will be reported in follow-up work.

\section{Agent-in-the-loop parameter optimisation}
\label{app:agent}

The optimisation space combining simulation parameters (noise $\sigma$,
connectivity constraints, $n_{\mathrm{frames}}$) and machine learning parameters
(e.g.\ for GNN, $\lambda_0,\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\lambda_3,\gamma_0,\gamma_1,\mu_0,\mu_1,\mu_2$, learning rates, etc.) is too large for grid search. We instead deploy a closed loop
coupling three components: a GNN training engine, a large language model
agent (Claude Code, Anthropic), and an explicit structured summary. The system
operates through text file exchange. The instruction file defines the inverse
problem, the quantitative score objective, the metrics to track, the parameter
ranges, and the logging format. The structured summary maintains accumulated
understanding in two tiers: established principles and open questions that
persist across blocks, and a working record of current hypotheses and recent
iterations. A configuration file is edited by the agent to set the next
experiment; a results file is written by the experiment engine and read by
the agent.

The primary objective of the agentic loop is to optimise hyperparameters
against a quantitative score ($R^2_{\mathbf{W}}$ or rollout accuracy). But we
also constrain the agent to hypothesis-driven protocol: a testable
hypothesis must be stated before each experiment, and only experimental
outcomes can validate or falsify it. The next experiment is therefore
selected by the hypothesis the agent currently seeks to test rather than by a
numerical exploration/exploitation trade-off.

Experiments are organised into blocks of 8-16 iterations (multiples of 4 to
match the parallel batch size). The agent does not start from scratch, it is initialised with a configuration established from prior works. The simulation
regime of each block can be specified by the user in the instruction file or
chosen by the agent. Within a block, the agent explores GNN training
parameters guided by its current hypothesis. To make the protocol robust, we
run $4$ experiments in parallel and submit the resulting batch of training
outcomes to the agent. Reasoning over a batch rather than a single trial
sharpens both confirmation and falsification. The structured summary also
serves as an explicit long-term memory, overcoming the finite context window
of the agent and enabling accumulation across hundreds of iterations. With
this protocol, convergence to the optimal configuration is reached within
$\sim 100$ GNN training experiments sweeping sometimes more than 10 parameters.

We initially adopted UCB tree search as in AlphaEvolve and FunSearch
\citep{romera-paredes_mathematical_2024,novikov_alphaevolve_2025}, where
parameter mutations are sampled from a tree balancing exploitation and
exploration. Once the hypothesis-driven constraint was introduced, we
found that the agent stopped consulting the UCB scores: parent selection and
parameter mutations were driven entirely by hypotheses formulated from the
structured summary and recent experimental results. We accordingly removed
the UCB component, which had become redundant.

\subsection{Known-ODE} \label{app:known_ode}
The known-ODE model fixes the functional form of the dynamics to the ground-truth flyvis equation and learns only the biophysical parameters and the connectivity:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\tau}_i\,\frac{d\hat{v}_i(t)}{dt}
= -\hat{v}_i(t) + \hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_i
+ \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i} \hat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}\,\mathrm{ReLU}\!\big(v_j(t)\big)
+ I_i(t).
\label{eq:known_ode}
\end{equation}
The learnable parameters are the per-edge weights $\hat{\mathbf{W}}_{ij}$, the per-neuron time constants $\hat{\tau}_i = \mathrm{softplus}(\tilde{\tau}_i)$ (with $\tilde{\tau}_i\in\mathbb{R}$ the underlying optimized parameter), and the per-neuron resting potentials $\hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_i$. During training, inputs $I_i(t)$, adjacency $\mathcal{N}_i$, and activity $v_i(t)$ are given; $\hat{\mathbf{W}}, \tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}, \hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}$ are optimized by minimizing the difference between simulator $y_i(t)=dv_i(t)/dt$ and known-ODE predictions $\hat{y}_i(t)=d\hat{v}_i(t)/dt$,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}} = \sum_{i,t}\|\hat{y}_i(t) - y_i(t)\|_2.
\label{eq:known_ode_loss_pred}
\end{equation}
Even with the ODE structure fixed, recovery of $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ from voltage traces remains non-unique (see Appendix~\ref{app:degeneracy}). To constrain the solution space, we added regularization terms to $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}
+ \gamma_0\|\hat{\mathbf{W}}\|_1 + \gamma_1\|\hat{\mathbf{W}}\|_2
+ \eta_0\|\tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}\|_1 + \eta_1\|\tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}\|_2
+ \eta_2\|\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}\|_1 + \eta_3\|\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}\|_2.
\label{eq:known_ode_loss}
\end{equation}
The $\ell_1$ term $\gamma_0$ on $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ promotes sparse effective connectivity, while the $\ell_2$ term $\gamma_1$ penalizes large synaptic weights. The $\ell_1$ penalty $\eta_0$ on $\tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}$ shrinks the time constants toward the softplus origin, while the $\ell_2$ penalty $\eta_1$ prevents drift. The $\ell_1$ penalty $\eta_2$ on $\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}$ promotes sparse resting potentials, while the $\ell_2$ penalty $\eta_3$ penalizes large biases. Eq.~\ref{eq:known_ode_loss} is a general form: the coefficients $\gamma_0,\gamma_1,\eta_0,\eta_1,\eta_2,\eta_3$ are hyperparameters tuned via the agentic optimization procedure described in Appendix \ref{app:agent} , which can set a subset of them to zero. In practice we retain a single regularization profile for every
Known-ODE result reported in the paper, namely the best paremeters found by the
agentic exploration run on noise-free data (Appendix~\ref{app:agent}):
$\gamma_0=10^{-5}$ ($\ell_1$ on $\hat{\mathbf W}$),
$\eta_1=3\!\times\!10^{-3}$ ($\ell_2$ on $\tilde{\boldsymbol\tau}$),
$\eta_3=10^{-3}$ ($\ell_2$ on $\hat{\mathbf V}^{\mathrm{rest}}$), with
the remaining three coefficients pinned to zero. The same coefficients
are applied unchanged to the noisy flyvis conditions and to the
FlyWireRF runs.

\subsection{MLP baseline}

We used a plain MLP to predict the time derivative of neural activity based on the input stimulus and past activity. We begin with the noise free system, where $\sigma = 0$ in \ref{eq:simulated_update}, and use the model
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dv}{dt} = \mathrm{MLP}(s(t), v(t-1))\, .
\end{equation}

We train this model to minimize the MSE loss between the predicted and ground truth $dv/dt$. We observed that by varying the hyperparameters of the MLP we were able to accurately capture the 1-step evolution of neural activity. However, we failed to achieve a stable roll out on validation data beyond a few hundred time steps. When measuring the MSE on an 8000 step rollout on validation data we observed large (RMSE $> 1.0$) low frequency oscillations that did not go to zero even after significant increases to the training time. To address this, we added ResNet-style skip connections, which enable the network to easily model the low frequency oscillations in the MSE that were missed by the plain MLP. This intervention helps us learn a generalizable model for neural activity evolution and we are able to generate autoregressive rollouts of over 8000 steps on the held-out test data with Pearson's $r > 0.90$ (data not shown here). However, 
as shown in Table \ref{tab:cv_baselines_sym}.

We found that the addition of stochastic dynamics via an model noise term as in Eq. \ref{eq:simulated_update} with $\sigma > 0$ hurts the ability of the MLP to learn the underlying drift dynamics. At the high noise level of $\sigma=0.5$ the activity at the next time step is dominated by the noise compared to the drift term that is mostly close to zero. To compensate for this, we trained the MLP model to make predictions of the next $R$ time steps and minimized the MSE loss over $t+1, t+2, \cdots, t+R$. The $R$ step rollout training helps average out the uncorrelated noise and the model is able to learn the correct drift dynamics. Additionally, we initialized the weights and biases of the final layer of the MLP to zero, since this starts the optimization at $dv_i/dt = 0$ generating a constant and bounded rollout. We still observe a performance gap between the noise free conditions and those with noise added. It may be possible to bridge this gap with more input data that would allow for further averaging out of the noise term, or other architectural and training innovations. 

\subsection{EED model}

To improve the performance of the MLP we introduce a bottleneck architecture that we call the Encoder-Evolver-Decoder model. This can be viewed as a state space model where the internal dynamics are described by an evolving latent representation, which is then read out by the measured neurons. We were motivated to consider this architecture by the observation that the neural activity matrix can be accurately reconstructed using top $K$ singular values for $K \ll N$ \comment{TODO: add reconstruction error vs K plot or just numbers inline}. The model architecture, depicted in Figure \ref{fig:baseline_arch}C, consists of an auto-encoder that projects to/reconstructs the observed neural activity from a lower dimensional latent space. The visual stimulus is also encoded into a smaller representation and the next latent state is a function of the stimulus latent and the activity latent. Similar to the MLP baseline architecture above we added linear skip connections to each of the encoder, decoder, evolver and stimulus encoder MLPs.
