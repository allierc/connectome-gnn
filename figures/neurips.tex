\section{Introduction}

Building mechanistic, parameter-resolved models of neural circuits has been a long-standing goal of neuroscience.
Small circuits have been modeled this way for decades, \citepos{hodgkin_quantitative_1952} squid axon to the stomatogastric ganlion \citep{marder_multiple_2011}.
Scaling these methods up has proven much harder: top-down, hand-specified large-scale simulations have struggled to be predictive of circuit function \citep{markram_reconstruction_2015,fregnac_neuroscience_2014}.
Synapse-resolution connectomes that are paired with neural activity recordings make data-constrained mechanistic models conceivable in principle \citep{the_microns_consortium_functional_2025}.
In practice, however, neither connectivity nor activity will ever be complete or noise free: connectomes carry reconstruction errors and uncertain synaptic weights, and activity recordings cover fractions of neurons with measurement noise.
The practical question is therefore not whether complete data will eventually arrive, but which combination of incomplete connectivity and activity is sufficient to recover circuit mechanism.

Three threads of recent work bound the answer but leave it open.  \emph{Foundation models of neural activity} trained on large-scale recordings achieve impressive cross-stimulus and cross-animal generalization \citep{wang_foundation_2025}, but are explicitly non-mechanistic.
Their parameters do not map to biophysical quantitites, and counterfactual interventions on connectivity or cell types are not interpretable.
\emph{Connectome-constrained models} make real progress toward mechanism but pre-specify the dynamics: a fixed biophysical neuron model on the \emph{C. elegans} connectome \citep{mi_connectome-constrained_2021} or on the fly visual system \citep{lappalainen_connectome-constrained_2024}, or a linear effective-connectivity model on the fly brain \cite{pospisil_fly_2024}.
\emph{In silico identifiability studies} probe the inverse problem directly \citep{yu_discovering_2025,lueckmann_discovering_2026}, but at small scale or without validating recovered connectivity.  None of these directly characterize when mechanistic recovery is feasible at circuit scale, and none offers a flexible inference method that does not pre-specify the dynamical equation form.

Because no current experimental dataset provides complete synapse-resolution connectivity together with dense activity recordings from the same animal, we use \citepos{lappalainen_connectome-constrained_2024} connectome-constrained Flyvis simulator as a proxy for paired data.
The simulator is a biologically plausible continuous-time dynamical system on the real fly connectome, driven by naturalistic video, with known per-neuron and per-synapse parameters.  We explore this system at different scales between $13$K and $50$K neurons, resulting in $0.4$M to $5$M edges, and heterogeneous wiring that we extracted from the FlyWire connectome \citep{dorkenwald_neuronal_2024,schlegel_whole-brain_2024} to test the convolutional assumption of the original model and connectivity-uncertainty.
On this in silico ground truth, we evaluate a range of inference methods---ML sequence models, and oracle baseline that knows the true ordinary differential equation (ODE), and our primary contribution: an interpretable continuous-time GNN that does not assume the form of the dynamical equation.
Instead, it learns the per-neuron update function and the per-synapse interaction function as shared MLPs conditioned on a learned per-neuron latent embedding, jointly with effective connectivity weights.
Because the same functional forms must produce the diverse dynamics of 65 cell types, cell-type identity emerges in the latent embedding as a consequence of mechanistic recovery.

Across realistic data degradations, we identify several non-obvious regularities about when recovery is feasible.
First, the GNN recovers the unknown update and synaptic-interaction functions from data alone, on par with a known-ODE oracle.
Cell-type identity emerges unsupervised in the latent embedding space, with clustering accuracy matching that obtained from ground-truth parameters.
Second, the connectome serves well as \emph{binary} structural prior: the GNN tolerates a 400\% inflation of the edge set with random null edges and still recovers true synaptic weights at $R^2=0.96$, while removing 50\% of true edges breaks recovery.
The practically useful connectome is therefore a superset of true edges, and knowing which pairs do \emph{not} connect matters more than precise weights.
Third, activity richness is the binding constraint on identification.
Naturalistic stimulation alone leaves the inverse problem degenerate, and per-neuron noise is what breaks these degeneracies and allows the GNN to match the known-ODE oracle.
We interpret model noise as one instance of the broader requirement that identification needs activity diversity beyond passive sensory drive, whether through intrinsic stochasticity or (by analogy and to be tested) through extrinsic perturbations such as targeted optogenetic stimulation.
Fourth, prediction is not mechanism.
ML sequence baselines reach one-step Pearson~$r\geq0.95$ on activity but cannot be used to probe mechanistic interventions such as ablating 50\% of synaptic connections that both the GNN and the known-ODE oracle naturally permit.
Finally, measurement noise on voltage traces remains the principal open bottleneck: a $\gamma = 0.1$ measurement noise leaves rollouts intact but degrades synaptic-weight recovery from $R^2 = 0.99$ to $0.63$, and we flag this as the central challenge for connectome-inverse methods on real data.

Our contributions are:
(1) an interpretable continuous-time GNN that recovers the per-neuron update function and per-synapse interaction function from connectivity and activity, without assuming the form of the dynamical equation.
Cell-type identity emerges in the learned latent embedding as a structural consequence; the framework extends with a SIREN module to additionally recover unobserved sensory inputs;
(2) a characterization of identifiability across realistic data degradations on a \emph{Drosophila} visual-system simulator, including FlyWire heterogeneous wiring and false-positive connectivity;
(3) an agentic hyperparameter-optimization protocol that yielded a single training configuration robust across conditions; and
(4) the finding that perturbation-driven activity richness, rather than architectural sophistication, is the binding constraint on mechanistic recovery from connectivity and activity.


\section{Related work}

\citet{lueckmann_discovering_2026} establish an in silico testbed for connectome-constrained system identification on a larval zebrafish visuomotor circuit of 36~units, showing that LLM-guided code search can recover effective connectivity when given structural priors.
The setup is closest to ours in spirit, and complementary in scope: their circuit is small enough to admit symbolic search, ours is two orders of magnitude larger and forces a parameterised inference method.
\citet{lappalainen_connectome-constrained_2024} demonstrated that a connectome-constrained recurrent network with a hand-designed ODE on the \emph{Drosophila} visual system can recapitulate plausible single-neuron physiology after training on optical flow.
We use their simulator as in silico ground truth precisely because of this evidence of biological plausibility, and ask the inverse question: can the underlying dynamical system be \emph{recovered} from activity and connectivity, without being told its functional form?

Connectome-constrained inference with a fixed biophysical neuron model has been demonstrated on \emph{C. elegans} calcium data \citep{mi_connectome-constrained_2021} and via linear effective-connectivity from perturbations on the \emph{Drosophila} connectome \citep{pospisil_fly_2024}; both pre-specify the dynamics.
Foundation models of activity \citep{wang_foundation_2025} achieve strong cross-stimulus and cross-animal prediction on $\sim 10^5$ real neurons but do not target mechanism.
Symbolic-regression and equation-discovery approaches \citep{yu_discovering_2025,prasse_predicting_2022} recover dynamical equations from simulated activity but do not validate connectivity weights or learn neuronal heterogeneity.
None of these directly characterises the conditions under which mechanistic recovery from connectivity and activity is feasible.

Our GNN inherits its decomposition principle from \citepos{allier_decomposing_2024,allier_graph_2026} work on random recurrent neural assemblies.
However, recovering the parameters of a biological circuit on a real, sparsely connected, repeated-column connectome is a different problem.
Neural assemblies with random weights do not exhibit the structural degeneracies that columnar visual systems do: when same-type neurons across columns produce time-shifted copies of one another's signals, large families of distinct synaptic weight matrices generate indistinguishable activity.
We characterise these degeneracies analytically (\cref{app:degeneracy}) and find, for instance, that a circuit retaining only 29\% of the original edges produces activity indistinguishable from the full circuit under naturalistic stimulation.
This motivates the methodological additions of this work: a Known-ODE oracle that quantifies the residual gap between data-driven inference and full equation knowledge, an edge-ablation mechanistic test, the identification of per-neuron noise as a perturbation that breaks columnar degeneracies, and an agentic hyperparameter-optimisation procedure that produces a single training configuration robust across conditions of connectivity completeness and activity quality.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\textbf{Flyvis simulations.}\label{sec:simulation}
We simulated neural activity in the fly visual system using Flyvis pretrained models.  Each neuron is modeled as a non-spiking voltage compartment governed by the ODE
\begin{equation}
\tau_i\frac{dv_i(t)}{dt} = -v_i(t) + V_i^{\text{rest}}
+ \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i}W_{ij}\,\text{ReLU}\!\big(v_j(t)\big)
+ I_i(t) + \sigma\,\xi_i(t),
\label{eq:simulated_update}
\end{equation}
where $v_i$ is the neuron membrane voltage, $\tau_i$ and
$V_i^{\mathrm{rest}}$ are cell-type parameters, $W_{ij}$ is the
connectome synaptic weight, and $I_i(t)$ is the visual input. Visual
inputs are derived from the DAVIS-2017 benchmark dataset
\citep{perazzi_benchmark_2016} for annotated
natural videos. A null
stimulus ($I_i(t)=0$) is prepended to every sequence so that neurons
relax toward $V_i^{\mathrm{rest}}$ during the first half of each clip,
which is essential for parameter identifiability
(\cref{app:stimulus}). $\xi_i(t)\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ is
independent Gaussian noise scaled by $\sigma$ (model noise), perturbing
the dynamical trajectory of $v_i(t)$. We additionally apply
measurement Gaussian noise scaled by
$\gamma$ directly to $v_i(t)$ (\cref{fig:stimulus_hexagons,fig:simulations}).  We first restricted the original $721$
retinotopic columns to the central subset of $217$
columns (Flyvis-217), covering the central visual field while substantially reducing memory and runtime requirements during tests and optimization. This simulated recurrent neural network contains $13{,}741$ neurons from $65$ cell types and $434{,}112$ synaptic connections. Ground-truth distributions of the per-neuron parameters $\tau_i$, $V_i^{\mathrm{rest}}$ and per-edge weights $W_{ij}$ across cell types are reported in \cref{fig:ground_truth_distributions}. Next, we expanded the simulations to a hybrid connectome that retains the trained Flyvis dynamical model but inherits per-column wiring heterogeneity and connectivity uncertainty from FlyWire, resulting in networks with $50{,}412$~neurons and up to $9{,}642{,}335$~edges (\cref{app:flywire,tab:hybrid_pipeline_stats}).  We evaluate these networks in two configurations, an 8 column extent central region corresponding to Flyvis-217 (e8 hybrid) and the full eye (FlyWire eye). 


\textbf{Graph neural network model}\label{method:GNN}
We approximate the simulated voltage dynamics by a message-passing GNN \citep{gilmer_neural_2017}
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\hat{v}_i(t)}{dt}
= f_\theta\!\Big(v_i(t),\,\mathbf{a}_i,\,
\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i} \hat{W}_{ij}\,
g_\phi\!\big(v_j(t),\mathbf{a}_j\big)^2,\,
I_i(t)\Big).
\label{eq:gnn_ct}
\end{equation}
Nodes of the GNN correspond to neurons and edges correspond to functional synaptic connections. The GNN learns a latent embedding $\mathbf{a}_i\in\mathbb{R}^2$ for each neuron. The learnable vectors $\mathbf{a}_i\in\mathbb{R}^2$ represent compact latent identity, they can map to cell-type-specific properties (like time constants and nonlinearities). Neuron update $f_\theta=\text{MLP}_0$ and edge message $g_\phi=\text{MLP}_1$ are three-layer perceptrons (width $80$, ReLU, linear output). $g_\phi$ maps presynaptic inputs $v_j$ to nonnegative messages (via squaring) depending on latent embedding $\mathbf{a}_j$, weighted by $\hat{W}_{ij}$. $f_\theta$ processes the postsynaptic voltage $v_i$, aggregated input, and external input $I_i(t)$ to predict $d\hat{v}_i(t)/dt$, depending on $\mathbf{a}_i$. During training, inputs $I_i(t)$, adjacency $\mathcal{N}_i$, and activity $v_i(t)$ are given (\cref{app:gnn-training}). The MLPs, $\hat{W}_{ij}$, and $\mathbf{a}_i$ are optimized by minimizing the difference between simulator $y_i(t)=dv_i(t)/dt$ and GNN predictions $\hat{y}_i(t)=d\hat{v}_i(t)/dt$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}} = \sum_{i,t}\|\hat{y}_i(t) - y_i(t)\|_2
\label{eq:loss_pred}
\end{equation}
Many synaptic weight configurations give rise to identical neural activity, making the recovery of $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ from voltage traces fundamentally non-unique (\cref{app:degeneracy}). To constrain the solution space, we added regularization terms to $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}$
\begin{eqnarray}
\mathcal{L} &=& \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}
+ \lambda_0\|\theta\|_1 + \lambda_1\|\phi\|_1 + \lambda_2\|\theta\|_2 + \lambda_3\|\phi\|_2
+ \gamma_0\|\hat{\mathbf{W}}\|_1 + \gamma_1\|\hat{\mathbf{W}}\|_2 \nonumber\\
&& +\; \mu_0\!\left\|\mathrm{ReLU}\!\left(-\tfrac{\partial\,f_\theta(v,m,\mathbf{a},I)}{\partial m}\right)\right\|_2
+ \mu_1\!\left\|\mathrm{ReLU}\!\left(-\tfrac{\partial\,g_\phi(v,\mathbf{a})}{\partial v}\right)\right\|_2 \nonumber +\; \mu_2\|g_\phi(v_\star,\mathbf{a})-v_\star\|_2.
\label{eq:loss_full}
\end{eqnarray}
The $\ell_1$ penalties $\lambda_0,\lambda_1$ on the MLP weights $\theta$ and $\phi$ promote sparsity, biasing $f_\theta$ and $g_\phi$ toward simpler functions; the $\ell_2$ penalties $\lambda_2,\lambda_3$ prevent weight explosion and stabilize training. The $\ell_1$ term $\gamma_0$ on $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ promotes sparse effective connectivity, while the $\ell_2$ term $\gamma_1$ penalizes large synaptic weights. The monotonicity term $\mu_0$ penalizes negative slopes of $f_\theta$ with respect to its aggregated synaptic input $m=\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i}\hat{W}_{ij}\,g_\phi(v_j,\mathbf{a}_j)$, encoding the prior that the postsynaptic derivative increases with net excitatory drive. The monotonicity term $\mu_1$ penalizes negative slopes of $g_\phi$ with respect to $v$, encoding the prior that synaptic drive increases with presynaptic voltage. The normalization term $\mu_2$ anchors $g_\phi$ at a reference voltage $v_\star$, removing the scale degeneracy between $g_\phi$ and $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ in \cref{eq:gnn_ct}. We tuned all the hyperparameters $\lambda_0,\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\lambda_3,\gamma_0,\gamma_1,\mu_0,\mu_1,\mu_2$ and the MLPs' structure with an agentic optimization procedure (\cref{app:agent}). After training, the circuit parameters are extracted from the GNN as described in \cref{app:extraction}.


\textbf{Benchmark}
We compare GNN results with four baseline models detailed in \cref{app:benchmark}: Known-ODE, which assumes knowledge of the full functional form of the ODE and learns the circuit parameters $\tau, \vrest, W$; \mlpbase, which predicts $dv/dt$ as a function of the current $v(t)$ and $I(t)$ using a 2-layer MLP (640 hidden units) with ResNet-style skip connections; \eedbase, which predicts neural activity using a 256D nonlinear dynamical system that is read out by the observed activity; and Stimulus-driven MLP, which predicts $v(t)$ based only on the past stimulus $I(t-(w-1)), \cdots, I(t-1), I(t)$. These baselines span the spectrum from full mechanistic knowledge (Known-ODE) to purely data-driven prediction (\stimbase); \mlpbase{} is a general first-order dynamical system of voltages, and the \eedbase{} assumes a lower-dimensional latent dynamical system that is read out by the observed voltages.

\textbf{Results with and without the ODE given.}
Predicting activity alone does not yield understanding of the underlying
circuit. Mechanistic interpretation requires recovering the governing
equation of the neural signal together with per-neuron and
connection parameters. If we know the differentiable ODE (\cref{eq:simulated_update}, $0.46$M parameters), we can directly learn the connectivity weights $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ and the per-neuron $\hat{\boldsymbol{\tau}}$, $\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}$. The shared ReLU
activation is known, but cell-type assignments are not. With $\ell_1$/$\ell_2$ regularisation on the parameters and an
MSE one-step prediction loss (\cref{app:benchmark}), this trained 
Known-ODE models achieves near-perfect activity prediction (one-step $r$ and rollout $r$ are close to $1.00$ over $8{,}000$ frames. Notably, the Known-ODE trained on noisy activity reproduces the noise-free rollout: it cannot explicitly fit the stochastic fluctuations and instead converges on the underlying
deterministic dynamics. The parameter recovery over $> 4.5\cdot 10^5$
values is comparably accurate even at high model noise ($R^2>0.95$, outliers $<10\%$, \cref{fig:know_ode_rollout,fig:known_ode_params_3col_noise_comparison}).
Although the inverse problem is strongly ill-posed, full knowledge
of the equation form combined with $\ell_1$/$\ell_2$ regularisation
makes it tractable.
In contrast to Know-ODE, the GNN ($0.48$M parameters) does not have access to the update equation. It learns jointly the connectivity, a per-neuron embedding, and two
signalling functions $f_\theta, g_\phi$ that together express a wide
family of update rules (\cref{eq:gnn_ct}). The training scheme involves a large hyperparameter set (architecture, learning rates, regularisation coefficients) too large
for grid search, so we tuned it with an agentic loop (\cref{app:agent,app:gnn-training}). At low and high model noise ($\sigma \in \{0.05,\,0.5\}$), the GNN results closely matches the Known-ODE upper bound. The deterministic
$\sigma=0$ regime is the harder case ($r=0.90\pm 0.21$,
$R^2_{\hat{\mathbf{W}}}=0.89\pm 0.04$, $R^2_{\hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}}=0.76\pm 0.09$ (outliers $14\%$)). This is consistent with our identifiability analysis. Per-neuron
noise breaks the degenerate directions of $\mathbf{A}_i$, and is
what enables the GNN to match the Known-ODE upper bound at
$\sigma > 0$. The learned latent embedding $\mathbf{a}_i$ alone separates the $65$
cell types, and combining it with $(\hat{\boldsymbol{\tau}},
\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}})$ and summary statistics of
$\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ matches the clustering accuracy of the same
procedure applied to ground-truth parameters
($0.92$ vs.\ $0.93$ at $\sigma = 0.05$;
\cref{fig:clustering_appendix,tab:cv_gnn_vs_baselines}).

\textbf{Edge ablation as a mechanistic test.}
We propose an edge-ablation test to certify that a learned model is
mechanistic, beyond merely predicting activity. For each of the three training-noise regimes ($\sigma \in \{0,\,0.05,\,0.5\}$), we randomly zero $50\%$ of the
$434{,}112$ edges, regenerate $8{,}000$ frames of held-out test
activity with the perturbed connectome, and apply the same edge mask
to the trained GNN's learned weights $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ before
running the autoregressive rollout. No retraining is performed, the
test is whether $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$, $\hat{\boldsymbol{\tau}}$,
$\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}$, $f_\theta$, $g_\phi$
extrapolate to a circuit with half its edges removed. The GNN passes this test at all model noise levels. The per-neuron Pearson correlation between the ablated rollout and the noise-free ablated simulation is $r \geq 0.99$ across all three noise levels
(\cref{fig:rollout_3col_noise_comparison_ablation50}). The
agreement holds for individual cell-type traces (\cref{fig:rollout_3col_noise_comparison_ablation50}a--c) and
across the full voltage range (\cref{fig:rollout_3col_noise_comparison_ablation50}d--f). The high cross-condition
agreement indicates that the learned $f_\theta$ and $g_\phi$ encode
the local update rule rather than memorising trajectories, satisfying
our mechanistic criterion and complementing the parameter-recovery
results of \cref{fig:gnn_params_3col_noise_comparison}.

\textbf{Data degradation (measurement noise, partial data, unknown stimulus).}\label{sec:degradation}
We evaluate the GNN ($\sigma=0.05$) on a grid of degradations expected in real experiments: measurement noise ($\gamma\in\{0.1,0.2\}$), connectome errors as false positives ($+400\%$ random null edges, $5\times$ inflated graph) and false negatives ($-20\%$, $-50\%$ edges removed), partial temporal sampling ($1/5$ frames), partial spatial sampling ($10\%$, $20\%$ neurons hidden), and unknown stimulus jointly recovered with a SIREN network (\cref{app:SIREN,tab:cv_cross_noise}). Activity prediction is robust across all conditions (one-step $r\geq0.95$ except $1/5$ frames at $0.86$). Parameter recovery is most resilient to false-positive edges. The GNN correctly identifies the $80\%$ of inflated edges as zero-weight and recovers the true $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ at $R^2=0.96$ even at $5\times$ the true edge count. It degrades under $-20\%$ edge removal ($R^2_{\hat{W}}=0.78$, rollout $r=0.99$), but breaks down at $-50\%$ removal ($R^2_{\hat{W}}=0.19$, rollout $r=0.56$). When half the true edges are missing from the prior, the remaining edges cannot absorb their contribution. Measurement noise primarily corrupts $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ ($R^2=0.64$ at $\gamma=0.1$, $0.39$ at $\gamma=0.2$) while leaving rollout largely intact. Partial observation of frames or neurons preserves $\tau$ recovery ($R^2_{\hat\tau}\geq0.83$) but undermines $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$. As a first attempt to close this gap, we couple the GNN with an Instant\nobreakdash-NGP coordinate network~\citep{muller_instant_2022} that learns to fill in the unobserved neuron activity. Preliminary results recover part of the lost $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ signal but do not help in parameter recovery (data not shown). When the input stimulus is itself hidden, we couple the GNN with a SIREN coordinate network~\citep{sitzmann_implicit_2020} that learns the photoreceptor drive jointly with the dynamics (\cref{fig:stim_rollout_inr,app:SIREN}). On 5-fold CV (unknown-stimulus row of \cref{tab:cv_cross_noise}), the joint optimisation reconstructs a stimulus tensor of $1{,}736$ photoreceptors $\times\,64{,}000$ frames ($1.11\!\times\!10^{8}$ scalar values) and yields a rollout $r=0.59\pm 0.32$ on training-frame indices since the SIREN cannot extrapolate to coordinates unseen at training. $\hat{\boldsymbol{\tau}}$ ($R^2=0.93\pm 0.04$) and $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ ($R^2=0.81\pm 0.18$) remain close to ground-truth values. Note that the stimulus is not truly unobserved. It is largely present in the activity of the $1{,}736$ retinal photoreceptors (R1--R8 across $217$ columns), which themselves also receive feedback from downstream neurons. The task is therefore to disentangle the feedforward stimulus drive from the recurrent feedback.

\textbf{GNN recovers fly visual system simulation with real synaptic connectivity.}  While the convolutional assumption of the Flyvis model (\cref{sec:simulation}) is a good approximation for the underlying biology, it discards per-column variation of connectivity that is present in the fly and available in the FlyWire whole-brain reconstructions. To test the counterfactual whether the GNN would recover a dynamical visual system with real biological synaptic wiring variability---or in other words, whether the GNN inference capability was limited by the convolutional assumption---we constructed a hybrid connectome variant that augments the Flyvis graph with column-resolved FlyWire connectivity data, while remaining compatible with the trained Flyvis model parameters (\cref{fig:flywire}a-d, \cref{fig:flywire_SI,sec:simulation,app:flywire}). 

We generated simulated data from the Flyvis ODE with low model noise ($\sigma=0.05$) on FlyWire-based connectomes and trained the GNN and Known-ODE to recover the dynamical system. 

On par with Known-ODE, the GNN trained on the e8 hybrid graph ($327{,}358$ edges across $13{,}741$ neurons) achieved on unseen stimuli a one-step Pearson $r = 1.00 \pm 0.00$ and rollout $r = 1.00 \pm 0.00$, and inference remains as accurate as under the convolutional assumption (GNN low-noise in \cref{tab:cv_gnn_vs_baselines}). Per-edge synaptic weight recovery was high ($R^2_{\hat{W}} = 0.94 \pm 0.04$), with time-constant recovery $R^2_{\hat{\tau}} = 0.99 \pm 0.01$ ($0.1\%$ outliers) and resting-potential recovery $R^2_{\hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}} = 0.87 \pm 0.03$ ($6.9\%$ outliers), confirming that the GNN can infer heterogeneous, column-specific connectivity accurately. Scaling to FlyWire eye ($1{,}266{,}378$ edges across $50{,}412$ neurons, extent~$=15$) preserved this performance: one-step $r = 1.00 \pm 0.00$, rollout $r = 1.00 \pm 0.00$, $R^2_{\hat{W}} = 0.97 \pm 0.01$, $R^2_{\hat{\tau}} = 0.99 \pm 0.00$ ($0.0\%$ outliers), and $R^2_{\hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}} = 0.93 \pm 0.02$ ($3.7\%$ outliers) (\cref{fig:flywire}e and \cref{tab:zero_edge_inliers}).

\textbf{GNN dynamical system inference overcomes false connectivity measurements.} Reconstruction of brain wiring requires nanometer precision for accurate synaptic resolution of network connectivity. Sample preparation and imaging artifacts cause reconstruction errors leading to false measurements of connectivity.

Extending the degradation experiments in \cref{sec:degradation}, we induced `biologically plausible' false-positive connectivity into the graph: based on spatial proximity (within $\approx\!6\,\mu$m, roughly one column in the fly visual system) of synapse clouds in the FlyWire anatomical coordinates, we estimated edges that were realistically confusable and included them in the connectivity measurement. The resulting connectivity graph augments the FlyWire-based connectome with these realistic candidate edges, increasing the edge count from $327{,}358$ to $2{,}418{,}403$ in e8 hybrid ($13{,}741$ neurons), and from $1{,}266{,}378$ to $9{,}642{,}335$ in FlyWire eye ($50{,}412$ neurons). This represents the worst case: every realistically confusable edge is included. In practice, the confused subset would be a random subsample of this set.


We again generated simulated data from the Flyvis ODE with neuronal noise ($\sigma=0.05$) on this null-edge-augmented, FlyWire-based connectome and trained the GNN and known-ODE control to recover the dynamical system (\cref{tab:zero_edge_inliers}). On the e8 hybrid graph, the GNN matches the known-ODE control on prediction (one-step $r = 1.00 \pm 0.00$, rollout $r = 1.00 \pm 0.01$) and recovers per-edge weights ($R^2_{\hat{W}} = 0.90 \pm 0.06$) and time-constants ($R^2_{\hat{\tau}} = 0.97 \pm 0.01$, $1.4\%$ outliers) at near-clean accuracy; resting-potential recovery degrades but remains informative ($R^2_{\hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}} = 0.65 \pm 0.06$, $25.4\%$ outliers). On the larger FlyWire eye, prediction stays accurate (\cref{fig:flywire}f; one-step $r = 0.99 \pm 0.00$, rollout $r = 1.00 \pm 0.00$); weight recovery becomes more variable across folds ($R^2_{\hat{W}} = 0.83 \pm 0.16$), time-constants remain robust ($R^2_{\hat{\tau}} = 0.95 \pm 0.02$, $1.0\%$ outliers), and the resting potential is the hardest parameter to identify ($R^2_{\hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}} = 0.61 \pm 0.01$, $40.8\%$ outliers). These results suggest that false-positive connectivity induced by measurement uncertainty and errors can be largely overcome with our system identification framework, with the residual difficulty concentrated in resting-potential identification at scale (\cref{fig:gnn_params_4col_flywire_comparison}).


\section{Discussion}

We asked how and when interpretable, parameter-resolved circuit models can be recovered from imperfect connectivity and activity, and addressed it on a $13.7$K--$50$K neuron \emph{Drosophila} visual-system simulator.

\textbf{Interpretable GNN without prescribed dynamics.} Our continuous-time GNN learns per-neuron update and per-synapse interaction functions jointly with effective weights, matching a Known-ODE oracle on weight, time-constant, and resting-potential recovery.
Cell-type identity emerges unsupervised in the 2D latent embedding as a structural consequence of sharing $f_\theta$ and $g_\phi$ across neurons, and a SIREN extension additionally recovers unobserved photoreceptor drive.
This bridges symbolic-search methods limited to small circuits \citep{lueckmann_discovering_2026} and large-scale connectome-constrained models that commit to a fixed biophysical form \citep{mi_connectome-constrained_2021,lappalainen_connectome-constrained_2024,pospisil_fly_2024}.

\textbf{Identifiability across degradations.} The connectome is most useful as a binary prior: tolerance to $+400$\% random (Flyvis-217) and $+661$\% spatially-plausible null edges (FlyWire) shows that knowing which pairs cannot connect matters more than precise weights, while removing 50\% of true edges breaks recovery.
For connectomics this shifts the quality target from synaptic-weight precision to edge-set completeness.
Edge ablation provides a cheap mechanistic certificate---zeroing 50\% of the connectome and masking $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ accordingly yields rollout $r \geq 0.99$ without retraining---and is structurally inaccessible to predictors lacking a learned $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$; we propose it as a standard check for connectome-inverse claims.

\textbf{Agentic HPO.} Across six conditions, the loop converged on a single configuration in which the $\ell_1$ prior on $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ is the only load-bearing regularizer, consistent with the analytical degeneracy discussed in \cref{app:degeneracy}.  The agent's accumulated principles are themselves interpretable scientific output, not opaque numerical optima.

\textbf{Perturbation richness, not architecture, is the binding constraint.} ML baselines with 17--60$\times$ more parameters match one-step prediction but learn Jacobians inconsistent with the connectome (\cref{fig:jacobian_l1_comparison}) and cannot support mechanistic interventions.
Per-neuron noise lifts the columnar degeneracies and rescues recovery to oracle level, suggesting that perturbation-driven experiments (sparse optogenetics, voltage-clamp) are likely more informative than longer passive recordings.  The analogy between intrinsic stochasticity and extrinsic perturbation remains to be tested.

\section{Limitations}

Several idealizations of the simulator do not transfer to real recordings. (1) Voltages are observed directly; calcium and voltage imaging are nonlinear, lower-bandwidth proxies. (2) Inference and simulation share $dt = 20\,\text{ms}$, so the piecewise-linear update is exact rather than approximate. (3) Conductance-based synapses are structurally unidentifiable from voltage alone---only the product of weight and driving force is constrained. (4) Static $\mathbf{W}, \tau, V^{rest}, and \mathbf{a}$ exclude neuromodulation and plasticity. (5) Flyvis is predominantly feedforward (cf. the $t_w=16$ stimulus-only baseline at $r=0.97$); stronger recurrent regimes are untested, though \citet{allier_graph_2026} suggests they need not be harder. (6) Connectome-activity registration is perfect in silico; in practice it will be imperfect, partly mitigated by the demonstrated false-positive robustness.

Measurement noise on voltage traces is the principal open bottleneck: $\gamma = 0.1$ leaves rollouts intact but collapses $R^2(\hat{\mathbf{W}})$ from $0.99$ to $0.63$. Promising directions are longer rollouts to average noise, likelihood-based losses matched to the noise model, and joint denoising via implicit neural representations as we did for the unknown stimulus (\cref{app:SIREN}). Compute scales steeply with graph size (\cref{tab:compute_resources}), but the shared $f_\theta$, $g_\phi$ make the model amenable to subgraph mini-batching, which we have not yet exploited.

\section{Degeneracy of the inverse problem}
\label{app:degeneracy}


% FROM RESULTS:
% In each case, the model almost perfectly predicts the one-step update and is able to perform an auto-regressive rollout on held out stimuli for 8000 steps. However, despite perfect information about the dynamical equation we find that certain parameters cannot be accurately inferred. Parameter recovery improves upon the addition of a small amount of noise $\sigma=0.05$ and the recovery is perfect at the highest level of $\sigma=0.5$. This is due to a continuous degeneracy in the parameter space of the Flyvis ODE model simulated on naturalistic videos, that is, there is a \emph{degenerate subspace} in parameter space along which different ODEs with the same initial conditions produce the exact same solution. The chosen simulation parameters $(\tau_i, W_{ij}, \vrest_i)$ comprise one point in the degenerate subspace from which we generate a training dataset $\{v_i(t) | \ t \in [0, T]\}$ but gradient descent with random initialization of parameters can converge to any point in this space. Concretely, as a toy example, the parameters $\theta_1, \theta_2$ of the ODE $\dot{x} = -(\theta_1 + \theta_2)x, \ x(0) = 0$, cannot be uniquely determined from the solution $x(t)$ alone, but the combination $\theta_1 + \theta_2$ can be. To understand the degenerate space in \cref{eq:simulated_update}, first we observe that while the equation is nonlinear in $v(t)$, it is linear in the parameters $\theta_\alpha := (\tau_i, W_{ij}, \vrest_i)$, and therefore the problem of determining the parameters from given training data $v(t)$ can be written as $A\theta = b$, where $A$ is $(NT - 1) \times (2N + N_E)$ design matrix and $b$ is a vector of length $NT-1$. With this formulation, it is easy to see that if the training data contained a neuron $i$ that was always at its resting potential, \ie, $v_i(t) = \vrest_i \ \forall \ t$, then it is impossible to identify $\tau_i$ because the column multiplying $\tau_i$ in $A$ is identically 0. However, upon the addition of model noise, the stochastic dynamics decreases the likelihood of finding such a neuron. Similarly, if a neuron is in a constantly excited state the training data for that neuron would allow us to probe its presynaptic weights accurately but we would be underpowered to determine $\tau$ and $\vrest$. We do not rule out the possibility that there exists a family of stimuli, perhaps uncorrelated white noise, that would allow for perfect recovery of parameters in the absence of noise. This baseline helps demonstrate that the Flyvis in the degenerate subspace can produce perfect one-step predictions and perfect rollouts but fail to recover the true parameters of the system. For further details on the degeneracy and parameter recovery, we refer the interested reader to \cref{app:degeneracy}.




Recall that the Flyvis stochastic dynamical system in \cref{eq:simulated_update}, in the absence of noise ($\sigma=0$), takes the form:
\begin{equation}
    \tau_i\frac{dv_i(t)}{dt} = -v_i(t) + \vrest_i
+ \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i} W_{ij}\,\text{ReLU}\!\big(v_j(t)\big)
+ I_i(t) \label{eq:Flyvis-app-noise-free-ode}
\end{equation}
Our goal is to infer the circuit parameters $(\tau_i, \vrest_i, W_{ij})$ given a solution of the ODE $\tilde{v}_i(t)$ and the stimulus $I_i(t)$ sampled at $T$ discrete points $t=0, \cdots T-1$. The ODE parameters can be decomposed into a separate set for each neuron $i$, namely, $\theta_{i\alpha} := (\tau_i, \vrest_i, W_{ij})$ is a vector of length $2 + d_i$ indexed by $\alpha$, where $\mathcal{N}_i$ is the set of presynaptic partners of neuron $i$ and $d_i := |\mathcal{N}_i|$ is the in-degree. We assume that the solution $\tilde{v}_i(t)$ was generated by a parameter set $\theta$ and solving the inverse problem gives us an inferred set $\hat{\theta}$. We show here that, in the absence of noise, the parameter determination is ill-posed and that there are \emph{continuous} families of learned $\hat{\theta}$ consistent with the given $\tilde{v}_i$ and $I_i$.
% We show here that there are \emph{continuous} deformations in parameter space that lead to equivalent solutions, therefore, the system does not admit a unique solution making the inverse problem ill-posed.

\Cref{eq:Flyvis-app-noise-free-ode} can be rewritten, for the purposes of parameter inference
\begin{equation}
    -\tau_i \dot{\tilde{v}}_i + \vrest_i + \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}_i} W_{ij}h_j(t) = \tilde{v}_i(t) - I_i(t)
    \label{eq:Flyvis-linear-rewrite}
\end{equation}
where $h_j(t) := \relu(\tilde{v}_j(t))$ are the pre-synaptic activities.
Observe that while the ODE is non-linear in the state variable $v$ it is
\emph{linear} in the circuit parameters $\theta_{i\alpha}$ defined above. So, 
we can rewrite \cref{eq:Flyvis-linear-rewrite} as a separate
linear system to solve for each neuron $i$,
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{A}_i \, \boldsymbol{\theta}_i = \mathbf{b}_i,
    \label{eq:parameter-linear-system}
\end{equation}
in terms of the design matrix $\mathbf{A}_i$ of dimension $T \times (2 + d_i)$,
and the vectors $\boldsymbol{\theta}_i$ of dimension $2 + d_i$ and
$\mathbf{b}_i$ of dimension $T$:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{A}_i := \begin{pmatrix}
        -\dot{\tilde{v}}_i(0) & 1 & h_{j_1}(0) & h_{j_2}(0) & \cdots  \\
        -\dot{\tilde{v}}_i(1) & 1 & h_{j_1}(1) & h_{j_2}(1) & \cdots  \\
        \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots &
    \end{pmatrix},\quad
    \boldsymbol{\theta}_i := \begin{pmatrix}
        \tau_i \\ \vrest_i \\ W_{ij_1} \\ W_{ij_2} \\ \vdots
    \end{pmatrix},\quad
    \mathbf{b}_i := \begin{pmatrix}
        \tilde{v}_i(0) - I_i(0) \\
        \tilde{v}_i(1) - I_i(1) \\
        \vdots
    \end{pmatrix}
    \label{eq:explicit-linear-system}
\end{equation}
where $j_1, j_2, \ldots$ are the pre-synaptic neurons in $\mathcal{N}_i$.
We emphasise that this linear system is not a linearisation or approximation
but a property of the voltage-based firing-rate models used in Flyvis
\citep{lappalainen_connectome-constrained_2024}.

The linear system \cref{eq:parameter-linear-system} can be solved to
infer the simulation parameters $\theta_{i\alpha}$. If the column space is
not full rank the system admits a continuous degeneracy defined by the
kernel $\mathrm{ker}(\mathbf{A}_i)$. In particular, if there exists a vector
$\boldsymbol{\eta}_i$ satisfying $\mathbf{A}_i \, \boldsymbol{\eta}_i =
\mathbf{0}$ for every $i$, then the family of parameters
$\boldsymbol{\theta}'_i = \boldsymbol{\theta}_i + \lambda \,
\boldsymbol{\eta}_i$ are solutions to the inverse problem for all $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$.
We solve the linear system using least squares, using float64 arithmetic,
column normalization of $\mathbf{A}_i$. We compute the singular value decomposition
of the $(2+d_i) \times (2+d_i)$ Gram matrix $\mathbf{A}_i^T\mathbf{A}_i$ and use the
ratio $\lambda / \lambda_{max}$ of singular values to define null (ratio $ < 64000 \times 10^{-22}$)
and sloppy (ratio $ < 64000 \times 10^{-12}$) directions in the parameter space \citep{Gutenkunst2007-so}.
The results, shown in \cref{fig:lstsq_param_recovery}, demonstrate that with the DAVIS video
stimulus, inference of $\tau, \vrest, W$ parameters from noise-free neural activity is indeed ill-posed.

If the network structure and stimulus are such that a neuron $i$ is in a resting state throughout 
the experiment, then we have $\tilde{v}_i(t) = \vrest_i$ and $\dot{\tilde{v}}_i(t) = 0$ for all observed 
$t$. In this case, the first column of $\mathbf{A}_i$ vanishes and $\tau_i$ is completely unconstrained 
by the data. We have verified this to be the case for all neurons in \cref{fig:lstsq_param_recovery}A.
Similarly, if a neuron $j$ is active but $v_j(t) < 0$ for all time then the $h_j(t)$ column of the
matrix $\mathbf{A}_i$ is identically zero, causing a null direction along the corresponding $\hat{W}_{ij}$.
There are no degeneracies \emph{purely} along the $\vrest$ direction because the corresponding column in
$\mathbf{A}_i$ is identically 1. However, if a pre-synaptic neuron $j$ is always at rest $v_j(t) = \vrest_j$, then the following transformation leaves the equations invariant:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{W}_{ij} \rightarrow \hat{W}_{ij} + x, \ 
    \hat{V}^\mathrm{rest}_i \rightarrow \hat{V}^\mathrm{rest}_i - \vrest_j x
\end{equation}
The examples above help explain the presence of null or sloppy directions for each of the parameters 
in \cref{fig:lstsq_param_recovery} despite a biological realistic connectivity and $64{,}000$ frames
of diverse video stimuli.

\subsubsection*{Highly discordant weights can generate similar rollouts}

Above, we demonstrated that the inverse problem of determining the circuit parameters with perfect
knowledge of the ODE and the connectivity edges between neuron pairs is ill-posed. Here, we show that
due to the symmetries of the connectome, large perturbations of the weight matrices can leave the neural
activity traces approximately invariant.

% In the following, we characterise the dynamical equivalence class of $\mathbf{W}$, the set of all weight matrices that reproduce the observed trajectories. We show that Eq.~\eqref{eq:simulated_update} can be linearised per neuron, that this linear system admits a solution, and finally that it admits many different solutions.

We fix the values of $\vrest$ and $\tau$ and rearrange \cref{eq:Flyvis-linear-rewrite} yielding
% \paragraph{Linearisation of the simulation equation.}
% Rearranging Eq.~\eqref{eq:simulated_update} to isolate the incoming synaptic
% weights gives
\begin{equation}
  \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i} W_{ij}\,\text{ReLU}\!\big(v_j(t)\big)
  = \tau_i \frac{dv_i(t)}{dt} + v_i(t) - V_i^{\text{rest}} - I_i(t).
\end{equation}
Redefining the residual term $b_i(t) = \tau_i \frac{dv_i(t)}{dt} + v_i(t) - V_i^{\text{rest}} - I_i(t)$, we have 
% Defining the presynaptic activity $h_j(t) = \text{ReLU}(v_j(t))$ and the
% residual $b_i(t) = \tau_i \frac{dv_i(t)}{dt} + v_i(t) - V_i^{\text{rest}} -
% I_i(t)$, and stacking the $T$ time-point constraints into matrix form, yields
% an independent linear system for each postsynaptic neuron $i$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{H}_i\,\mathbf{w}_i = \mathbf{b}_i,
  \label{eq:linear_system}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{H}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_i}$ is the presynaptic
activity matrix, $d_i$ is the in-degree of neuron $i$, $\mathbf{w}_i \in
\mathbb{R}^{d_i}$ the incoming weight vector, and $\mathbf{b}_i \in
\mathbb{R}^T$ the stacked residuals. This holds for each postsynaptic neuron
$i = 1, \ldots, N$, where $N = 13{,}697$ and $d_i$ ranges from $1$ to $208$.
The null space $\ker(\mathbf{H}_i) = \{\boldsymbol{\delta} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_i}
: \mathbf{H}_i\boldsymbol{\delta} = \mathbf{0}\}$ characterizes the
degeneracy: any $\mathbf{w}_i + \boldsymbol{\delta}$ with $\boldsymbol{\delta}
\in \ker(\mathbf{H}_i)$ satisfies Eq.~\eqref{eq:linear_system} equally and
produces identical dynamics. In the following, we characterize the
dynamical equivalence class of $\mathbf{W}$, the set of all weight
matrices that reproduce the observed trajectories.

\section{Video stimulus in simulations}
\label{app:stimulus}
Visual inputs are derived from the DAVIS-2017 dataset
\citep{Pont-Tuset_arXiv_2017}, a benchmark dataset for annotated natural
videos. We used 151 videos (10{,}474 frames) in total, 80 videos
(5{,}635 frames) of which were used for training (80\%) and validation
(20\%), while 71 videos (4{,}839 frames) were withheld and solely used
for reporting results in this manuscript. DAVIS sequences are augmented
with horizontal/vertical flips and four rotations.

The frames are converted to grayscale, scaled to the range $[0, 1]$,
and resampled onto the hexagonal photoreceptor lattice of $217$ columns,
giving $1{,}736$ input neurons ($8$ photoreceptor types R1--R8 per
column).

\paragraph{Null stimulus injection.}
For the simulation conditions reported in the main results, we prepend
a zero-stimulus window to every sequence (see \cref{fig:simulations}
for an example trace, in which the null window is highlighted in
blue): the first $50\%$ of each video clip is replaced by frames of
constant zero luminance ($I_i(t)=0$ for the corresponding photoreceptor
inputs), while the remaining $50\%$ contains the natural DAVIS frames.
Concretely, for a sequence of length $T$ we set $I_i(t)=0$ for
$t \in [0, T/2)$ and pass the original DAVIS frames during
$t \in [T/2, T)$. The network's pre-stimulus warm-up at $0.5$
luminance is left unchanged.


\section{Flyvis simulations with realistic FlyWire edges}
\label{app:flywire}

\paragraph{Coregistration}

\citet{lappalainen_connectome-constrained_2024} 
compiled whole-eye connectivity from local reconstructions.
The resulting Flyvis model connectome is a convolutional (translation-invariant) graph: Every visual column shares the same set of $2,355$ edge templates, each parametrized by source type, target type, and column offset.
This reductionist approach discards real synaptic connectivity variability across columns and anatomical location information of neurons and synaptic connections.
To recover this information, we coregistered the Flyvis connectome to FlyWire with a pipeline including the following phases:
We tiled the Flyvis convolutional template ($65$ cell types, $2{,}355$ edge templates) across every reconstructed column of the right-optic-lobe in FlyWire. This resulted in $50{,}412$ candidate nodes and $1{,}650{,}671$ candidate edges for coregistration. Of these, $64.9\%$ of nodes register to a FlyWire \texttt{root\_id} and $48.5\%$ of edges have both endpoints registered, jointly covering $93.5\%$ of the $2{,}355$ templates and matching $3{,}800{,}254$ FlyWire synapses. The hybrid graph keeps FlyWire-observed edges wherever the postsynaptic neuron's anatomical receptive field was reconstructed in FlyWire and falls back to the Flyvis template edges elsewhere. It adds novel edges present only in FlyWire for type pairs that already exist in the Flyvis template. This results in two graphs: `FlyWire eye` ($50{,}412$ neurons; $1{,}266{,}378$ edges) and ``e8 hybrid'', the 217-column restricted central portion of the eye ($13{,}741$ neurons; $327{,}358$ edges) (\cref{tab:hybrid_pipeline_stats}).  

Because FlyWire synapse counts were systematically higher than Flyvis template values (median ratio $~ 1.39\times$ at shared offsets), we applied a multiplicative per-type-pair mean-ratio correction to each FlyWire edge, so that edge weights remain compatible with the trained Flyvis scaling parameters and therefore ODE for simulation.
% The full pipeline is released as the \texttt{flyrewire} package to produce all hybrid variants from FAFB-v783 inputs.

% \paragraph{Connectivity uncertainty augmentation}

% In our simulations, false positive edges carry no synapse counts, i.e., the data-generating Flyvis ODE assigns them zero edge strength and simulated trajectories are unchanged by the augmentation. The GNN student instead places a learnable edge weight (initialised at zero) on every edge, and must learn from the dynamics whether each null edge should remain inactive or carry signal.

\paragraph{Connectivity uncertainty augmentation}

To model false-positive connectivity from electron-microscopy reconstruction errors, with anatomical location available from the coregistration, we constructed an augmented variant of the hybrid connectome in which every neuron pair within $\approx 6\,\mu\text{m}$ ($\sim$one column in the optic lobe) inherits
each other's edges with synapse count zero, across all type
pairs. These null edges (n.e.) are spatially plausible but
unmeasured connections that the GNN must learn to leave at zero or
activate. The augmentation expands `e8 hybrid` to $2{,}418{,}403$ edges and `FlyWire eye` to $9{,}642{,}335$ edges and represents the worst case in
which every confusable edge is included. 


\section{GNN}
\label{app:GNN}
\subsection{Training and agentic HPO.}
\label{app:gnn-training}


The optimised networks of the GNN are $g_\phi$ and $f_\theta$ of
\cref{eq:gnn_ct}, both modelled as MLPs (ReLU activation, hidden
dimension $=80$, $3$ layers, output size $=1$). Other learnables are
the two-dimensional latent vector $\mathbf{a}_i$ associated with each
neuron and the connectivity matrix $\hat{\mathbf W}$. We implemented
the GNNs in PyTorch~Geometric \citep{fey_fast_2019} and used Adam
gradient descent with separate parameter-group learning rates
$\text{lr}_W\!=\!9{\cdot}10^{-4}$ (connectivity),
$\text{lr}\!=\!1.8{\cdot}10^{-3}$ ($g_\phi$, $f_\theta$),
$\text{lr}_\text{emb}\!=\!2.3{\cdot}10^{-3}$ (embeddings), batch size
$4$, $1$ nominal epoch with
\texttt{data\_augmentation\_loop}$=500$ over $64\,000$ training
frames (${\approx}5{\cdot}10^{5}$ to $2{\cdot}10^{6}$ gradient steps
across conditions). Time points are sampled uniformly at random at
each step.

The agentic loop of \cref{app:agent} was run independently
for each of the six conditions of \cref{tab:unified_hp}
($\sim$100 iterations of 4 parallel slots per condition,
single-parameter mutations, 4-seed robustness validation of every
candidate winner). All six converged on the same consistent conclusion
about the role of each term of \cref{eq:loss_full}. The
$\ell_1$ prior on $\hat{\mathbf W}$ ($\gamma_0$) is the only
important regularizer: setting $\gamma_0\!=\!0$ leaves rollouts
nearly unchanged ($r\!=\!0.999$) but collapses $R^2_{\hat W}$ from
$0.97$ to $0.39$, a direct manifestation of the degeneracy discussed in
\cref{app:degeneracy}. $\gamma_1$, which controls $\ell_2$ regularization,
provides no measurable benefit.
Among the $g_\phi$ priors, $\mu_1$
prevents the trivial collapse $g_\phi\!\equiv\!0$ and was selected in
$[750, 2000]$ across conditions; $\mu_2$ stabilises at $0.9$ for the
$\sigma=0.05$ sub-conditions and is driven to 0 only in the noise-free condition;
$\lambda_1$ takes a distinct value in the
noise-free condition ($\lambda_1\!=\!0.14$, $+0.054$ on
$R^2_{\hat W}$) versus a uniform $0.28$ across the $\sigma=0.05$ 
sub-conditions. The two $f_\theta$ priors split by noise regime:
$\lambda_0$ ($\ell_1$) is needed at the $\sigma=0.05$  working point
($\lambda_0\!=\!0.05$, identical across the four $\sigma=0.05$ 
sub-conditions) but is driven to $0$ in the noise-free and $\sigma=0.5$ 
extremes; $\lambda_2$ ($\ell_2$) acts as the magnitude anchor of
$\hat{\mathbf W}$ and stays at $10^{-3}$ for every noisy condition.

A clean cross-condition pattern emerged: the four $\sigma{=}0.05$
sub-conditions share an identical regulariser block, with the
noise-free and $\sigma{=}0.5$ winners deviating at opposite poles.
The $\sigma{=}0.05$ winner sits between the two extremes, is the
closest single point to the per-coefficient medians of
\cref{tab:unified_hp}, and is the only one validated under
12-seed robustness with zero catastrophic failures; we therefore
retain it unchanged for every GNN result reported in the paper,
including the FlyWire and degraded-connectome variants.

\paragraph{Recurrent training for the 1/5-frames row of \cref{tab:cv_cross_noise}.}
With only every fifth frame observed, consecutive targets are separated by $5\Delta t$ and the one-step regression used in every other condition no longer applies. We replace it with a $K\!=\!5$-step truncated backpropagation-through-time (BPTT) rollout: the GNN is unrolled under its own predictions for five Euler steps from an observed frame, and gradients propagate through the full unroll to the next observed frame. To stabilise the unroll we raise $\mu_1$ from $750$ to $9{,}000$ and lower $\mu_2$ from $0.9$ to $0.1$, run batch size $1$ and $n_{\text{epochs}}\!=\!2$; all other hyperparameters of \cref{tab:unified_hp} are kept at the consensus values.

\subsection{Extraction of synaptic weights, time constants, and resting potentials}
\label{app:extraction}

The GNN of \cref{eq:gnn_ct} does not expose the membrane time
constants $\hat\tau_i$, the resting potentials
$\hat V^{\mathrm{rest}}_i$, or a directly comparable per-edge
synaptic weight as learnable parameters. The leaky-integrator term
of \cref{eq:simulated_update} is absorbed into the postsynaptic
update $f_\theta$, while the per-edge weight $\hat{W}_{ij}$
that appears in the message is entangled with the gain of $g_\phi$
on the presynaptic side and the gain of $f_\theta$ on the
postsynaptic side: a common rescaling of $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ and the
two MLPs leaves the predicted dynamics unchanged. We therefore
extract the three biophysical quantities reported in the main paper
in a post-processing step that linearises $f_\theta$ and $g_\phi$
around each neuron's working point on the same training trajectory
$v_i(t)$ that drives the prediction loss. The extraction is run
unchanged across every noise regime, FlyWire variant, and
degraded-data condition.

\paragraph{Per-neuron working range.}
For every neuron $i$ we summarise its trajectory by the empirical
mean $\mu_i$ and standard deviation $\sigma_i$ of $v_i(t)$ over
the training horizon, and define the natural activity range
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{D}_i \;=\; \big[\mu_i - 2\sigma_i,\; \mu_i + 2\sigma_i\big].
    \label{eq:extraction_range}
\end{equation}
All affine fits of $f_\theta$ and $g_\phi$ are restricted to
$\mathcal{D}_i$, so that the recovered slopes and offsets describe
the learned dynamics where they are actually evaluated, rather than
in extrapolation regions that the trained model never visits.

\paragraph{Time constant and resting potential.}
The leaky-integrator term of \cref{eq:simulated_update} is
linear in $v_i$ at fixed cell-type identity. We probe the
postsynaptic update at zero aggregated input and zero stimulus,
\begin{equation}
    \phi_i(v) \;:=\; f_\theta\!\big(v,\,\mathbf{a}_i,\,m=0,\,I=0\big),
\end{equation}
sample $\phi_i$ on a dense grid of $v\in\mathcal{D}_i$, and fit a
least-squares affine model
\begin{equation}
    \phi_i(v) \;\approx\; s_i\,v + b_i.
    \label{eq:phi_linear_fit}
\end{equation}
Identifying \cref{eq:phi_linear_fit} with the input-free,
noise-free version of \cref{eq:simulated_update},
$dv_i/dt = -v_i/\tau_i + V_i^{\mathrm{rest}}/\tau_i$, gives the
learned time constant and resting potential as
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\tau}_i \;=\; -\,\frac{1}{s_i},
    \qquad
    \hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_i \;=\; -\,\frac{b_i}{s_i}.
    \label{eq:tau_vrest_extraction}
\end{equation}
Neurons with a degenerate slope ($s_i \to 0$) and time constants
that fall outside the physical range are clipped, and contribute
to the outlier fraction reported alongside the parameter $R^2$.

\paragraph{Effective synaptic weights.}
The message in \cref{eq:gnn_ct} couples each edge weight
$\hat{W}_{ij}$ to two gain factors that the network is
free to absorb: the rate of change of the rectified output of
$g_\phi$ at the presynaptic working point and the rate of change of
$f_\theta$ in its message channel at the postsynaptic working
point. A common rescaling of $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ and either gain
leaves the predicted dynamics unchanged, so $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$
alone is not directly comparable to the simulator weight. The
postsynaptic gain also folds in the learned membrane time constant:
matching \cref{eq:gnn_ct} term-by-term against the time-derivative
form of \cref{eq:simulated_update} shows that the message channel
of $f_\theta$ contributes $\hat W_{ij}\,g_\phi^{\,2}/\hat\tau_i$,
so that the correction must include a factor of the learned
$\hat\tau_i$ from \cref{eq:tau_vrest_extraction}.

We therefore linearise both functions on each neuron's activity
range. For every presynaptic neuron $j$ we evaluate the squared
output $g_\phi(v,\mathbf{a}_j)^{\,2}$ that actually enters the
message on the positive part of $\mathcal{D}_j$, fit it with an
affine model, and record its slope $\eta_j$. For every postsynaptic
neuron $i$ we evaluate the partial derivative of $f_\theta$ with
respect to its message argument at a small set of operating points
sampled uniformly along the training trajectory and take the
median across probes, yielding $\kappa_i$. The recovered effective
weight on edge $(i,j)$ is
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathbf{W}}^{\mathrm{eff}}_{ij}
    \;=\;
    \hat{\tau}_i\,\kappa_i\,\eta_j\,\hat{W}_{ij}
    \;=\;
    -\,\frac{\kappa_i\,\eta_j\,\hat{W}_{ij}}{s_i},
    \label{eq:weight_extraction}
\end{equation}
where the second form uses $\hat\tau_i = -1/s_i$ from
\cref{eq:tau_vrest_extraction}. The two gain factors and the
learned time constant cancel the corresponding factors in
\cref{eq:gnn_ct} when $g_\phi^{\,2}$ and the message channel of
$f_\theta$ are well approximated by their linear fits on
$\mathcal{D}_i$ and $\mathcal{D}_j$. The connectivity $R^2$
reported in the main paper and in \cref{app:metrics} is
computed between $\hat{\mathbf{W}}^{\mathrm{eff}}_{ij}$ and the simulator
weight $W_{ij}$, summed over every edge of the prior
graph; for the degraded-connectivity variants of
\cref{tab:cv_cross_noise}, this includes the augmented
zero-weight edges, which the GNN optimization must drive to zero.


\subsection{Joint stimulus recovery with SIREN.}
\label{app:SIREN}
The unkown-stimulus results of \cref{tab:cv_cross_noise} is obtained by hiding the visual stimulus over the $1{,}736$ retina neurons. To test whether the visual input $I_i(t)$ entering \cref{eq:known_ode} can itself be recovered from activity alone, we extend the GNN with an implicit neural representation of the stimulus field $\hat I^*(t,x,y)$, modelled as a coordinate-based
MLP with sine activations \citep[SIREN,][input size~$=3$,
hidden dimension~$=2048$, $4$ hidden layers, output size~$=1$,
$\omega_0\!=\!4096$, outermost-linear head]{sitzmann_implicit_2020}.
The SIREN is queried at the $1\,736$ photoreceptor positions
$(x_i,y_i)$ at every time step and substitutes for $I_i(t)$ in the
GNN forward pass. The GNN and the SIREN are jointly optimised on the
same prediction loss but with strongly asymmetric learning rates
and a two-phase ``alternate-training'' schedule: epoch~$0$ uses the
full GNN learning rates above with
$\text{lr}_\text{SIREN}\!=\!10^{-8}$; epochs~$1$–$2$ scale the GNN
learning rates by $0.05$ while $\text{lr}_\text{SIREN}$ is held
constant, letting the SIREN catch up on a stable graph backbone.

\section{Agent-in-the-loop parameter optimisation}
\label{app:agent}

The optimisation space combining simulation parameters (noise $\sigma$,
connectivity constraints, $n_{\mathrm{frames}}$) and machine learning parameters
(e.g.\ for GNN, $\lambda_0,\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\lambda_3,\gamma_0,\gamma_1,\mu_0,\mu_1,\mu_2$, learning rates, etc.) is too large for grid search. We instead deploy a closed loop
coupling three components: a GNN training engine, a large language model
agent (Claude Code, Anthropic), and an explicit structured summary. The system
operates through text file exchange. The instruction file defines the inverse
problem, the quantitative score objective, the metrics to track, the parameter
ranges, and the logging format. The structured summary maintains accumulated
understanding in two tiers: established principles and open questions that
persist across blocks, and a working record of current hypotheses and recent
iterations. A configuration file is edited by the agent to set the next
experiment; a results file is written by the experiment engine and read by
the agent.

The primary objective of the agentic loop is to optimise hyperparameters
against a quantitative score ($R^2_{\mathbf{W}}$ or rollout accuracy). But we
also constrain the agent to hypothesis-driven protocol: a testable
hypothesis must be stated before each experiment, and only experimental
outcomes can validate or falsify it. The next experiment is therefore
selected by the hypothesis the agent currently seeks to test rather than by a
numerical exploration/exploitation trade-off.

Experiments are organised into blocks of 8-16 iterations (multiples of 4 to
match the parallel batch size). The agent does not start from scratch, it is initialised with a configuration established from prior works. The simulation
regime of each block can be specified by the user in the instruction file or
chosen by the agent. Within a block, the agent explores GNN training
parameters guided by its current hypothesis. To make the protocol robust, we
run $4$ experiments in parallel and submit the resulting batch of training
outcomes to the agent. Reasoning over a batch rather than a single trial
sharpens both confirmation and falsification. The structured summary also
serves as an explicit long-term memory, overcoming the finite context window
of the agent and enabling accumulation across hundreds of iterations. With
this protocol, convergence to the optimal configuration is reached within
$\sim 100$ GNN training experiments sweeping sometimes more than 10 parameters.

We initially adopted UCB tree search as in AlphaEvolve and FunSearch
\citep{romera-paredes_mathematical_2024,novikov_alphaevolve_2025}, where
parameter mutations are sampled from a tree balancing exploitation and
exploration. Once the hypothesis-driven constraint was introduced, we
found that the agent stopped consulting the UCB scores: parent selection and
parameter mutations were driven entirely by hypotheses formulated from the
structured summary and recent experimental results. We accordingly removed
the UCB component, which had become redundant.

\section{Benchmark models}
\label{app:benchmark}

We describe below four baseline models that are distinctive in their architecture and inductive bias.
The Known-ODE baseline incorporates maximal knowledge about the underlying system and sets an upper bound on the GNN, which assumes 
no knowledge of the underlying non-linearity and only knowledge of the non-zero edges in the connectome graph. 
The remaining models, depicted in \cref{fig:baseline_arch}, make different assumptions about the dynamics and stimuli. All of them assume that the stimuli are intrinsically low-dimensional, which is a reasonable assumption for natural images drawn from the DAVIS dataset. \stimbase{} is the most general: only making the assumption that neural activity contains some encoding of past stimuli. \mlpbase{} assumes that the neural activity can be described as a first order dynamical system whose variables are the activities themselves. \eedbase{} is a state space model that enforces that the neural dynamics is intrinsically low-dimensional, an active area of research starting from the early work of \citep{churchland_neural_2012, pandarinath_inferring_2018}. We show that despite the \mlpbase{} and \eedbase{} models being first order models of neural activity like the GNN (and Known-ODE) and accurately reproducing the auto-regressive rollout on held out test data, they do not capture the \emph{true} dynamics. The binary connectome edges provide a very specific prior that allows the GNN to infer the true dynamics and models like the above lack.

\subsection{Known-ODE} \label{app:known_ode}
The known-ODE model fixes the functional form of the dynamics to the ground-truth Flyvis equation and learns only the biophysical parameters and the connectivity:
\begin{equation}
\hat{\tau}_i\,\frac{d\hat{v}_i(t)}{dt}
= -\hat{v}_i(t) + \hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_i
+ \sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i} \hat{W}_{ij}\,\mathrm{ReLU}\!\big(v_j(t)\big)
+ I_i(t).
\label{eq:known_ode}
\end{equation}
The learnable parameters are the per-edge weights $\hat{W}_{ij}$, the per-neuron time constants $\hat{\tau}_i = \mathrm{softplus}(\tilde{\tau}_i)$ (with $\tilde{\tau}_i\in\mathbb{R}$ the underlying optimized parameter), and the per-neuron resting potentials $\hat{V}^{\mathrm{rest}}_i$. During training, inputs $I_i(t)$, adjacency $\mathcal{N}_i$, and activity $v_i(t)$ are given; $\hat{\mathbf{W}}, \tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}, \hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}$ are optimized by minimizing the difference between simulator $y_i(t)=dv_i(t)/dt$ and known-ODE predictions $\hat{y}_i(t)=d\hat{v}_i(t)/dt$,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}} = \sum_{i,t}\|\hat{y}_i(t) - y_i(t)\|_2.
\label{eq:known_ode_loss_pred}
\end{equation}
Even with the ODE structure fixed, recovery of $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ from voltage traces remains non-unique (\cref{app:degeneracy}). To constrain the solution space, we added regularization terms to $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}$:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}
+ \gamma_0\|\hat{\mathbf{W}}\|_1 + \gamma_1\|\hat{\mathbf{W}}\|_2
+ \eta_0\|\tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}\|_1 + \eta_1\|\tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}\|_2
+ \eta_2\|\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}\|_1 + \eta_3\|\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}\|_2.
\label{eq:known_ode_loss}
\end{equation}
Note that the regularization we apply here differs from the regularization applied in \cref{app:degeneracy}; 
there we find the minimum norm solution whose components are zero along the degenerate directions.
The $\ell_1$ term $\gamma_0$ on $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$ promotes sparse effective connectivity, while the $\ell_2$ term $\gamma_1$ penalizes large synaptic weights. The $\ell_1$ penalty $\eta_0$ on $\tilde{\boldsymbol{\tau}}$ shrinks the time constants toward the softplus origin, while the $\ell_2$ penalty $\eta_1$ prevents drift. The $\ell_1$ penalty $\eta_2$ on $\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}$ promotes sparse resting potentials, while the $\ell_2$ penalty $\eta_3$ penalizes large biases. \cref{eq:known_ode_loss} is a general form: the coefficients $\gamma_0,\gamma_1,\eta_0,\eta_1,\eta_2,\eta_3$ are hyperparameters tuned via the agentic optimization procedure described in \cref{app:agent} , which can set a subset of them to zero. In practice we retain a single regularization profile for every
Known-ODE result reported in the paper, namely the best paremeters found by the
agentic exploration run on noise-free data (\cref{app:agent}):
$\gamma_0=10^{-5}$ ($\ell_1$ on $\hat{\mathbf W}$),
$\eta_1=3\!\times\!10^{-3}$ ($\ell_2$ on $\tilde{\boldsymbol\tau}$),
$\eta_3=10^{-3}$ ($\ell_2$ on $\hat{\mathbf V}^{\mathrm{rest}}$), with
the remaining three coefficients pinned to zero. The same coefficients
are applied unchanged to the noisy Flyvis conditions and to the
FlyWireRF runs.

The Flyvis model optimizes a biophysically motivated, connectome-constrained model on an optical flow task, which requires the neural activities to encode past visual stimuli. The stimulus baseline model  (\cref{fig:baseline_arch}) predicts the activities $v_i(t)$ of all the neurons using the input stimulus at times $t, t-1, t-(t_w-1)$, where $t_w$ denotes a context window. The stimulus encoder MLP (2 layers, 128 hidden units) encodes each stimulus frame into a 64D latent vector and the predictor MLP (3 layers, 512 hidden units) maps the concatenated $64\times t_w$ latents to a prediction of 13{,}741 neural activities. Both modules are jointly trained to minimize the MSE loss between prediction and ground truth voltage. \cref{fig:stimulus_ctx_pearson} and \cref{tab:cv_baselines_sym} show the variation of prediction performance, quantified using Pearson's r, as a function of $t_w$; at $t_w=1$ the performance is poor ($r \sim 0.4$) and increases monotonically as a function of context length
reaching $r \sim  0.97$ at $t_w=16$. This baseline demonstrates that an accurate prediction of neural activity cannot be made with the instantaneous stimulus alone.


\subsection{\mlpbase}

We built a black-box recurrent neural network using an MLP architecture (2 layers, 640 hidden units, leaky ReLU activation; $\sim 29$M parameters) to predict the time derivative of neural activity based on the input stimulus and past activity. We begin with the noise free system, where $\sigma = 0$ in \cref{eq:simulated_update}, and use the model
\begin{equation}
    \frac{dv}{dt} = \mathrm{MLP}(s(t), v(t-1))\, .
    \label{eq:mlp_update}
\end{equation}
We train this model by generating an auto-regressive rollout of 20 time steps and minimize the MSE loss between the predicted and ground truth voltage traces. We observed that by varying the hyperparameters of the MLP we were able to accurately capture the 1-step evolution of neural activity. However, we failed to achieve a stable roll out on validation data beyond a few hundred time steps. When measuring the MSE on an 8{,}000 step rollout on validation data we observed large (RMSE $> 1.0$) low frequency oscillations that did not go to zero even after significant increases to the training time (data not shown here). To address this, we added ResNet-style skip connections \citep{he_deep_2015}, which enable the network to easily model the low frequency oscillations in the MSE that were missed by the plain MLP. This intervention helps us learn a generalizable model for neural activity evolution and we are able to generate autoregressive rollouts of over 8{,}000 steps on the held-out test data with Pearson's $r > 0.90$ as shown in \cref{tab:cv_baselines_sym}.

\Cref{fig:rollout_3col_noise_comparison_mlp} visualizes the noise free rollout on models trained with varying levels of noise. Unlike the GNN, the rollout performance of the \mlpbase{} is negatively impacted by increasing model noise. This is likely because with the high capacity of the model the MSE loss overfits to noise patterns in the data that do not generalize. The 20-step rollout training helps average out the noise contributions and without it the model memorizes the noise and does not generalize. It may be possible to bridge this gap with more input data that would allow for further averaging out of the noise term, changing the loss function to the likelihood under the noise model, or other architectural and training innovations. 

Although the model faithfully reproduces the rollout, it does not capture the true underlying dynamics. This is best illustrated in \cref{fig:jacobian_l1_comparison} where we plot the Jacobian of the MLP update with respect to the stimuli and the pre-synaptic neuron activities, which shows a large deviation from the ground truth simulated ODE. Without the inductive bias provided by the connectome constraint, the MLP picks a different combination of stimulus and past activity to predict the next step. This agrees with the learnings from the \stimbase{} baseline, as neural activity is a specific encoding of past stimuli and therefore many such combinations could in principle exist. Adding L1 regularization does promote sparsity, and we observe that many entries in the Jacobian are pushed towards zero, but it does not help recover the \emph{correct} Jacobian structure in the ground truth as shown in the figure.

\subsection{EED model}

\begin{figure}[ht!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{figure/fig_svd_variance_explained.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Low-rank reconstruction of the neural activity matrix.}
    Per-group Nash--Sutcliffe efficiency
    $R^2(K) = 1 - \overline{\mathrm{MSE}}_g(K) / \overline{\mathrm{Var}}_g$
    for the rank-$K$ truncated SVD reconstruction $A_K$ of the $(T \times N)$
    voltage matrix on noise-free  data
    ($T{=}64{,}000$, $N{=}13{,}741$). For each neuron $n$ we compute the temporal
    variance $\mathrm{Var}_t(A_{:,n})$ and the reconstruction error
    $\mathrm{MSE}_n(K) = \tfrac{1}{T}\sum_t (A_{t,n} - (A_K)_{t,n})^2$;
    both are averaged over neurons within a coarse cell-type group $g$
    (R1--R8, L1--L5, Lawf, Am, C2--C3, CT1, Mi, T, Tm). All groups exceed

    Encoder--Evolver--Decoder bottleneck.}
  \label{fig:svd_variance_explained}
\end{figure}
We were motivated to consider a bottle-neck architecture by the observation that the 
time variation of simulated neural activity matrix can be accurately 
reconstructed using $K$ singular values for $K \ll N$ as shown in 
\cref{fig:svd_variance_explained} \citep{churchland_neural_2012, pandarinath_inferring_2018}. 
We built a bottleneck architecture that we call the Encoder-Evolver-Decoder model (19M parameters), which 
is a neural state space model where the internal dynamics are described by an evolving latent 
representation, which is then read out by the measured neurons. 
The model architecture, depicted in
\cref{fig:baseline_arch}C, consists of an auto-encoder that projects to/reconstructs the observed neural activity 
from a 256D latent space. The visual stimulus is also encoded into a smaller 128D representation by a stimulus encoder.
The time evolution operator maps the 384D concatenation of the current stimulus latent and the past neural activity latent to the next step neural activity latent. During training we perform an auto-regressive rollout of the latent state in 256D over 5 steps and use an MSE loss over the decoded activities vs the ground truth. We found that shallow MLPs (1 layer) with linear skips were sufficient to capture the dynamics in latent space. Similar to the MLP the model performance degrades with the addition of model noise. However, the EED model is able to produce stable $8{,}000$ step rollouts over test data as shown in \cref{fig:rollout_3col_noise_comparison_eed}. The latent bottleneck imposes a structural prior on the learned dynamics, however, this is \emph{distinct} from the structure of the ODE imposed by the connectome graph. So while the rollout is accurately captured the Jacobian computation for the EED (not shown here) similarly misses the true structure of the simulation.

\section{Evaluation metrics}
\label{app:metrics}

We define here the four metrics reported throughout the paper: the
one-step and auto-regressive rollout Pearson~$r$ on neural activity,
and the parameter-recovery $R^2$ on the synaptic weights
$\hat{\mathbf{W}}$, the membrane time constants
$\hat{\boldsymbol{\tau}}$, and the resting potentials
$\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}$.

\paragraph{One-step Pearson $r$.}
On a held-out trajectory of $T$ frames, at every time step
$t = 0, \ldots, T-2$ the model is fed the ground-truth voltage
$\tilde v(t)$ together with the stimulus $I(t)$ and predicts the next
state $\hat v(t+1)$. For each neuron $i$ we compute the Pearson
correlation between the predicted and the ground-truth trace,
\begin{equation}
r_i^{\mathrm{1\text{-}step}} = \mathrm{corr}\!\big(\hat v_i(\,\cdot\,),\,\tilde v_i(\,\cdot\,)\big)
\end{equation}
Neurons whose predicted or ground-truth trace is flat
($\sigma < 10^{-8}$) are dropped. The per-neuron correlations are
then pooled in Fisher-$z$ space,
\begin{equation}
    \bar z \;=\; \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\mathrm{arctanh}\,r_i,
    \qquad
    \bar r \;=\; \tanh\bar z,
    \label{eq:fisher_pool}
\end{equation}
and we report $\bar r \pm (r_+ - r_-)/2$ with
$r_\pm = \tanh(\bar z \pm \sigma_z)$ and $\sigma_z$ the standard
deviation of $\mathrm{arctanh}\,r_i$ across the $N$ neurons.

\paragraph{Auto-regressive rollout Pearson $r$.}
For the rollout the model receives only the initial voltage
$\tilde v(0)$ and the full stimulus stream
$\{I(0),\ldots,I(T-1)\}$; from $t = 1$ onwards it is fed back its own
prediction $\hat v(t-1)$ to produce $\hat v(t)$, with no resetting to
ground truth. The trajectory length is $T = 8{,}000$ frames
($160$~s at $\Delta t = 20$~ms) for every Flyvis evaluation. The
rollout trace is compared against the noise-free ground truth
$\tilde v_i(\cdot)$ on a per-neuron basis, and the Fisher-$z$ pooling
of \cref{eq:fisher_pool} is applied unchanged.

\paragraph{Synaptic-weight $R^2$.}
The recovery of the connectome is quantified by the
Nash--Sutcliffe efficiency on the edge weights,
\begin{equation}
    R^2_{\hat{\mathbf{W}}}
        \;=\; 1 \;-\;
        \frac{\frac{1}{|\mathcal{E}|}\sum_{(i,j)\in\mathcal{E}}
              \big(W_{ij} - \hat{W}_{ij}\big)^2}
             {\mathrm{Var}_{(i,j)}\!\big(W_{ij}\big)},
    \label{eq:r2_identity}
\end{equation}
pooled over all edges $\mathcal{E}$ of the prior graph. The numerator
is the raw mean squared error, so under a linear miscalibration
$\hat{W}_{ij} = a\,W_{ij} + b$ both a wrong slope $a \neq 1$
\emph{and} a non-zero offset $b$ inflate the residual: the metric
penalises noise, scale, and bias errors. The range is
$(-\infty, 1]$, with $R^2 = 1$ requiring $\hat{W}_{ij} = W_{ij}$
exactly. We additionally report the diagnostic slope $a$ from a
linear fit of $\hat{W}$ against $W$, which disambiguates scale
miscalibration from random scatter when $R^2$ is low (\eg \cref{fig:known_ode_params_3col_noise_comparison}). For models
trained with extra null edges (e.g.\ the $+400\%$ condition) the sum
runs over the augmented graph, and recovering
$\hat{W}_{ij} \approx 0$ on the null edges is part of the
reported number. No outlier filtering is applied to
$R^2_{\hat{\mathbf{W}}}$.

\paragraph{Parameter $R^2$ for $\hat{\boldsymbol{\tau}}$ and
$\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}$ with outlier filtering.}
For the per-neuron parameters $\tau_i$ and $\vrest_i$ we use the same
identity-line statistic of \cref{eq:r2_identity}, restricted to an
inlier set. A neuron $i$ is flagged as an outlier when its absolute
residual exceeds a hard, parameter-specific threshold,
\begin{equation}
    |\hat\theta_i - \theta_i| \;>\; \delta_\theta,
    \qquad
    \delta_\tau = 0.1,\;\;
    \delta_{\vrest} = 0.2,
    \label{eq:outlier_threshold}
\end{equation}
in the same units as the corresponding ground-truth parameter. The
thresholds are fixed once and held constant across every condition in
the paper, so that outlier counts are directly comparable across rows
of any cross-noise table. The filtering is motivated by
how $\tau$ and $\vrest$ are extracted: both are read off from the
slope and offset of the learned function
$f_\theta$ around the neuron's working point, and a small number of
inactive or saturated neurons can produce extreme estimates that
would otherwise dominate the variance in
\cref{eq:r2_identity}. The reported $R^2_{\hat{\boldsymbol{\tau}}}$
and $R^2_{\hat{\mathbf{V}}^{\mathrm{rest}}}$ are therefore
inlier-set values, with the outlier fraction
$n_{\text{out}}/n_{\text{total}}$ (in percent) in parentheses next to
each value, e.g.\ $0.94 \pm 0.01\,(3.3)$ in
\cref{tab:cv_cross_noise}.

\paragraph{Cell-type clusterability (GMM accuracy, ARI, NMI).}
To assess whether learned per-neuron quantities recover the $65$-class
cell-type partition of Flyvis, we cluster each learned model feature space with a Gaussian Mixture Model and report three quantities
(\cref{fig:clustering_appendix}). Features are $z$-scored
column-wise so that the high-variance $\mathbf{W}$-statistics do not
drown out $\tau$ and $V_{\mathrm{rest}}$, then a GMM with $K=100$
mixture components is fit. Each neuron is assigned to its argmax
component, and the predicted label set is mapped to the ground-truth
cell types by the Hungarian algorithm on the $65 \times K$ confusion
matrix; the GMM \emph{accuracy} is the fraction of neurons whose
Hungarian-mapped label matches the true cell type. The Adjusted Rand
Index (ARI) and Normalised Mutual Information (NMI) are computed
directly between true labels and raw GMM labels and are therefore
invariant to the cluster-to-type permutation. We also report $k$, the
number of mixture components that received at least one neuron at
inference time; $k < K$ when the GMM collapses redundant components
(typical for the $2$D embedding panel of
\cref{fig:clustering_appendix}c). The four candidate feature spaces are: ground-truth $(\tau, V_{\mathrm{rest}})$ concatenated with $8$ per-neuron $\mathbf{W}$-statistics
$\{\mathrm{mean}, \mathrm{std}, \min, \max\}$ of incoming and outgoing
edge weights computed on the true $\mathbf{W}$ ($10$D); the same
$10$D vector built from the learned $\hat{\mathbf{W}}$; the
learned $2$D node embedding $\mathbf{a}_i$ used directly as input to
the GNN; and the $12$D concatenation of $\mathbf{a}_i$ with
$(\hat{\tau}, \hat{V}_{\mathrm{rest}},
\hat{\mathbf{W}}\text{-stats})$. UMAP is used solely to visualise
the $>2$D feature spaces; the GMM, the Hungarian mapping, and all
reported numbers are computed in the raw feature space, never in UMAP
coordinates.

\paragraph{Cross-validation aggregation.}
For five-fold CV results, every metric above is computed independently
in each fold; the table value is the mean across folds and the
$\pm$ figure is one standard deviation across folds. Pearson~$r$
values are averaged in Fisher-$z$ space across folds as well
(\cref{eq:fisher_pool}, with the $N$ now being the total number of
finite per-neuron entries pooled over all folds).
